# CCJ — Cameco

## Summary

Backbone of the Western nuclear fuel cycle, priced as a cyclical commodity producer. Tier-one mines (McArthur River at ~16% grade, Cigar Lake), integrated conversion/fabrication, and 49% Westinghouse stake span the full path from yellowcake to reactor fuel for the non-Russian world. Three demand drivers converge simultaneously: AI data center baseload (183 TWh in 2024, doubling by 2030), government-mandated energy sovereignty post-Hormuz crisis, and a nuclear buildout of 75+ reactors under construction and 120+ planned. 65% of post-2027 demand remains uncontracted — an imminent re-contracting wave at prices 3-5x above legacy levels, with long-term contracts already at $90/lb (highest since 2008). Structural removal of Russian enrichment (~44% of global capacity) and financialization through Sprott ($12B+ holdings) reinforce this cycle unlike any prior. The $80B U.S. government nuclear partnership with Westinghouse/Brookfield/Cameco transforms nuclear from energy choice to national security mandate.

## Business Model & Product Description

Cameco's business model has evolved from a commodity miner into what is effectively a "nuclear fuel utility" — the only Western-aligned company spanning the full fuel cycle from mine to reactor. The correct analogy is not a gold or copper miner (single-product, price-taker) but rather a vertically integrated energy infrastructure company like Schlumberger in oil services, where each step in the value chain creates switching costs and margin stacking. Revenue flows through three complementary segments — upstream mining (commodity-exposed but protected by tier-one asset quality and long-term contract structures with price escalators), midstream fuel services (conversion/processing with oligopoly pricing — one of only three Western facilities), and downstream reactor services via the 49% Westinghouse stake (recurring, high-margin, decoupled from commodity prices). The Westinghouse stake transforms the earnings profile: even if uranium spot prices decline, Westinghouse's $219M EBITDA (+51% YoY) provides a services floor that pure miners lack.

Cameco operates across the full Western nuclear fuel cycle through three integrated segments:

### Uranium Mining

Second-largest global uranium producer (14–20% of world output), trailing only Kazakhstan's Kazatomprom. Tier-one assets in Saskatchewan, Canada:

- **McArthur River / Key Lake**: The world's highest-grade uranium mine at ~16% U3O8 (vs. global average ~0.1–0.3%). Underground mining operation paired with the Key Lake mill for processing. Produces uranium concentrate ("yellowcake" / U3O8) that is the feedstock for the entire nuclear fuel chain.
- **Cigar Lake**: High-grade underground mine using jet boring mining method to manage the high-grade ore in water-saturated sandstone. Ore is processed at the McClean Lake mill (operated by Orano).

Production guided at 31.5–38.5M lbs U3O8 (100% basis) for 2026. Revenue is driven by a deep portfolio of long-term utility contracts with price escalators and floor mechanisms, providing revenue visibility through multi-year delivery commitments. Average realized prices guided at CAD 85–89/lb for FY2026. Jurisdictional advantage: Canada is an allied, politically stable mining jurisdiction with zero sanctions risk — a qualitative premium vs. Kazakhstan (Russia-adjacent, uncertain production discipline) and African producers (permitting and infrastructure risk).

### Fuel Services

Mid-cycle processing of uranium through Cameco's Canadian facilities:

- **Blind River Refinery**: Converts raw yellowcake (U3O8) into uranium trioxide (UO3), the intermediate step in the fuel processing chain.
- **Port Hope Conversion Facility**: Converts UO3 into uranium hexafluoride (UF6) — the gaseous form required for the enrichment step — and uranium dioxide (UO2) for reactors that use natural uranium fuel (e.g., CANDU reactors). One of only three major Western conversion facilities globally, creating a structural bottleneck as Russian fuel supply chains are sanctioned and replaced.

Cameco also holds a partial interest in **Global Laser Enrichment (GLE)**, a next-generation uranium enrichment technology using laser-based isotope separation. GLE received $28M in U.S. DOE funding — potentially a future Western enrichment capacity contributor addressing the ~44% Russian share of global enrichment.

### Westinghouse Electric (49% stake, with Brookfield)

Nuclear reactor technology, services, and fuel fabrication — the downstream completion of the fuel cycle:

- **Reactor Technology**: Westinghouse designs pressurized water reactors (PWRs) that power ~50% of the world's nuclear fleet. The AP1000 is the current-generation advanced passive safety reactor design with units operating in China and under construction globally.
- **Nuclear Fuel Fabrication**: Manufactures finished fuel assemblies (uranium pellets loaded into zirconium alloy fuel rods, assembled into fuel bundles) delivered ready for reactor loading. This completes the chain from mine to reactor.
- **Reactor Services**: Maintenance, instrumentation & control, outage management, decommissioning, and life extension services for the global installed reactor fleet. High-margin recurring revenue tied to the operational reactor base rather than new construction.
- **Government & Defense**: Part of the $80B U.S. government nuclear partnership with Brookfield/Cameco for reactor construction and national energy security infrastructure.

Westinghouse delivered $219M adjusted EBITDA to Cameco in FY2025 (+51% YoY). This recurring, high-margin services revenue is qualitatively different from commodity-price-driven mining revenue and structurally insulates Cameco from uranium spot price volatility.

## Industry Context

### The Structural Supply Deficit

The uranium market has been in structural deficit since the Fukushima-driven demand collapse (2011) led to a decade of mine closures and underinvestment. Global uranium production meets only ~90% of current reactor demand, with the gap filled by inventory drawdowns, secondary supplies (reprocessing, government stockpile sales), and Russian-origin material. With secondary supplies depleting and Russian material being sanctioned, the deficit is projected to widen to 197 million pounds cumulatively by 2040. The 2026 supply deficit is estimated at ~5 million lbs, but this understates the forward imbalance: mine restarts take 3–7 years, greenfield development takes 10–15 years, and there are no tier-one deposits in the pipeline comparable to Cameco's Saskatchewan assets.

### The Nuclear Renaissance in Numbers

2026 is the inflection year for global nuclear capacity:
- **75 reactors** currently under construction in 15 countries
- **~15 reactors** expected online in 2026, adding ~12 GW of new capacity (vs. net decline in 2025)
- **120+ reactors** planned (~110 GWe), **300+ proposed**
- **China** accounts for 50%+ of global construction (38 of 75 units); Linglong One SMR targeting first commercial operation in H1 2026 — world's first onshore commercial SMR
- **U.S. executive orders** target 400 GW nuclear capacity by 2050 (from ~100 GW today), 10 new large reactors under construction by 2030
- **DOE funding**: $400M each to TVA and Holtec for advanced light-water SMR deployment; $2.7B total for domestic nuclear fuel supply chain

### Cameco's Competitive Position

Cameco is the second-largest uranium producer globally (14–20% of world output), trailing only Kazakhstan's Kazatomprom. However, Cameco's competitive advantages are qualitatively different:

| Dimension | Cameco | Kazatomprom | Peer Group (UEC, NXE, DNN) |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Asset quality** | Tier-one: McArthur River (highest-grade mine globally at ~16% U3O8), Cigar Lake | Large-scale ISR operations, lower grade | Junior deposits, most pre-production |
| **Vertical integration** | Mining + conversion + fabrication + 49% Westinghouse (reactor services) | Mining + some conversion | Mining only (most) |
| **Jurisdictional safety** | Canada — allied, stable, no sanctions risk | Kazakhstan — geopolitically complex, Russia-adjacent, production discipline uncertain | Mixed (U.S., Canada, Africa) |
| **Contract book** | Deep portfolio of long-term utility contracts with price escalators and floor mechanisms | Primarily spot/medium-term | Limited or no contract books |
| **Valuation (P/NAV)** | 2.3x NAV ($25.5B attributable NAV) | 1.0x NAV ($20.7B) | Varies widely |

The valuation premium reflects the market's recognition that Cameco's fuel-cycle integration and jurisdictional safety are worth paying for — but the full extent of the re-rating (from commodity producer to energy infrastructure) is still underway.

### The Western Enrichment Bottleneck

Even with sufficient yellowcake, the Western nuclear fuel cycle faces a critical enrichment bottleneck. Russia's ~44% share of global enrichment capacity and near-monopoly on HALEU (High-Assay Low-Enriched Uranium, required for next-gen reactors) means that sanctions create a multi-year supply gap that cannot be closed quickly. Western enrichment capacity is concentrated at Urenco (Netherlands/UK/Germany/U.S.) and Orano (France). The U.S. DOE has awarded $900M each to Centrus Energy and General Matter (Peter Thiel-backed) for HALEU enrichment, plus $28M to Global Laser Enrichment (part-owned by Cameco) for next-gen enrichment technology. This bottleneck paradoxically benefits Cameco: as the provider of natural uranium and conversion services feeding these enrichment facilities, Cameco's upstream position becomes more valuable as enrichment capacity constrains the entire chain.

### The Financialization Dynamic

Physical uranium trusts (primarily Sprott Physical Uranium Trust) and institutional vehicles hold $12B+ in uranium inventory, tightening the spot market. This financialization amplifies price moves in both directions:
- **Bullish mechanism**: Physical buying removes pounds from the spot market, compressing available supply and forcing utilities to contract at higher prices
- **Bearish risk**: A sentiment reversal triggering trust redemptions could flood the spot market. However, Sprott's structure (closed-end trust with physical backing) makes forced liquidation less likely than open-ended fund structures
- **Structural implication**: Financialization has shifted the uranium market from a purely utility-driven procurement market to one with speculative/investment demand — creating both higher volatility and structurally higher price floors

### The Iran-Hormuz Geopolitical Accelerant

The 2026 U.S.-Iran conflict and effective Hormuz closure has served as a catalyst — not a cause — of the nuclear re-rating. The crisis demonstrated that:
- Oil, LNG, and coal supply chains are all vulnerable to maritime chokepoint disruption
- Nuclear is the only baseload source with terrestrial, allied-nation supply chains
- The insurance mechanism (war risk premiums making Hormuz transit commercially unviable) means even partial reopening doesn't immediately restore energy flows
- The policy response (reactor life extensions, new build acceleration, executive orders) outlasts the crisis itself

Uranium is ranked #1 most positively impacted commodity by the Iran conflict in the Gemini commodity impact analysis, and "strong second-round long" in the ChatGPT analysis — but critically, it is a 6–12 month policy-response trade, not a first-wave supply disruption play like LNG or crude.

## Bull Case

- Re-contracting wave drives long-term uranium contract prices to $100–120/lb, pulling Cameco's average realized prices materially higher through 2027–2028 as legacy contracts roll off and new floor mechanisms step up
- Uranium spot breaks $100/lb sustainably as Kazatomprom cuts, mine restart delays, and Russian sanctions compress available supply
- AI data center nuclear commitments (Meta 6.6 GW, Microsoft TMI restart, Amazon/Google SMR investments) create multi-decade demand visibility independent of geopolitics
- Westinghouse EBITDA compounds at 20–30% annually as reactor services and fuel fabrication scale with the global buildout; provides Cameco with high-margin recurring revenue insulating it from commodity price volatility
- U.S. $80B nuclear partnership materializes with favorable terms for Cameco/Westinghouse, providing both capital and demand certainty for decades
- Iran conflict persists or de-escalation fails to reverse the energy sovereignty policy momentum — reactor life extensions and new builds are multi-year commitments that don't reverse on a ceasefire
- China's reactor buildout (38 under construction) creates incremental demand for Western uranium as domestic Chinese supply falls short
- Western enrichment capacity development (Centrus, Global Laser Enrichment) eventually creates new demand for Cameco's upstream products

## Bear Case

- Geopolitical de-escalation collapses the energy sovereignty premium; Hormuz reopens, oil normalizes, and the political urgency for nuclear buildout fades
- Kazatomprom reverses planned production cut, flooding the market with low-cost ISR uranium; Kazakhstan returns to 100%+ of subsoil use agreement levels
- SMR deployment timelines slip by 3–5 years (NuScale already restructured once), reducing near-term demand growth and undermining Big Tech nuclear commitments
- Financialization unwind: Sprott redemptions or institutional liquidation floods the spot market, triggering a cascading selloff from $80/lb to $50–60/lb
- Valuation at 2.3x NAV and implied ~92x P/E leaves no margin for execution disappointment; any guidance miss triggers severe multiple compression
- African uranium supply comes online faster than expected (Niger, Namibia, Tanzania greenfield projects) easing the structural deficit
- Western enrichment bottleneck persists so long that it actually constrains reactor fuel loading, reducing effective uranium demand even as raw demand grows
- Nuclear regulatory/permitting headwinds: NIMBY opposition, NRC licensing delays, or a reactor safety incident reverses public/political sentiment

- **Iran conflict resolution or escalation** — either path affects the pace (not the direction) of the energy sovereignty shift
- **Sprott Physical Uranium Trust buying activity** — net purchases vs. redemptions signal institutional conviction
- **Western enrichment capacity announcements** — Centrus/Global Laser Enrichment commercial-scale timelines

## Risks

1. **Geopolitical de-escalation risk:** Iran deal or Hormuz reopening removes urgency from energy sovereignty narrative; while structural deficit persists, the valuation premium compresses sharply
2. **Kazatomprom supply risk:** Kazakhstan production discipline is historically unreliable; a return to full capacity would add 5–10M+ lbs annually, easing the deficit
3. **Valuation risk:** At $50.6B market cap, ~2.3x NAV, and ~92x trailing P/E, the stock prices in substantial future growth; any execution miss or uranium price decline triggers disproportionate downside
4. **SMR deployment risk:** NuScale's restructuring and general SMR licensing uncertainty could push Big Tech nuclear timelines out by years, reducing demand growth narrative
5. **Financialization/unwind risk:** $12B+ of institutional uranium holdings amplify both upside and downside; a sentiment reversal could temporarily overwhelm fundamental supply-demand
6. **Enrichment bottleneck risk:** If Western enrichment capacity doesn't scale, the effective demand for natural uranium could be constrained even as theoretical demand grows — reducing Cameco's revenue visibility
7. **Regulatory/NIMBY risk:** Reactor approvals face political headwinds; a single safety incident could reverse decades of pro-nuclear sentiment
8. **China self-sufficiency risk:** If China develops domestic supply chains at scale, it could reduce one of the largest incremental demand sources for Western producers
