# ASMI - ASM International

## Summary

ASMI is mispriced as an "ALD pure-play with cyclical capex exposure" when it is structurally a process-of-record (POR) annuity business with two compounding engines (ALD + Epi) and ~$400-500M SAM expansion per logic node. The sell-side framing of "55% ALD market share" obscures the real economics: each node win locks ASMI into 5-7 years of consumables-like equipment revenue across every wafer the foundry runs at that geometry, and the 1.4nm transition (TSMC pilot H2 2026, Samsung 2027) is doubling ALD intensity per wafer through gate-all-around (GAA), backside power delivery, and dipole engineering layers. The market is pricing ASMI on TTM P/E of ~45x assuming Lam's ALTUS Halo and AMAT's Trillium close the share gap; the non-consensus read is that those tools attack adjacencies (Mo ALD for NAND wordlines, batch ALD for spacers) rather than ASMI's high-k metal gate stack core, and the POR annuity from N3/N2 wins funds the next decade regardless of competitive noise. With CFET (epi-grown stacked channels) emerging as the post-GAA architecture beyond 2030, ASMI's epi franchise — currently treated as a #2 footnote behind AMAT — becomes a second compounding leg. Conviction medium pending verification of 1.4nm POR slot count.

## Key Non-consensus Insights

**The POR qualification annuity is the real business, not market share.** When ASMI's Intrepid XP qualifies as the process-of-record for a critical layer at TSMC N2 or Samsung SF2, the revenue stream from that win extends across every wafer that fab produces at that node for the next 5-7 years. Sell-side models treat WFE share as fungible — "ASMI did 55% in 2024, will it be 52% in 2026?" — but the underlying contracts behave more like SaaS renewals than capital sales. Once a foundry has burned 18+ months qualifying a tool on a specific layer, switching to a competitor mid-node would require requalifying the entire process flow, which essentially never happens. The implication: ASMI's revenue stream from N3/N2 wins is locked through ~2031, and the only competitive battle that matters is the qualification race for the next node (1.4nm and below). This reframes ASMI from a cyclical equipment vendor to a structurally compounding annuity stream where each node win adds a 5-7 year revenue layer on top of the existing book — closer to the EUV-tool economics ASML enjoys than the deposition-tool economics analysts price in.

**ASMI is structurally a two-engine play (ALD + Epi), and the Epi engine is dramatically underweighted.** Consensus treats ASMI as "the ALD company," with epi as a $700M-ish footnote behind Applied Materials' Epi business. This ignores that epi becomes architecturally critical at CFET (complementary FET, the post-GAA architecture targeted for 2030+), where the n-channel and p-channel transistors are stacked vertically rather than placed side-by-side, requiring multiple precisely epi-grown SiGe and Si layers for channel formation. ASMI's Intrepid Epitaxy platform is already the #2 player and has been gaining share at TSMC and Samsung in selective epi for source/drain regions at N3/N2. If CFET arrives on schedule, epi intensity per wafer roughly doubles, and ASMI's epi franchise transitions from "diversification line item" to "second ALD-style compounding engine." The sell-side does not yet treat epi as a strategic franchise; the equity gets re-rated when they do.

**Hidden book value in ASMPT stake and Axus acquisition optionality.** ASMI owns ~25% of ASMPT (HK: 0522), the back-end packaging and surface-mount equipment company, currently worth ~$1.0-1.2B on a market basis but carried on the balance sheet at lower equity-method value. Activist investors have repeatedly pressed ASMI to either consolidate or divest the stake; either path is value-accretive — divestiture would unlock cash for buybacks at a 40x multiple, consolidation would add hybrid bonding (a structural HBM growth vector) to the front-end-equipment story. Separately, the December 2024 Axus Technology acquisition put ASMI into chemical-mechanical polishing (CMP), historically a Lam/AMAT/Ebara market. Axus is small (~$50M revenue) but ASMI is now positioned to bundle CMP with adjacent ALD process modules — a competitive vector that has not been priced in. Both options are out-of-the-money calls the market is pricing at zero.

**The "Lam ALTUS Halo / AMAT Trillium will steal ASMI's share" narrative attacks adjacencies, not the core franchise.** The bear case repeated in sell-side notes is that Lam's ALTUS Halo Mo (molybdenum) ALD tool and AMAT's Trillium platform will erode ASMI's ALD position at advanced nodes. Reading the actual product positioning shows these tools target Mo replacement of W (tungsten) in NAND wordlines and DRAM bitlines — both domains where ASMI was already a smaller player and where conductor-fill applications differ chemically from ASMI's high-k metal gate (HKMG) stack. ASMI's deepest moat is in the gate stack itself: HfO2 high-k, dipole engineering layers (lanthanum, aluminum), work-function metals (TiN, TiAl), and channel cap layers — chemistries Lam and AMAT have not productized at HVM scale. Treating ALD as monolithic when it is actually 8+ chemically distinct sub-markets is the core analytical error in the bear thesis.

**Complexity-driven cycle dynamics support a process-control-style multiple, not a deposition-tool multiple.** Process-control vendor KLAC trades at ~30x forward earnings on a thesis that complexity at advanced nodes drives metrology intensity per wafer non-linearly (more layers = exponentially more inspection). ASMI's economics are structurally similar: layer count at N2 vs N5 is up ~40%, and ALD-deposited layers specifically rise faster than overall layer count because new architecture features (GAA inner spacers, backside power vias, dipole stacks) all require ALD. ASMI gets paid per process step, and process steps per wafer at advanced nodes are growing faster than wafer counts. The market still applies a deposition-tool multiple (~25x consensus) which prices in the cyclical capex pattern; the complexity-cycle reframe argues for KLAC-like 30-35x as a structural ceiling. The 45x forward currently embedded already partly reflects this — but the gap is justified, not bubble-like.

## Outstanding Questions

**How many POR slots does ASMI hold at TSMC 1.4nm and Samsung SF1.4 versus N2/SF2, and what is the layer-count escalation specifically for ALD?** ASMI has guided $400-500M SAM expansion per node, but this aggregates across customers and layer types. The investment case strengthens materially if ASMI is winning incremental layers (not just maintaining existing slot count) at the 1.4nm transition. TSMC's N2 design rules are public; the 1.4nm rules are not yet, and analyst estimates of GAA inner-spacer ALD count vary from 4 to 8 layers per stack. Confirmation that ASMI is locking incremental POR wins at 1.4nm — rather than defending existing slots against Lam — is the single highest-impact data point. Watching: TSMC Q3 2026 earnings commentary on N2/A14 yield and tool qualification cadence; ASMI Q2 2026 earnings (mid-July) for booking commentary on 1.4nm tool shipments.

**Does the 3D DRAM architectural transition (~2027-2028) shuffle ALD vendor share, and does it favor or disadvantage ASMI?** Memory currently uses primarily batch ALD tools where ASMI has lower share than Tokyo Electron (TEL) and Kokusai. 3D DRAM moves the architecture toward stacked dielectric/conductor pairs that look more like 3D NAND — potentially favoring batch-style throughput. But the dielectric chemistries (high-k stacks, ferroelectric HfO2 for FeRAM-adjacent designs) align with ASMI's single-wafer expertise. The vendor share at first 3D DRAM HVM (Samsung 2027 reportedly) could go either way and is genuinely under-modeled. Watching: Samsung 2027 capex breakdown by tool category; SK Hynix 3D DRAM roadmap commentary.

**What is the actual scope of MATCH Act servicing-ban exposure, and how much overlaps with ASMI's installed base?** The proposed MATCH Act extends US export controls to include service and software for restricted equipment — already shipped tools could become unservicable for Chinese customers. ASMI's China revenue (~20% of total) is concentrated in trailing-edge ALD for analog/power applications at SMIC, Hua Hong, and Yangtze Memory. The question is whether existing tools fall under the servicing ban or whether the ban is forward-looking only. Bear case: a 2026-2027 enforcement adds 5-8% revenue cliff. Bull case: the ban is grandfathered and only restricts new shipments, which were already declining. Watching: BIS final rule publication, ASMI Q2/Q3 2026 commentary on China shipping pattern.

**Does the ASMPT stake monetization (consolidation, divestiture, or activist resolution) crystallize in 2026, and at what multiple?** Activist pressure on the ASMPT relationship has built since 2023 (Petrus Advisers, others) and the stake is not strategic to ASMI's front-end equipment franchise. ASMPT trades on Hong Kong at ~3.5x book, ~25x earnings — well below ASMI's 45x but above hypothetical liquidation. Divestiture proceeds (~$1.0-1.2B) could fund a buyback that retires ~3% of ASMI shares at the current price. Watching: ASMI AGM (May 11, 2026) for capital allocation commentary; activist 13D filings or open letters.

**Is the current 45x forward P/E sustainable through a 2027 capex normalization, or does multiple compression dominate growth?** The 2025-2026 hyperscaler capex surge is the proximate driver of ASMI's revenue acceleration. If hyperscaler capex normalizes (or contracts) in 2027, ASMI's bookings would slow even as POR wins keep base revenue intact. Historical equipment cycles show multiples compress 30-50% during downturns, even when revenue holds — the question is whether the POR annuity reframing is accepted by then or whether ASMI gets re-rated like a generic deposition tool vendor. Watching: Hyperscaler capex guidance (Microsoft, Meta, Google, Amazon Q4 2026 commentary), TSMC capex guidance for 2027.

**Has the Axus CMP acquisition produced any product-revenue synergy, or is it still a strategic option?** Axus brought ~$50M revenue and was positioned as enabling integrated CMP-ALD process modules — a thesis that depends on customer willingness to bundle. As of Q1 2026, ASMI has not disclosed CMP-specific revenue traction. The acquisition is either a quietly successful tuck-in or a nascent strategic option that may or may not pay off. Watching: ASMI Capital Markets Day (rumored Q4 2026) for CMP product disclosure.

## Business Model & Product Description

ASMI sells deposition equipment to the world's leading semiconductor manufacturers — TSMC, Samsung, Intel, SK Hynix, Micron, and Chinese foundries. Conceptually the company is the "atomic-scale 3D printer vendor" for the chip industry: each tool deposits films one atomic layer at a time using sequential gas-phase chemical reactions, building up the precise material stacks (high-k dielectrics, work-function metals, channel materials) that make modern transistors function. Revenue mix splits roughly 65% logic/foundry, 20% memory, 15% other (analog, power, image sensors).

**Atomic Layer Deposition (ALD) — Intrepid platform.** ALD is the core franchise. The Intrepid XP (single-wafer) and Intrepid ES platforms deposit 1-30nm thick films one atomic layer at a time, alternating gas pulses to grow material with sub-nanometer precision. The economic moat comes from process-of-record (POR) qualification: customers spend 12-24 months qualifying each ASMI tool on a specific layer in a specific process; once qualified, the tool runs that layer for the entire node lifecycle (5-7 years). At 2nm/N2, ASMI estimates each 100,000 wafer-per-month fab generates $400M of equipment SAM for ASMI, doubling from the $200M figure at the 7nm node. ASMI holds ~55% market share in single-wafer ALD globally, with TEL and Kokusai dominating batch ALD. The chemistries that matter most: HfO2 (gate dielectric), TiN (work-function metal for nFET), TiAl/TiAlC (work-function metal for pFET), Al2O3 (capping/spacer), La/LaO (dipole engineering), and SiOC/SiCN (low-k spacers). Each is a distinct sub-market with distinct competitive dynamics.

Q: Why do fans still use single wafer equipment for ALD instead of relying on more efficient multi wafers. Why didn’t ASMI produce multi wafer machines

**Epitaxy — Intrepid Epi platform (formerly Epsilon).** Epi grows single-crystal silicon and silicon-germanium (SiGe) layers on top of existing crystal wafers — used to form the source/drain regions of FinFET and GAA transistors and to grow the channel layers of advanced devices. ASMI is #2 globally in epi (behind AMAT), with strong positions in SiGe selective epi for advanced logic at TSMC and Samsung. The CFET architecture (post-GAA, ~2030+) requires roughly 2x the epi steps per wafer because the n-channel and p-channel are stacked vertically with epi-grown channels separating them. This is the sleeper line of business: not yet priced in, but architecturally critical to the next decade.

**PEALD and PECVD adjacencies.** Plasma-enhanced ALD and CVD tools target lower-temperature and conformal-deposition applications — used in spacers, hardmasks, and certain low-k dielectrics. Smaller revenue contribution; competitive positioning vs Lam and AMAT depends on specific application.

**Axus CMP (December 2024 acquisition).** Chemical-mechanical polishing — historically dominated by AMAT and Ebara — now adds a process-module adjacency. Initial revenue ~$50M; strategic value lies in integrated CMP-ALD bundling.

**Geographic mix:** Q4 2025 — Korea ~35%, Taiwan ~25%, China ~20%, US/Europe ~15%, Japan ~5%. China exposure has declined from peak ~30% as export controls have tightened.

**Financial profile:** Q1 2026 revenue €863M (+27% YoY), gross margin 53.3%, operating margin 33.1%, net cash position ~€1B. Roughly 25% revenue CAGR 2022-2025, decelerating to ~12% constant-currency growth guided for 2026 as the 2nd half of 2025 hyperscaler capex pull-forward normalizes.

## Industry Context

ASMI competes in deposition (~$22B WFE segment in 2024, growing to $30B+ by 2030) against four major peers: Applied Materials (AMAT), Lam Research (LRCX), Tokyo Electron (TEL), and Kokusai Electric. Each has a distinct historical position:

| Vendor | Deposition strength | ALD position | Epi position |
|--------|--------------------|--------------|--------------|
| **AMAT** | CVD, PVD, Epi (#1) | Smaller in single-wafer ALD; Trillium platform launched 2024 | #1 in epi (Centura) |
| **LRCX** | CVD (NAND), conductor etch | ALTUS Halo Mo ALD launched 2024 — targets W replacement | Minor |
| **TEL** | CVD, batch ALD (#1) | Dominant in memory batch ALD | Minor |
| **ASMI** | Single-wafer ALD (#1, ~55%) | #1 single-wafer; $400M+ SAM/100K WSPM at 2nm | #2 in epi (Intrepid Epi) |
| **Kokusai** | Batch furnace, batch ALD | Strong in memory; weaker in logic | None |

**The competitive landscape splits along three axes:** (1) tool architecture — single-wafer vs batch — where ASMI dominates single-wafer ALD and TEL/Kokusai dominate batch; (2) chemistry — high-k/metal-gate vs conductor fill (Mo, W, Co) — where ASMI leads gate-stack and Lam/AMAT are pushing into Mo conductor fill; (3) application — logic vs memory — where ASMI is ~70% logic-weighted and TEL/Kokusai are more balanced.

**Customer concentration:** TSMC + Samsung + Intel together account for roughly 65-70% of ASMI revenue. The relationship with TSMC is the deepest — ASMI tools have been embedded in every node from N7 forward, and TSMC's ~$60B+ annual capex feeds the bookings cycle. Samsung is the swing customer: any Samsung capex acceleration or pause moves ASMI bookings 5-10% in the relevant quarter. Intel's foundry ramp (Intel 18A, ~2026 HVM) is a smaller incremental line but adds optionality.

**Pricing power and margin trajectory:** ASMI gross margin has expanded from ~45% in 2018 to ~53% in 2025, driven by mix shift to advanced-node logic (higher-priced tools, more chemistries per node) and operational leverage as installed-base service revenue grows. Operating margin compounds further: 33% in Q1 2026 vs 25% in 2018, with management guiding 35% medium-term target. The pricing dynamic is asymmetric: customers cannot negotiate aggressively on POR-qualified tools without risking a re-qualification cycle, so ASMI captures most of the value created by node-over-node intensity expansion.

**Value chain position:** ASMI sits in the "essential but invisible" layer of the chip supply chain. The end customer (Apple, NVIDIA, AMD, hyperscaler) sees TSMC; TSMC sees ASMI as one of ~12 critical equipment vendors whose tools must qualify. The leverage point is the foundry's own switching cost — each node transition requires hundreds of tools to qualify, and the foundry has no incentive to displace incumbent vendors mid-cycle without strong reason. This structural dynamic favors incumbents broadly and ASMI specifically given the depth of qualification work at HKMG.

## Bull Case

ASMI doubles revenue from ~€3.2B (2025) to ~€6.5-7B by 2030 driven by three independent vectors: (1) **1.4nm ALD layer-count expansion** adds $400M+ SAM per major fab, with ASMI capturing 55%+ of the layer wins on existing POR; (2) **CFET architecture** (~2030+) doubles epi intensity, repositioning ASMI's epi franchise as a second ALD-class growth engine; (3) **3D DRAM transition** opens a memory-mix expansion if ASMI captures share at the 2027-2028 inflection. Operating margin expands to 36-38% on mix and operating leverage. The ASMPT stake monetizes (divestiture or consolidation) at ~$1.5B+, funding a buyback. Valuation re-rates to a process-control-style 35-40x forward earnings as the POR annuity model gets recognized. 2030 EPS €30-35; 35x = €1,050-1,225 share price (vs ~€840 today) — roughly 30-50% upside over 4 years before dividends and buybacks, ~10% IRR. Asymmetry comes from the optionality stacking: each independent vector compounds, and the multiple expansion is gravy.

## Bear Case

Lam Research's ALTUS Halo Mo ALD tool wins POR at TSMC 1.4nm or Samsung SF1.4 for one or more critical layers (e.g., the work-function metal stack), and AMAT's Trillium captures share in HKMG dipole engineering. ASMI's effective single-wafer ALD share compresses from ~55% to ~45-48% by 2028, and the layer-count expansion narrative breaks because ASMI is gaining fewer slots per node than the bull case requires. Simultaneously, hyperscaler capex normalizes in 2027 (compute demand growth slows; AI training infrastructure overbuilt), TSMC and Samsung pause N2/1.4nm capacity additions, and ASMI bookings decline 15-20% YoY. The MATCH Act enforcement adds a 5-8% China revenue cliff. Multiple compresses from 45x to 25x (deposition-tool average through cycle). EPS holds flat at ~€14-15 through 2029. Stock falls to €350-425 (-50% to -60% from current). The CFET narrative is real but pushed to ~2032+, deferring the epi franchise re-rating beyond the relevant investment window. ASMPT stake remains stranded as activists fail to force resolution. Bear case is not implausible — competitive intensity in ALD has risen visibly in the past 18 months, and ASMI bulls may be over-extrapolating from N3/N2 wins to 1.4nm wins that haven't been confirmed.

## Risks

**Customer concentration risk.** TSMC + Samsung + Intel = ~65-70% of revenue. A single-customer capex pause (especially TSMC) compresses bookings 15%+ in the relevant quarter. TSMC's 2027 capex plan is the single most important variable.

**Competitive encroachment from Lam (ALTUS Halo) and AMAT (Trillium).** Both have launched competitive single-wafer ALD platforms targeting ASMI's home turf. Even modest share loss (5-7 percentage points) materially compresses revenue growth and margin.

**China escalation / MATCH Act.** A servicing ban on already-shipped tools could create a 5-8% revenue cliff and force impairment of installed-base service contracts. Forward-looking only ban is a smaller hit.

**Valuation risk.** 45x forward P/E embeds significant POR-annuity recognition. If hyperscaler capex normalizes faster than expected or competitive share losses crystallize, multiple compression to 25-30x adds 30-50% downside independent of EPS.

**Hyperscaler capex normalization.** AI training infrastructure capex is the proximate driver of 2025-2026 acceleration. A normalization or contraction in 2027 cycles back through TSMC/Samsung capex into ASMI bookings.

**Activist outcome risk on ASMPT.** Activist pressure could force a value-destructive resolution (e.g., dilutive consolidation at premium price) rather than the value-accretive divestiture the bull case assumes.

**Position risk distinct from thesis risk:** Even if the thesis is correct (ASMI maintains POR share, 1.4nm wins materialize), the stock can decline sharply on hyperscaler capex headlines or sell-side downgrades unrelated to ASMI fundamentals — semicap stocks have 1.5-2x beta to the broader semiconductor cycle.
