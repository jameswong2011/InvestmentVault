# LNG — Cheniere Energy

## Summary

~53 mtpa operational liquefaction capacity (growing toward 75+ mtpa), ~95% take-or-pay contracts averaging 16 years remaining, $120B+ contracted revenues through 2050, zero Hormuz transit exposure — toll-road economics generating $6.9B FY2025 EBITDA with infrastructure-grade downside protection. The March 2026 Ras Laffan attack (12.8 mtpa damaged, 3-5 year repair timeline) transformed a secular growth story into an acute geopolitical hedge, sending the stock +45% YTD to ~$284. The April 8 ceasefire introduces near-term uncertainty but does not resolve the structural supply gap: replacement gas turbines face 2-4 year lead times, Qatar has declared long-term force majeure to four countries, and the insurance mechanism that killed commercial Hormuz transit normalizes far slower than the ceasefire implies. At 7.5-8.5x trailing EV/EBITDA (vs. five-year avg ~9.8x), five credit upgrades to BBB+, $10B+ buyback targeting ~175M shares, and a path to >$30/share DCF by decade's end — the geopolitical premium is a bonus layered on an already compelling infrastructure compounder.

## Key Non-consensus Insights

- **The toll-road contract model is the most misunderstood business model in energy, and it means volatility is Cheniere's friend rather than its enemy.** ~95% of cash flows from take-or-pay SPAs with fixed fees ($2.25-3.50/MMBtu) owed regardless of cargo lifting; $1/MMBtu market margin change impacts EBITDA by <$50M on <1 mtpa open capacity. Volatility accelerates new contract signings at higher fees, drives FID, and generates spot windfalls — 2022 precedent: EBITDA spiked to $11.6B (TTF >$50/MMBtu) then normalized to $6.2B, revealing both optionality and contracted floor. *(Source: Q4 2025 earnings call)*

- **Europe's "LNG bridge" strategy has become a structural trap that the April 8 ceasefire does not resolve — this is a double lock-in the market has not fully modeled.** 12.8 mtpa of Qatari capacity (Trains 4 and 6) requires replacement gas turbines with 2-4 year lead times — a binding industrial constraint, not a geopolitical one. European storage at ~29% vs. 59% five-year average creates restocking demand through the next heating season. European procurement officers who watched Qatar go offline overnight will structurally favor Cheniere's Gulf Coast facilities — a demand lock-in persisting years beyond any ceasefire. *(Source: web research Apr 2026)*

- **The insurance mechanism has created a permanent "normalization lag" that markets consistently underestimate — and the ceasefire demonstrates this rather than refuting it.** War risk premiums surged from 0.125% to 1-3% of hull value within 48 hours ($1M+ per laden LNG carrier transit). Iran charging $1M+ tolls, insurers demand weeks-to-months of sustained safety before repricing, 14 laden LNG cargoes trapped in the Gulf. No pipeline bypass exists for LNG (unlike crude with 3.5-5.5 mb/d pipeline capacity) — yet equity investors anchor to Brent backwardation to price Cheniere. Crude-LNG normalization divergence is the most actionable mispricing in the energy complex. *(Source: web research Apr 2026)*

- **Cheniere's brownfield cost advantage is widening at exactly the moment when it matters most, and competitors' execution risk is cascading in a way that deepens the moat with every stumble.** All-in delivered cost $6.50-7.00/MMBtu brownfield vs. $8.50-10.80/MMBtu greenfield competitors facing EPC escalation, tariffs, and Gulf Coast labor competition. CCL Stage 3 Trains 1-5 on/ahead of schedule; FID on Trains 8&9 June 2025 with piling halfway done; SPL Expansion (~20 mtpa) targeting 2027 FID. Meanwhile: Venture Global's commissioning dispute triggered Shell/BP/TotalEnergies arbitrations (TotalEnergies refuses new contracts citing trust); Golden Pass EPC contractor Zachry filed bankruptcy; Tellurian spent $1B+ pre-FID before Woodside acquired it for $900M. 4,160+ cumulative cargoes to 45+ countries over 10+ years. *(Sources: Cheniere investor presentations, web research Apr 2026)*

- **Gas-to-coal substitution is a demand floor, not a demand ceiling — and the ceasefire paradoxically extends the elevated pricing regime rather than killing it.** 40-90 million tonnes of gas-to-coal switching underway across Asia and Europe; Japan relaxing coal-power limits, Newcastle thermal coal at 14-month high. Coal substitution acts as a pressure valve preventing panic-driven demand destruction that would crash LNG prices, while preserving long-run structural demand (coal-to-gas reverses on normalization). Japan has ~3 weeks of strategic LNG reserves; ceasefire prevents economic collapse but Ras Laffan damage means the supply gap persists for years. Best possible environment for Cheniere's contracted + spot model.

## Business Model & Product Description

Cheniere's business model is best understood through an analogy: if global LNG trade is an interstate highway system, Cheniere owns the largest and most heavily-trafficked toll plazas. Trucks (LNG cargoes) must pass through, paying a fixed toll (the liquefaction fee) regardless of what they're carrying or what the goods are worth at their destination. Cheniere doesn't own the trucks, doesn't set the price of goods, and doesn't care whether the economy is booming or recessing — it collects a toll on every passage. The geopolitical crisis has simply increased traffic volume and given Cheniere leverage to charge higher tolls on new highway lanes under construction.

More precisely, Cheniere operates two world-class liquefaction facilities on the US Gulf Coast — **Sabine Pass LNG** in Cameron Parish, Louisiana (6 operational trains, ~30 mtpa) and **Corpus Christi LNG** in San Patricio County, Texas (~23 mtpa operational across original trains plus Stage 3 Trains 1-5, with Trains 6-7 completing by end 2026). Combined operational capacity now exceeds **53 mtpa**, making Cheniere the largest LNG producer in the Western Hemisphere and one of the largest globally. An additional ~8 mtpa is under construction (CCL Stage 3 remaining trains plus Trains 8&9 debottlenecking), and the SPL Expansion (~20 mtpa) is targeting permits by end 2026 and FID on Phase 1 in 2027. At full buildout, Cheniere's platform could exceed **80+ mtpa** — equivalent to roughly 10% of projected global LNG trade by 2030.

### Revenue Architecture: The SPA Tolling Model

The core revenue engine is the **Sale and Purchase Agreement (SPA)**, which follows a two-component pricing formula:

1. **Fixed Liquefaction Fee: $2.25-$3.50/MMBtu** — This is the toll. It is take-or-pay, meaning the offtaker owes this fee regardless of whether they actually lift the cargo. If a buyer cancels, they forfeit the variable fee but still owe the full fixed fee — creating a revenue floor even in depressed markets. A portion (11.5-15%) of the fixed fee escalates annually with CPI, providing built-in inflation protection. Contracts typically run **20 years with 10-year extension options**.

2. **Variable Fee: 115% of Henry Hub** — This is essentially a cost pass-through. Cheniere procures feedstock natural gas at approximately Henry Hub prices and charges 115% to cover processing, fuel gas shrinkage (~8-10% of throughput), and minor operating costs. The 15% markup above Henry Hub is Cheniere's operational margin on the variable component.

The critical insight is that the fixed fee is Cheniere's core margin, and it is contractually guaranteed. Cheniere has **$120B+ in remaining fixed-fee contracted revenues through 2050** with **35+ counterparties** carrying a weighted-average credit rating of **A-/A3**. This is the contracted floor beneath which cash flows cannot fall.

### The IPM Marketing Overlay

Cheniere's **Integrated Production Marketing (IPM)** agreements are a mirror image of traditional SPAs, designed to capture the other side of the LNG value chain. Under IPM deals, an upstream gas producer sells natural gas to Cheniere at a price indexed to international benchmarks (JKM or TTF) minus a fixed liquefaction fee and shipping costs. Cheniere then sells the LNG at the same international benchmark. The structure locks in a fixed liquefaction margin regardless of absolute commodity prices while giving Cheniere **direct portfolio exposure to international price dynamics**.

Recent IPM agreements include a 15-year deal with **Canadian Natural Resources** for ~0.85 mtpa at JKM-minus pricing and a TTF-linked supply agreement with **ARC Resources** for the SPL Expansion. These agreements diversify Cheniere's gas procurement beyond the Gulf Coast and provide upstream producers with access to premium international markets through Cheniere's liquefaction and marketing infrastructure.

IPM contracts are marked-to-market as derivatives, creating substantial non-cash earnings volatility — $7.1B of favorable fair value changes in 2023 collapsed to $1.4B in 2024. But the realized cash economics flow through as stable, contracted margins. Investors should look through the accounting noise to the underlying economics.

### Revenue Segmentation: Contracted vs. Spot

Cheniere's revenue composition reflects a deliberate balance between cash flow predictability and commodity optionality:

- **~95% Contracted (Take-or-Pay SPAs + IPM):** This is the infrastructure-grade floor. Approximately 47-50 mtpa of the 53 mtpa operational capacity is committed under long-term contracts. These volumes generate predictable EBITDA regardless of market conditions — the $6.2B in FY2024 EBITDA during normalized markets demonstrates the floor.

- **~5% Uncommitted/Spot:** Less than 1 mtpa in 2026 is open to spot sales. On these volumes, Cheniere captures the full international benchmark price minus feedstock and shipping costs. At the current ~$15-16/MMBtu JKM-HH spread, this translates to ~$350-450M of incremental EBITDA above base guidance. During the 2022 crisis (TTF >$50/MMBtu), spot and marketing activities contributed an estimated $3-5B of incremental EBITDA. The spot optionality is real but deliberately constrained — management prioritizes contracted certainty over speculative upside.

### The Capital Return Machine

Cheniere's capital allocation framework is the compounding engine that transforms stable EBITDA into accelerating per-share value:

- **$10B+ share repurchase authorization through 2030**, targeting a share count reduction from ~210M to ~175M (approximately ~10% of shares already retired since the 20/20 Vision plan began)
- **$2.22/share annual dividend** growing at ~10% annually, with the dividend increased by ~68% since inception in late 2021 and a payout ratio of only 8.5%
- **"20/20 Vision" plan completed ahead of schedule** ($20B deployed, $20/share DCF achieved), replaced by a new target: >$25B of available cash through 2030 to achieve >$30/share run-rate distributable cash flow
- **Five credit upgrades in 2025** across the corporate structure, now solidly investment grade at BBB+

The combination of ~10% annual dividend growth, aggressive buybacks, and capacity expansion from ~53 mtpa to potentially 80+ mtpa creates a per-share cash flow growth trajectory that belies the modest headline EBITDA growth. The path to $30/share DCF represents roughly 20-25% upside from today's ~$25/share on a metric the market will eventually capitalize at infrastructure multiples.

## Industry Context

### The Global LNG Supply-Demand Landscape

Global LNG trade in 2025 was approximately **400 mtpa**, with demand driven by three structural forces: European gas security post-Russia, Asian industrialization (particularly Southeast Asia and India), and growing interest in LNG as a transition fuel. Pre-crisis projections anticipated ~300 bcm of new global LNG capacity by 2030, potentially creating a ~65 bcm surplus. The Ras Laffan attack has absorbed much of this projected surplus, but the interaction between new supply (arriving 2026-2028 from multiple US projects) and the pace of Qatari restoration will determine whether oversupply re-emerges.

### The Competitive Landscape: Why No Competitor Matches Cheniere's Moat

**Qatar** — the only player with a structural cost advantage globally at $3.80-5.60/MMBtu all-in delivered — saw its expansion timeline called into question by the Ras Laffan attack. North Field East (~33 mtpa, first train commissioning was targeting mid-2026) and North Field South (~16 mtpa) represent massive low-cost supply additions, but the March 2026 damage and ceasefire uncertainty have introduced a timeline premium that didn't exist before. Even at full capacity, Qatar's Hormuz transit requirement now carries a permanent risk discount that advantages Atlantic Basin producers.

**Venture Global** (~30 mtpa across Calcasieu Pass and Plaquemines, CP2 under construction, plus a 31 mtpa expansion application): The most aggressive US competitor, with FY2025 EBITDA of $6.3B — approaching Cheniere's scale. Plaquemines Phase 1 COD targeted Q4 2026. Liquefaction fees of ~$2.30/MMBtu undercut Cheniere's $2.25-3.50. But the **5.95x debt-to-equity ratio**, ongoing arbitration with Shell, BP, and TotalEnergies (won Repsol case; TotalEnergies refuses new contracts), and the prolonged "commissioning" dispute where Venture Global sold commissioning cargoes at spot prices instead of honoring contracted rates have created a reliability discount. The IPO (NYSE: VG) in early 2026 provided capital but also transparency into a highly leveraged balance sheet. For any offtaker evaluating a 20-year take-or-pay commitment, the counterparty credit quality and operational trust gap between Cheniere and Venture Global remains wide.

**Sempra / Port Arthur LNG** (~13 mtpa Phase 1, Train 1 expected 2027, Train 2 2028; ~13 mtpa Phase 2 FID September 2025): Well-capitalized JV with ConocoPhillips and KKR, EPC by Bechtel. Phase 2 owned jointly with Blackstone-led consortium. Likely the most credible new entrant given Sempra's utility-grade balance sheet and Bechtel's execution record, but first LNG is still over a year away.

**NextDecade / Rio Grande LNG** (~30 mtpa across Trains 1-5, all under construction): Commercially well-supported by ADNOC, TotalEnergies, Aramco, and JERA. First train commissioning targeting year-end 2026, ahead of schedule. An ambitious greenfield with strong offtaker backing, but unproven operational track record.

**Freeport LNG** (~15-17 mtpa, private): Suffered a major explosion in 2022 and periodic outages since. Considering Train 4 expansion but no FID. Operational reliability concerns persist.

**Woodside Louisiana LNG** (formerly Driftwood/Tellurian, 16.5 mtpa Phase 1): FID April 2025 after Woodside acquired the financially distressed Tellurian for $900M. First LNG targeted 2029 — years behind Cheniere's active projects.

**Australia** (~88 mtpa operational): Mature, high-cost ($7-9/MMBtu delivered), with limited expansion potential and recent cyclone-related disruptions.

**Mozambique** (TotalEnergies ~13 mtpa): Resumed construction January 2026 after years of insurgency-related delays. First LNG not until 2029. Security risk remains.

**Russia / Arctic LNG 2**: Effectively stranded under Western sanctions. Russia's ability to influence global LNG markets is structurally constrained.

### Value Chain Analysis: Where Pricing Power Resides

The LNG value chain comprises four stages: **gas procurement → liquefaction → shipping → regasification/delivery**. Cheniere's competitive position is strongest at the liquefaction stage, where barriers to entry are highest (permitting timelines of 3-5+ years, $10B+ capital requirements per project, EPC execution risk) and where the toll-road contract model concentrates margin.

Critically, the Hormuz crisis revealed that the **logistics and insurance layer** of the value chain has become a structural determinant of LNG pricing. War risk premiums, transit tolls, rerouting around Africa (adding 10-14 days to Asia delivery), and vessel availability now represent a permanent "logistics surcharge" that advantages Cheniere's Gulf Coast facilities. Cheniere's direct Atlantic routing to Europe and Panama Canal access to Asia represent a geographic advantage that cannot be replicated by Middle Eastern producers, regardless of their cost structure.

The **insurance mechanism** deserves special emphasis. In the 1980s Tanker War, insurance remained available at elevated but manageable premiums and shipping continued despite attacks. In 2026, the financialization of maritime risk meant insurers autonomously killed commercial Hormuz transit before any navy fired a shot. This structural change means that even partial geopolitical tension — not just outright conflict — can disrupt LNG supply chains through insurance repricing alone.

### The Demand Picture

European demand is the swing variable. The IEA projects OECD European gas demand declining up to 10% by 2030, with gas-to-power demand falling 25% as renewables surge. IEEFA forecasts European LNG imports declining 20% to 134 bcm by 2030 from a peak of ~170 bcm. But Wood Mackenzie projects traded gas prices almost halving by 2030 to ~€24/MWh, which could paradoxically slow demand decline by making gas competitive for industrial use. The critical variable is the pace of renewables deployment versus the phase-out of remaining Russian gas and natural decline of Algerian/Norwegian pipeline flows post-2030.

Asian demand provides the structural growth driver. Japan and South Korea (combined ~100+ mtpa of LNG imports) have only ~3 weeks of strategic reserves and rely on LNG for baseload power. Southeast Asia (Vietnam, Thailand, Philippines, Bangladesh) is adding regas capacity and LNG import terminals. India's LNG imports are growing. China remains the world's largest incremental LNG buyer. The Hormuz crisis has reinforced the strategic imperative for Asian buyers to diversify toward Atlantic Basin supply — a secular tailwind for Cheniere that operates independently of the conflict's resolution.

- **Zero Hormuz transit exposure**: the only major LNG supplier with 53+ mtpa of capacity entirely outside any conflict zone, providing structural supply security that commands a premium in post-crisis contracting
- **Ras Laffan damage absorbs the projected global oversupply**: 12.8 mtpa offline for years, with replacement gas turbines facing 2-4 year lead times — even under optimistic repair scenarios, the supply gap persists through 2028-2029

## Bear Case

- **Ceasefire holds and Ras Laffan repairs faster than expected**: if Qatar restores full capacity within 2 years rather than 3-5, the supply gap narrows and spot premiums compress, potentially removing $500M-1B of incremental EBITDA from 2027-2028
- **European LNG demand is structurally declining**: IEA/IEEFA project 10-20% declines by 2030 as renewables surge — Cheniere's largest market is shrinking, and the crisis has merely borrowed forward demand through panic restocking
- **Competitor capacity wave**: Venture Global (60+ mtpa pipeline), Sempra (26 mtpa), NextDecade (30 mtpa), Woodside (16.5 mtpa) — US LNG export capacity could roughly double by 2030, creating genuine oversupply regardless of Ras Laffan

- **Limited near-term spot upside**: management's stated sensitivity of <$50M EBITDA per $1/MMBtu change on <1 mtpa of open capacity means the crisis pricing environment benefits Cheniere's narrative more than its near-term income statement
