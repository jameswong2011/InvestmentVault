# UBER — Uber Technologies

## Summary

Capital-light platform monopoly disguised as a rideshare company. At ~$72 (~$150B market cap), down ~30% from October 2025 highs despite record results: $10B annual FCF, 20%+ gross bookings growth, expanding margins in every segment. At 21x forward earnings and PEG below 1.0, the market prices a mature utility rather than an accelerating multi-sided platform with compounding network effects across mobility, delivery, advertising, and AV distribution. Uber One (46M members), $2B+ advertising business, and asset-light AV strategy (25+ partnerships across 10+ live cities, $1.25B Rivian deal for 50,000 exclusive robotaxis) create structural advantages not reflected in the price. Key debate: whether Uber's AV distribution strategy entrenches dominance or vertically integrated players (Waymo, Tesla) bypass the platform.

## Key Non-consensus Insights

- **Uber is completing a business model transition from operator to platform tax collector, and the market is still using the old mental model.** $2B+ advertising business (growing 50%+ YoY) is near-pure margin that raises take rates without increasing consumer prices -- the same dynamic that transformed Amazon's e-commerce margins after 2019. Uber for Business (200,000+ corporate accounts, bookings up 50% YoY), Uber Health, grocery/retail delivery, and Blacklane extend the platform into adjacent verticals without incremental driver supply.

- **The AV strategy is the most misunderstood aspect of the investment case — Uber is building the "Android of autonomy," not competing with Waymo.** Waymo robotaxis on Uber's network in Austin/Atlanta generate 30% more trips per vehicle per day and 25% shorter wait times than on Waymo's own platform -- the most powerful evidence that demand aggregation creates value even for the most advanced AV operator. Rivian deal ($1.25B for 50,000 exclusive R2 robotaxis) and WeRide's Dubai commercial launch validate the model: multiple AV providers choose Uber's platform because demand density, operations, insurance, and regulatory compliance are prohibitively expensive to replicate. The AI data-tagging business (NVIDIA partnership) generating "hundreds of millions" is a hidden asset monetising AV infrastructure regardless of robotaxi timelines.

- **Uber One's 46 million members represent behavioural lock-in that is structurally different from — and more durable than — typical subscription loyalty programs.** Members spend 4x non-members and drive 60% of Delivery bookings. March 2026 removal of the Eats service fee discount for new members is the first meaningful pricing power test within the subscription cohort. If retention holds through Q2/Q3, it validates genuine behavioural stickiness rather than price-driven loyalty.

- **The "cultural turnaround" under Khosrowshahi is complete, and Uber's management discipline is now a competitive advantage that the market underweights because it remembers 2017-2019.** Freight achieved breakeven EBITDA for the first time in three years via cost discipline, not revenue growth. Getir Turkiye and Blacklane demonstrate small, targeted M&A vs prior billion-dollar empire-building. 84% of developers are agentic coding users with 65-72% of code AI-generated. Counter-signal: CFO Balaji Krishnamurthy is the third CFO in three years (February 2026).

- **Investor bias creates a persistent discount: the market cannot reconcile "former cash-burning unicorn" with "$10B annual FCF compounder."** The ~30% selloff from October 2025 highs -- despite record Q4 results -- reflects anchoring to the Kalanick-era mental model. 47 Buy-rated analysts with $105-107 average targets recognize the transformation, but the buy side has not fully re-underwritten the business.

## Outstanding Questions

- **How real is the Waymo disintermediation threat, and at what scale does it become material?** Waymo completes 200,000+ paid rides/week and has expanded to Dallas, D.C., and Miami via its own app (not Uber). At what fleet size and city count does Waymo's own demand generation make Uber's platform negligible? Alphabet's balance sheet means it can scale aggressively if unit economics work.

- **Is the 20-25% AV take rate sustainable, or will margin compress as AV partners gain leverage?** These economics are not publicly disclosed. If Waymo, Aurora, or Motional achieve scale for independent demand generation, they could renegotiate downward -- the same dynamic that occurred when large Amazon marketplace sellers gained commission leverage.

- **What happens to Uber One retention when benefit reductions compound?** March 2026 removed the Eats service fee discount for new members; variable minimum baskets also introduced. No churn rate has been publicly disclosed. The Gridwise survey (60.4% reducing usage due to higher prices) suggests the price-sensitive segment is large. If the direction is benefit extraction rather than benefit addition (the Amazon Prime model), churn could accelerate.

- **Are the insurance reserves ($2.7B growing to $3.3B) an iceberg risk?** Actuarial assumptions are not publicly disclosed. AV rides create novel liability frameworks, and the $8.5M bellwether sexual assault verdict (against $140M sought) with 3,000+ consolidated lawsuits remaining signals potential adverse development. Reserve inadequacy would not appear in headline EBITDA.

- **Can Uber Eats sustainably operate as #2 against DoorDash's 56-67% US share?** DoorDash's Deliveroo acquisition (£2.8B) creates an even more formidable global competitor with 45 markets. Uber Eats' 40% EBITDA growth in Q4 2025 is encouraging but off a low base -- is margin expansion from genuine operating leverage or from shifting costs to restaurants and consumers?

- **How does the worker classification endgame play out across jurisdictions?** EU Platform Workers Directive creates a rebuttable presumption of employment (member state implementation deadline December 2026). California AB 1340 grants union rights to ~800,000 gig workers. Reclassification in any major jurisdiction would mean 20-30% cost increases. Prop 22 was upheld in California, but portability to other markets is unproven.

- **Is $1.8B in annual SBC a sustainable drag, or will it compress as the company matures?** SBC represents ~18% of operating cash flow. Combined with equity investment mark-to-market volatility, GAAP earnings quality remains poor relative to adjusted metrics. Cedar Street Research argues "true organic FCF" is ~$4.1B vs reported ~$9.8B after adjusting for SBC and insurance float.

- **Does agentic AI bypass the Uber app entirely?** Khosrowshahi himself identified this risk (Semafor, March 2026): users telling AI agents "Get me a car" creates the same disintermediation vector threatening Google Search. Uber's counter-strategy is Uber One behavioral lock-in plus direct ChatGPT integration, but if 2B+ ChatGPT users comparison-shop across rideshare platforms via agentic interfaces, Uber's app-level moat erodes.

## Business Model & Product Description

### The Platform Architecture

Uber's business model is best understood by analogy to Amazon's marketplace architecture. Just as Amazon evolved from an online bookseller into a multi-sided platform (marketplace + AWS + advertising + Prime), Uber has evolved from a rideshare app into a multi-modal mobility and delivery platform with four distinct but mutually reinforcing revenue engines.

The platform sits at the intersection of three types of supply (drivers, restaurants/merchants, and autonomous vehicles) and four types of demand (riders, eaters, businesses, and advertisers). This multi-sided architecture creates compounding network effects: more riders attract more drivers, which reduces wait times, which attracts more riders — and the same dynamic plays out across delivery, where more restaurants attract more consumers, which attracts more restaurants. Uber One bundles these two-sided marketplaces into a single subscription, creating cross-modal network effects that neither Lyft (rides only) nor DoorDash (delivery only) can replicate.

### Revenue Segments

**Mobility (~55-60% of revenue):** The core rideshare business operates in 15,000+ cities globally. Revenue comes from a take rate (the percentage of gross bookings retained by Uber) that has expanded to approximately 28-29% as pricing algorithms optimise for yield. Key product tiers include UberX (economy), Uber Black (+35% growth), Uber Lux (+63% growth), and Uber Comfort. The premium tier growth is significant because it represents pricing power in action — riders willingly pay more for differentiated service, expanding margin without competitive pressure. Uber also operates Uber Reserve (scheduled rides), Uber Shuttle (shared rides on fixed routes), and Uber for Business (200,000+ corporate accounts generating bookings up 50% YoY). International operations span the Middle East (via Careem, $3.1B acquisition), Latin America, Europe, India (~45% share vs Ola's ~50%), and Australia/NZ. Uber exited China (retaining ~15.4% Didi stake) and Southeast Asia (selling to Grab in 2018).

**Delivery (~35-40% of revenue):** Uber Eats is the #2 US food delivery platform with 23-25% market share. The business extends beyond food into grocery delivery (partnership with Instacart, direct merchant integrations), retail delivery, and quick-commerce (via the Getir Türkiye acquisition). Delivery EBITDA grew 40% in Q4 2025 — faster than Mobility's 25% — demonstrating the segment's margin expansion trajectory as it scales. Approximately 65-70% of Uber's 15,000+ cities offer both Rides and Eats, and cross-selling is central to the strategy: users engaging with both products generate over 3x the gross bookings of single-product users, with roughly 40% of customers now using multiple products. The Delivery segment's take rate is lower than Mobility's but is expanding as Uber shifts to a marketplace model (connecting merchants directly with consumers) rather than the older logistics-heavy model.

**Freight (~5% of revenue):** Uber Freight is a digital freight brokerage connecting shippers with carriers. The business achieved breakeven EBITDA for the first time in over three years in Q4 2025, but its flat revenue trajectory and disconnection from the consumer platform have prompted analysts to discuss a potential divestiture or spin-off. Freight is strategically non-core but demonstrates management's willingness to pursue discipline over growth at any cost.

**Advertising ($2B+ annual run rate, growing 50%+ YoY):** The advertising business is arguably the most underappreciated segment because it is not reported as a standalone revenue line — it is embedded within Mobility and Delivery economics. Uber's advertising platform allows restaurants, merchants, CPG brands, and retailers to promote products within the Uber and Uber Eats apps. This is an almost pure-margin contribution that effectively raises blended take rates without increasing consumer-facing prices — precisely the playbook Amazon used to transform its e-commerce margin structure. At $2B+ and growing 50%+, this business alone would be valued at $20-30B as a standalone ad-tech company.

### The Uber One Flywheel

Uber One is a $9.99/month (or $99.99/year) subscription that bundles benefits across Mobility and Delivery: 5% savings on rides, free delivery on eligible orders, priority access to top-rated drivers, and exclusive promotions. With 46 million members (as of Q4 2025), Uber One is the cross-modal subscription backbone of the platform.

The economics are powerful: members spend 4x what non-members do and account for 60% of Delivery bookings. The subscription acts as a behavioural lock-in mechanism — users who pay monthly develop habits around Uber for both rides and meals, increasing switching costs. CEO Khosrowshahi has acknowledged that new subscribers are initially loss-making but create "significant moat" over their lifetime. The March 2026 removal of the Eats service fee discount for new members is the first meaningful test of pricing power within this cohort.

### Autonomous Vehicle Strategy

Uber's AV strategy is deliberately asset-light: rather than spending billions to develop proprietary autonomous driving technology (as Waymo, Tesla, and Cruise have done), Uber positions itself as the distribution, operations, and demand-generation layer that any AV manufacturer needs to scale. The "Uber Autonomous Solutions" division (launched February 2026) formalises this, providing: AV training data (3M+ hours via "AV Labs" and a Data Factory partnership with NVIDIA), fleet operations, "AV Mission Control" remote-assistance software, in-vehicle UI, customer service, insurance, roadside assistance, regulatory services, and fleet financing. The AV data-tagging business from this infrastructure is already generating "hundreds of millions" in revenue with potential to become "another billion-dollar business" (Khosrowshahi, Semafor interview March 2026).

**Major AV Partnerships (as of April 2026):**
- **Waymo:** Live in Phoenix, San Francisco, Los Angeles, Austin, Atlanta. Relationship showing signs of cooling — Waymo launched Dallas with Avis (not Uber), expanded to D.C. and Miami via own app. However, Uber data shows Waymo robotaxis on Uber's network generate 30% more trips/vehicle/day and 25% shorter wait times vs. Waymo-only platforms
- **Rivian (March 2026):** $1.25B deal for up to 50,000 R2 robotaxis (10K firm + 40K option). $300M initial investment. Deployments begin San Francisco and Miami in 2028, expanding to 25 cities by 2031. R2 vehicles exclusive to Uber's platform
- **WeRide (February 2026):** 1,200+ robotaxis committed across Dubai, Abu Dhabi, and Riyadh. Launched fully driverless commercial operations in Dubai on March 31, 2026 — first Level 4 robotaxis in commercial service in the city
- **VW/MOIA (April 2026):** Self-driving ID. Buzz vehicles began testing on LA roads. 100+ vehicles in test phase; launches on Uber with safety operators by end 2026, fully driverless in 2027. Long-term: fleet of thousands across multiple US markets
- **Nuro/Lucid (April 2026):** Premium robotaxi testing in San Francisco with Nuro-equipped Lucid Gravity vehicles. 20,000 vehicle commitment over six years ($300M Uber investment in Lucid)
- **Aurora, Motional (Hyundai/Aptiv), Serve Robotics, May Mobility, Avride (ex-Yandex), Waabi**, and others

**City count:** 10+ live cities with AV operations, targeting 15 by end of 2026. International expansion includes Dubai, Abu Dhabi, Riyadh (live), with planned launches in London, Zurich, Madrid, Munich, and Hong Kong.

Uber has publicly suggested an **80/20 revenue split** (Uber takes ~20%, AV partner keeps ~80%). Analyst estimates for Waymo specifically range 10-20%. At Uber's current Mobility take rate of ~29.9%, AV rides are dilutive to blended take rate but potentially accretive on absolute volume given zero driver cost. Khosrowshahi envisions a three-tier fleet ownership model: (1) large institutional investors (Blackstone-type), (2) small entrepreneurs with 20-vehicle garages, (3) individual "super hosts" (Airbnb analogy).

## Industry Context

### US Rideshare: A Structurally Stable Duopoly

The US rideshare market is a textbook stable duopoly: Uber holds 74-76% market share versus Lyft's 24-26%, a ratio that has barely moved since 2019. Neither company is engaged in destructive price wars, and competitive intensity is structurally low. This stability is notable because it contradicts the bear thesis that rideshare is a commodity — in reality, the network effect of driver supply creates a natural monopoly tendency (more drivers → shorter wait times → more riders → more drivers) that is extremely difficult for new entrants to challenge.

Lyft's most significant strategic shift is its April 2025 acquisition of FREENOW (BMW/Mercedes) for €175 million, providing access to 9 European countries and ~€1 billion in annual gross bookings — its first meaningful international expansion. This move actually reinforces the duopoly thesis: Lyft is pursuing geographic expansion rather than domestic price competition, which is the rational response to Uber's dominant position.

Take rates reveal the competitive dynamics:
- Uber: ~28-29% take rate, expanding through advertising and operational efficiency
- Lyft: ~27% take rate, improving but structurally lower due to smaller scale
- DoorDash (delivery): ~13-14% marketplace take rate + commissions

### US Food Delivery: DoorDash Dominant, Uber a Strong #2

In US food delivery, DoorDash dominates with 56-67% market share versus Uber Eats at 23-25%. Grubhub was sold by Just Eat Takeaway to Wonder Group for just $650 million — a staggering 90% discount from its $7.3 billion purchase price — underscoring the market's harsh consolidation. DoorDash's ~$3.9B acquisition of Deliveroo (closed late 2025) is integrating "ahead of initial expectations" with $200M EBITDA contribution forecast. DoorDash now operates in 45 markets worldwide with 30 European markets (Wolt + Deliveroo combined), building a unified global tech stack and autonomous delivery capabilities.

However, Uber's delivery position is qualitatively different from a pure-play delivery competitor's because of the cross-modal Uber One subscription. A significant portion of Uber Eats orders come from users who are also Uber riders — this cross-selling reduces customer acquisition costs to near-zero for the delivery segment and creates retention dynamics that DoorDash cannot replicate without a rideshare business.

### International Competitive Landscape

- **China (exited):** Uber sold its China operations to Didi in 2016, retaining ~15.4% equity stake. Didi dominates with ~80% market share. Uber has no path back into China.
- **Southeast Asia (exited):** Sold to Grab in 2018. Grab is the regional super-app dominant in 8 countries.
- **Middle East:** Operates via Careem ($3.1B acquisition), the dominant platform in the region.
- **India:** Locked in tight competition with Ola (~50% vs Uber's ~45%). India is the most contested major market.
- **Europe:** Uber is the leading pan-European rideshare platform but faces competition from Bolt (600+ cities, EUR 1.99B revenue in 2024, planning IPO potentially 2026, partnered with Stellantis for 100K L4 AVs by 2035) and local players. Lyft's FREENOW acquisition (EUR 175M, closed July 2025) marks its first European entry with 9 countries and ~EUR 1B gross bookings. The EU Platform Workers Directive creates regulatory risk.
- **Latin America:** Uber is dominant in most major markets (Brazil, Mexico, Argentina), with local competition from 99 (Didi-owned) in Brazil and Cabify in Spain/LatAm.
- **Australia/NZ:** Uber is dominant with ~70% share; Ola was the main competitor but has retreated.

### Autonomous Vehicle Competitive Landscape

The AV landscape is the most consequential competitive dynamic for Uber's long-term positioning:

- **Waymo (Alphabet):** The most advanced commercial AV operator, completing 200,000+ paid rides per week. Valued at ~$126B. Expanding to 20+ cities in 2026, including first international market (London) and testing in Tokyo. Has its own consumer app and is expanding independently into new cities (Dallas with Avis, D.C., Miami, Orlando — all without Uber). Waymo represents the primary disintermediation threat because it has both the technology and Alphabet's balance sheet to build its own demand platform. However, Uber data shows Waymo vehicles generate 30% more trips/vehicle/day on the Uber network than on Waymo's own platform — this is the core of Uber's value proposition. The relationship is "not black or white" (Khosrowshahi) — Waymo uses both direct channels and Uber partnerships.
- **Tesla:** Operating an unsupervised robotaxi fleet of ~10 Model Y vehicles in Austin as of April 2026 — no human safety monitors inside the cabin, with remote assistance operators monitoring rides. The geofence has expanded well beyond the original June 2025 boundaries into complex downtown areas. FSD v14.3 delivered a 20% faster reaction time. Tesla's Cybercab (purpose-built robotaxi) is expected to begin production in April 2026. Tesla's camera-only approach carries higher technical risk than Waymo's LiDAR-based system, and the fleet remains tiny (~31 vehicles total), but the installed base of millions of Tesla vehicles represents optionality at a scale no other AV player can match. Has not applied for California deployment permits.
- **Aurora:** Acquired Uber's self-driving unit (ATG) in 2020. Focused on autonomous trucking first, with plans for passenger rides via Uber's platform. Aurora represents the best-case AV partnership model for Uber.
- **Motional (Hyundai/Aptiv JV):** Operating AV taxi pilots in Las Vegas with Uber and Lyft, plus autonomous food delivery in LA via Uber Eats. A key Uber partnership.
- **Cruise (GM):** Had its permits suspended in California after safety incidents. Operations paused indefinitely. GM recently announced restructuring of Cruise, reducing the near-term competitive threat.

### Regulatory Landscape

Regulation is fragmenting globally, creating both risk and opportunity:

- **United States:** No federal AV deployment law; state-by-state patchwork. California has the strictest framework (separate testing and deployment permits); Arizona and Texas are permissive. NHTSA is updating safety standards but has not mandated regulations. California Supreme Court upheld Proposition 22 unanimously, preserving independent contractor status — but California AB 1340 (effective January 2026) grants union rights to ~800,000 rideshare/delivery drivers, a new complication. FTC plus 21 states filed amended complaint (December 2025) on Uber One billing practices (enrolment without consent, cancellation requiring 23 screens). The California AG launched a surveillance pricing probe in January 2026 under CCPA and AB 325 (algorithmic price-fixing). Chicago expanded rideshare congestion surcharges ($1.50/ride), Washington State is considering surge price caps.
- **European Union:** The EU Platform Workers Directive (adopted 2024) creates a rebuttable presumption that platform workers are employees — the most significant regulatory risk to Uber's business model outside the US. The EU has also adopted unified Level 4 AV technical requirements, creating a standardised path for robotaxi deployment.
- **United Kingdom:** The Automated Vehicles Act 2024 creates a comprehensive legal framework for AV deployment by 2026, with liability shifting from the occupant to the manufacturer/software provider when in autonomous mode.
- **China:** Rapidly advancing government-backed AV deployments. Baidu's Apollo Go operates robotaxis in multiple cities. Uber has no exposure (exited to Didi).
- **India:** Limited AV regulatory framework. Worker classification is not currently a major risk given India's labour law structure.

### Pricing Power and Consumer Elasticity

Academic research consistently shows Uber demand is relatively inelastic, with price elasticity estimates of -0.5 to -0.6 in developed markets (Cohen et al., NBER) — meaning a 10% price increase reduces trip volume by only 5-6%. However, consumer surveys reveal a more nuanced picture: 60.4% of riders in a January 2026 Gridwise survey said they had reduced rideshare usage due to higher prices, up 16.6 percentage points from December 2024. Yet Uber's actual trips grew 22% in Q4 2025 and MAPCs grew 18% — the paradox is explained by Uber capturing new suburban markets, premium ride types (Black +35%, Lux +63%), and new demographics faster than it loses price-sensitive existing users.

The premium tier growth is the most significant pricing power signal: consumers are voluntarily selecting higher-priced ride options at an accelerating rate, indicating willingness-to-pay that extends well beyond base UberX pricing. Combined with the $2B+ advertising business (which raises effective take rates without increasing consumer prices), Uber's monetisation engine is expanding along multiple dimensions simultaneously.

Regulatory scrutiny on pricing is intensifying: the FTC has labelled Uber's personalised pricing as "surveillance pricing," the House Oversight Committee demanded algorithm documentation by March 2026, the California AG launched an individualised pricing probe in January 2026, Chicago expanded its rideshare congestion surcharge ($1.50/ride in new zones), and Washington State is considering surge price caps after events. The question is whether these constraints will bite before Uber's premium mix shift and advertising revenue render surge pricing less important to the margin profile.

### Value Chain Analysis

Uber sits at the orchestration layer of the mobility value chain — between supply (drivers, vehicles, restaurants) and demand (consumers, businesses). Its value-add is:
1. **Demand aggregation:** Millions of daily ride/delivery requests, concentrated in a single app
2. **Dynamic pricing and matching:** Real-time algorithms that optimise driver-rider matching, pricing, and routing
3. **Trust and safety infrastructure:** Ratings, background checks, insurance, dispute resolution
4. **Regulatory compliance:** Licences, permits, and government relationships across 15,000+ cities
5. **Financial services:** Payment processing, driver advances, and tipping infrastructure

This orchestration position becomes more valuable — not less — in an AV future, because AV manufacturers need the demand density, operational infrastructure, and regulatory relationships that Uber provides. The risk is that a single AV player (Waymo) becomes dominant enough to build its own demand generation, bypassing Uber's platform.

## Bull Case

- **Platform monopoly at utility pricing:** PEG below 1.0 on $10B annual FCF — the market prices Uber as a mature utility despite 35% EBITDA growth and 20%+ bookings growth, an anomaly for a dominant platform compounder
- **AV distribution platform wins regardless of technology winner:** 25+ partnerships across 7 cities mean Uber benefits from AV adoption without bearing development risk; the "Android of autonomy" strategy favours the platform over any single vertically integrated player
- **Uber One creates irreplicable multi-modal lock-in:** 46M members with 4x spend uplift and 60% of Delivery bookings create behavioural stickiness that no single-modal competitor (Lyft or DoorDash) can replicate
- **Advertising is a hidden margin engine:** $2B+ run rate growing 50%+ YoY is near-pure margin that raises blended take rates without increasing consumer prices — the same dynamic that transformed Amazon's e-commerce profitability
- **Rideshare duopoly is structurally stable:** 74-76% market share has not moved since 2019 — competitive intensity is low and pricing power is demonstrated
- **Delivery margin expansion accelerating:** Delivery EBITDA growing 40% (vs Mobility's 25%) off improving unit economics and scale leverage
- **Premium mix shifting up:** Uber Black +35%, Lux +63% — consumers willingly paying more for differentiated service signals pricing power beyond algorithmic optimisation

## Bear Case

- **Waymo disintermediation is a when-not-if risk:** Waymo's independent expansion (Dallas, D.C., Miami without Uber) signals decreasing dependence on Uber's demand platform — at sufficient fleet scale, Waymo's own app may generate enough demand to bypass Uber
- **Worker classification is existential in tail scenarios:** EU Platform Workers Directive, FTC surveillance pricing probe, California AG investigation, and congressional scrutiny could force reclassification in major markets, increasing costs 20-30%
- **Insurance reserves are an opaque risk:** Growth from $2.7B to $3.3B with undisclosed actuarial assumptions creates potential for adverse reserve development, particularly as AV rides create novel liability frameworks
- **GAAP earnings quality remains poor:** Equity investment volatility (Didi, Aurora) and $1.8B annual SBC distort real profitability — free cash flow yield is lower than adjusted metrics suggest
- **DoorDash + Deliveroo creates a global delivery giant:** DoorDash's 56-67% US share plus Deliveroo's UK/European footprint creates a more formidable competitor than Uber Eats can match in delivery-only contexts
- **Uber One benefit reductions risk churn:** Removing the Eats service fee discount while introducing variable minimum baskets signals willingness to extract margin from the most loyal cohort — if retention breaks, the flywheel decelerates
- **Tesla robotaxi wildcard:** Tesla's unsupervised Austin fleet (~10 Model Ys, expanding geofence, Cybercab production starting April 2026) represents a faster-than-expected progression. If Tesla's camera-only approach works at scale and its fleet of millions of consumer vehicles becomes ride-hailable, it would represent a category disruption that Uber's partnership model cannot address — Tesla would be the first AV player with both the technology and the fleet to bypass Uber entirely
