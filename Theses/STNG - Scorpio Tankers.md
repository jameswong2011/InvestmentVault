# STNG — Scorpio Tankers

## Summary

World's largest product tanker owner (90 vessels). Chokepoint infrastructure play: Hormuz closure since February 28, 2026 blocked ~14% of global refined product trade with no pipeline bypass (unlike crude), sending LR2 rates from $36,767 to $120,000/day — ton-mile demand shock 3x larger than 2022-23 Russian rerouting. Q1 2026 TCE: LR2 $51,000/day, MR $32,000/day. Annualized EPS $8-12+ at current rates. Stock repriced ~$30s to ~$77 but trades at 7-10x crisis-era earnings with fortress balance sheet (net cash $334M, $11,000/day breakeven). Key remaining asymmetry is duration: April 8 ceasefire collapsed April 12, US counter-blockade in effect, ceasefire expires April 21, and even full reopening requires until July for normalization (600-800+ stranded vessels, mine-clearing, insurance repricing). Conviction medium — core scenario playing out, but further upside depends on crisis persistence and headline risk from diplomatic resolution is real.

## Business Model & Product Description

**What STNG Does — and Why It's Misunderstood**

Scorpio Tankers is best understood as a floating infrastructure company, not a commodity producer. The company earns revenue by renting out physical assets — 90 product tankers — by the day, expressed as Time Charter Equivalent (TCE) rates. Unlike an oil producer whose revenue scales with commodity price, STNG's revenue scales with the scarcity of the infrastructure needed to move refined products from surplus to deficit regions. When shipping routes get longer (rerouting around the Cape of Good Hope instead of through Hormuz), or when ships get stranded or trapped, the same number of barrels requires more ship-days to transport. This is the "ton-mile" concept — it's why a chokepoint closure can triple tanker rates even if underlying oil demand is flat or declining.

The closest analogy is a toll road operator during a highway shutdown. STNG's ships are the alternative routes, and the daily TCE rate is the toll. When the main highway (Hormuz) closes, traffic reroutes to the back roads (Cape of Good Hope), and the toll skyrockets because the infrastructure is suddenly scarce relative to demand. The key insight investors miss is that STNG does not need oil demand to grow — it needs oil to move, and it needs the routes to be long.

**Fleet Composition**

STNG's 90 owned vessels break down as:

- **34 LR2** (80,000-120,000 DWT): Long-haul refined product routes (Middle East→Europe, Middle East→Asia). These are the highest day-rate earners ($51,000/day in Q1 2026) and the most sensitive to Hormuz disruption — approximately 50% of global LR2 ton-mile demand originates west of Hormuz. LR2s are the backbone of the thesis.
- **42 MR** (40,000-55,000 DWT): The workhorse class of global product tanker trade. Regional and inter-regional routes with the most liquid charter market. Moderate rates ($32,000/day in Q1 2026) but the highest volume of voyages.
- **14 Handymax** (25,000-40,000 DWT): Smaller vessels for short-haul, coastal, and specialty cargo routes. Rates ($34,000/day Q1 2026) supported by niche demand and lower fleet supply.

All vessels are ECO-specification, consuming 20-25% less fuel than conventional designs. Approximately 80% are scrubber-fitted, allowing them to burn cheaper high-sulfur fuel oil (HSFO) while meeting IMO 2020 emissions standards — saving $3,000-$5,000/day per vessel depending on the HSFO-LSFO spread. The average fleet age is 10.1 years versus an industry average of ~12-13 years, with 17% of the global product tanker fleet over 20 years old.

STNG has actively renewed its fleet since mid-2025, selling ~10 older vessels for $400M+ in proceeds while contracting 10 newbuilds: 4 MR (delivery 2026-2027), 4 LR2 (delivery 2027-2029), and 2 VLCCs from Hanwha Ocean at ~$128M each (delivery H2 2028). The VLCC orders represent STNG's first foray back into crude tankers since exiting in 2014.

**Revenue Model: Maximum Spot Leverage**

STNG operates ~80% of its fleet on the spot market or in commercial pools, with ~20% on time charters. This high spot exposure captures the full upside of rate spikes but also the full downside during troughs. The commercial pool model (Scorpio Commercial Pools, or SCP) manages ~150 vessels for ~20 third-party owners in addition to STNG's own fleet. Pool earnings are distributed based on vessel class and speed, creating a diversified revenue stream with real-time geographic flexibility — pools can redirect vessels toward the highest-paying routes as conditions change.

Every $1,000/day change in fleet-wide TCE translates to ~$31.6M in annualised revenue and ~$0.55-0.61/share in EPS. This sensitivity is the critical input for modelling STNG's earnings across rate scenarios:

**Balance Sheet: From Leveraged Cyclical to Fortress Cash**

STNG has undergone the most dramatic balance sheet transformation in the product tanker sector. Net debt collapsed from $3.1B in 2021 to net cash of ~$334M as of early 2026. Total debt stands at ~$589M against $974M in cash and a $747M undrawn revolving credit facility, providing ~$1.7B in total liquidity. All bank amortisation has been prepaid through 2027, with no debt maturities until 2028. Cash breakeven has fallen to ~$11,000/day — the lowest in company history and among the lowest in the product tanker sector.

In April 2026, STNG raised $375M in 1.75% convertible senior notes due 2031 (conversion price ~$100.39/share, 35% premium), using ~$100M immediately for share repurchases (1.34M shares at $74.36). Cumulative buybacks now exceed $860M. Combined with a $0.45/quarter dividend (~2.4% yield), total shareholder yield is approximately 8.6%.

**Strategic Signals**

Two recent moves reveal management's forward thinking:
1. **VLCC re-entry** — The two Hanwha Ocean VLCC orders signal management sees value in crude tanker exposure at a point in the cycle where product tanker franchise values are high. This hedges against product tanker cycle normalisation while accessing a market with its own geopolitical tailwinds.
2. **Micronuclear partnership** — A $10M investment in AMPERA Inc. for micronuclear maritime propulsion (thorium fuel, subcritical solid-state reactor) positions STNG at the frontier of post-carbon shipping. Financially immaterial today, but strategically notable as a long-duration optionality play.

## Industry Context

**The Global Product Tanker Fleet and the Bifurcation**

The global product tanker fleet comprises approximately 3,800 vessels. The fleet is aging — 17% of vessels are over 20 years old, expected to reach 30% by 2028 — but scrapping was suppressed to 30-year lows through 2024 as older vessels were absorbed into the "shadow fleet" servicing sanctioned trades. In 2025, ship recycling increased 65% YoY (7.9M gt vs 4.7M gt) as regulatory crackdowns pushed substandard tonnage toward scrapyards.

The fleet is functionally split into two tiers: the **compliant pool** (~85-90%) comprising Western-flagged, Western-insured vessels that trade transparently, and the **shadow pool** (~10-15%) comprising older, often poorly maintained tonnage operating with opaque ownership, non-Western P&I insurance, and AIS manipulation. This bifurcation, which emerged from Russian sanctions in 2022, has been dramatically reinforced by the Hormuz crisis — Western insurers withdrew coverage for the entire Persian Gulf, effectively barring compliant vessels from transit regardless of military activity.

The orderbook has expanded to ~19% of the existing fleet, with 146 MR and 88 LR2 vessels scheduled for delivery in 2026 alone — a 52% increase over 2025. This is the most aggressive supply growth in years and represents the primary bear case for rates post-normalisation. However, the orderbook is partially offset by scrapping of the oldest cohort and by the continued absorption of vintage vessels into unsanctioned shadow fleet operations that removes them from the compliant market.

**Chokepoint Mechanics: Why Product Tankers Are Different**

The Strait of Hormuz normally handles ~20M bbl/day of petroleum liquids (25-27% of global seaborne oil trade) and ~19% of global LNG trade. Of this, **~5.5-6.0M bbl/day is refined products — approximately 14% of all global refined product trade.** This is the critical asymmetry versus crude: pipelines exist to bypass Hormuz for crude (Saudi East-West Pipeline ~7M bbl/day to Yanbu, UAE ADCOP ~1.5-1.8M bbl/day to Fujairah, Iraq Kirkuk-Ceyhan ~200K bbl/day operational). Combined bypass capacity is ~9-10M bbl/day, covering less than half of crude flows. **But these pipelines carry crude only — no pipeline bypass exists for refined products.** Every barrel of Middle East refined product from SATORP, Yasref, Jazan (Saudi), Ruwais (UAE), Al-Zour (Kuwait), and Qatari condensate splitters must exit by ship through Hormuz.

Since February 28, 2026, Hormuz transits have collapsed ~92% from ~138 vessels/day to near-zero. The situation has escalated beyond the initial closure to a **dual blockade regime**: Iran's original IRGC closure, plus — following the April 12 peace talk collapse — a US naval counter-blockade targeting vessels servicing Iranian ports. As of April 14, 600-800+ vessels are stranded in the Gulf (325+ tankers per Lloyd's Intelligence), with ~400 loaded tankers waiting to exit. Even full, immediate reopening would require until **July** for flows to normalise, given the vessel queue, mine-clearing operations, and insurance repricing timeline. The ceasefire expires April 21 with Iran's enriched uranium program as the unresolved sticking point.

**Ton-Mile Demand Shock**

The rerouting math is simple but powerful:

| Route | Via Suez | Via Cape | Additional NM | Additional Days |
|-------|---------|---------|---------------|-----------------|
| ME Gulf → NW Europe | ~6,000 nm / ~20 days | ~12,000 nm / ~35-40 days | +6,000 (77% longer) | +14 |
| India → Europe | ~4,500 nm | ~10,000 nm | +5,500 | +12 |
| ME Gulf → East Asia | ~3,500 nm | Blocked — no logical Cape reroute | Must be replaced by USGC (+8,000 nm) | N/A |

ME Gulf→East Asia product flows (~2-3M bbl/day on Western tonnage) are blocked at Hormuz with no logical Cape reroute. These volumes must be replaced by alternative supply from the US Gulf Coast, India, or intra-Asia — all of which are further from East Asian demand centres, adding ton-miles. Replacement from USGC adds 8,000+ nm vs ~3,500 nm from the Gulf — more than doubling ton-miles per barrel.

The tanker supply curve is nearly vertical above ~85% utilisation — small changes in demand create enormous rate moves. The Russian product rerouting of 2022-23 added an estimated 5-8% to ton-mile demand and pushed MR spot rates from ~$15k to $40-50k/day. The Hormuz closure creates a 15-25% ton-mile demand shock — 3x the magnitude.

| Demand Component | Fleet Impact |
|-----------------|-------------|
| Cape rerouting (ME/India → Europe) | +8-10% |
| Displacement (ME → East Asia replaced by longer-haul sources) | +5-10% |
| Shadow fleet constraints pushing cargo back to compliant pool | +2-5% |
| **Total estimated ton-mile demand increase** | **~15-25%** |

Current rate data confirms the framework: LR2 rates spiked from $36,767/day (Feb 20) to $120,000/day (Mar 6), settling ~$83,000/day. LR1 rates hit $224,400/day — a tenfold increase. VLCC rates reached an all-time high of $423,736/day. Q2 2026 LR2 spot rates are being booked at ~$101,000/day with only 16% of days covered.

**Crack Spreads: Confirming the Product Premium**

The crisis has validated the thesis that product tankers capture the tighter barrel:
- NY Harbor distillate crack: $1.42/gal — highest since 2022 (5-year avg $0.68/gal)
- Diesel cracks: $44-50/bbl in key regions
- US diesel retail: peaking >$5.80/gal
- Jet fuel cracks: peaked near $100/bbl
- Singapore gasoil: +57%; jet fuel: +114% vs Brent: +55-60%

A Hafnia-TORM merger would create a 200+ vessel fleet, potentially challenging STNG's position. TORM's "One TORM" platform demonstrates that commercial operational excellence (extracting 3-10% higher TCEs) can meaningfully differentiate peers — a gap STNG has yet to close.

**Historical Precedent: The 1984-1988 Iran-Iraq Tanker War**

During the Iran-Iraq Tanker War, 451 attacks were carried out on merchant vessels and 55 tankers were destroyed, yet the Strait of Hormuz was never formally closed and shipping continued at elevated insurance costs. The current closure is structurally more severe: the insurance mechanism has changed (modern financialisation of war risk means autonomous withdrawal of coverage), the shadow fleet adds market bifurcation complexity that didn't exist in the 1980s, and the physical destruction of infrastructure (Qatar's Ras Laffan, 12.8 mtpa of LNG capacity requiring 2-4 year gas turbine lead times) creates supply gaps that persist regardless of ceasefire outcomes.

## Bull Case

- **Sustained Hormuz disruption through Q3+ 2026** pushes blended fleet TCE to $40-60k+/day, generating $15-30+/share in annualised EPS. At current price, stock trades at 3-5x annualised crisis-era earnings — historically cheap for a net-cash company with the lowest breakeven in the sector
- **Dual blockade escalation**: the April 12 peace talk collapse and US naval counter-blockade create a regime where even partial reopening is complex and slow. Nuclear issue (Iran's enriched uranium) is a deep structural disagreement, not a tactical negotiating point — resolution could take months or years
- **Recovery timeline alone supports rates**: even full, immediate reopening requires until July for flow normalisation given 600-800+ stranded vessels, mine-clearing, and insurance repricing. Q2 and likely Q3 2026 rates are structurally elevated regardless of diplomatic outcomes
- **Product spread widening amplifies tanker demand**: diesel cracks at $44-50/bbl incentivise maximum refinery throughput, generating more refined cargo. Product tankers capture the tighter barrel that crude plays miss
- **Hafnia-TORM consolidation is bullish for sector pricing discipline**: fewer independent operators, more rational chartering, higher structural rates
- **Shadow fleet crackdown tightens compliant pool**: UK/EU sanctions on shadow tonnage, port state inspections, and insurer withdrawal shrink the non-compliant fleet, pushing more cargoes onto STNG's vessels
- **Capital return continues**: $860M+ cumulative buybacks, $375M convertible at $100 conversion, $0.45/quarter dividend. Management has proven willingness and ability to return cash aggressively
- **Fleet renewal positions for next cycle**: 10 newbuilds including VLCCs provide growth optionality; sales of older vessels lock in high S&P values

## Bear Case

- **Rapid diplomatic resolution collapses the thesis overnight**: Pakistan-hosted talks could resume any day; Trump has stated talks "could be happening over the next two days." A full ceasefire with Hormuz reopening would crater rates within weeks, and the stock would retrace to $50-60 range (still above pre-crisis levels on balance sheet strength)
- **Orderbook delivers into weakening demand**: 19% orderbook-to-fleet ratio is the highest in years. If 234 new vessels deliver in 2026 while geopolitical premium fades, utilisation falls below the ~85% threshold where the supply curve goes vertical — and rates collapse
- **Global recession kills demand**: if Brent sustains $100+, demand destruction eventually overwhelms rerouting demand. Every prior major oil supply shock has ended in recession within 12-18 months. The IEA has already cut 2026 oil demand forecasts
- **TCE underperformance persists**: if STNG cannot close the 3-10% rate gap with peers, the P/Book discount is structural and the stock deserves to trade below Hafnia and TORM on a relative basis
- **VLCC entry dilutes the thesis**: $256M committed to crude tankers may prove to be poor capital allocation if the product tanker franchise was the right place to reinvest
- **Dark fleet expands and absorbs disrupted volumes**: if non-compliant tonnage grows faster than expected, it reduces the effective demand shock on STNG's compliant fleet
- **Convertible dilution caps upside**: $375M converts at $100.39 create a ~7% overhang if the stock rallies to those levels
- **Chinese refinery throughput slowdown**: reduces intra-Asia product flows, offsetting rerouting benefits

## Catalysts

- **April 21 ceasefire expiry**: if the ceasefire lapses without extension or deal, the geopolitical premium re-escalates sharply. This is the single most important near-term binary event
- **Q1 2026 earnings (expected May 1)**: at reported TCEs of $32-51k/day, earnings will demonstrate the rate leverage in real time. Guidance on Q2 forward bookings (currently $101k/day LR2 at 16% booked) sets expectations
- **Any military incident in or near Hormuz**: even a single tanker seizure or mine strike reprices the entire forward rate curve
- **Insurance market repricing**: Lloyd's and P&I club decisions on Persian Gulf war risk designations are a leading indicator. Downgrade = rates stay elevated; normalisation = flows resume
- **US executive action on shadow fleet**: sanctions targeting shadow fleet operators or STS transfers would tighten the compliant pool
- **OPEC+ production decisions**: GCC increases are largely trapped behind the blockade — but any signal of post-crisis spare capacity ramp affects medium-term crude/product balances
- **Hafnia-TORM merger announcement**: would catalyse sector-wide re-rating and positioning STNG as either consolidator or premium target
- **Scrapping acceleration**: mass demolition of 20+ year old tonnage would tighten the fleet and provide a structural floor for rates

## Risks

- **Geopolitical de-escalation is the primary risk and is inherently unhedgeable**: the thesis is event-driven, and the event can resolve on a headline. The April 8 ceasefire already demonstrated how quickly sentiment can shift. Position sizing must reflect this binary character
- **Rate normalisation to $15-20k/day pre-orderbook delivery**: at these rates, STNG earns ~$2.50-4.00 EPS and the stock at $77 looks expensive (19-31x earnings)
- **Broader equity risk-off**: a global recession or credit event (private credit default rates at 9.2%, trending toward 12-15%) could compress shipping multiples even if rates are strong
- **OPEC+ spare capacity unlock post-crisis**: if Saudi/UAE ramp production once Hormuz reopens, oversupply in crude could spill over into product tanker demand via lower refinery throughput
- **Fleet age / drydock capex**: STNG has 90 vessels, several of which require periodic drydocking. Scheduling drydocks during elevated rate periods means opportunity cost of ~$3-5M per vessel in foregone revenue
- **Regulatory / ESG**: tightening emissions regulations (CII ratings, EU ETS for shipping) could disadvantage older vessels in the fleet or require costly retrofits
- **China demand risk**: a Chinese economic slowdown reduces intra-Asia product flows, which are the largest single demand driver for MR tankers
