# KAMBI — Kambi Group plc

## Summary

Kambi (~SEK 3.6B / ~€320M market cap) powers 45+ operators across 60+ regulated markets, processing over one billion wagers annually against €17B+ of trading liquidity. The market has over-extrapolated the DraftKings vertical-integration narrative into a thesis that B2B sportsbook is structurally impaired — but the industry is bifurcating: a handful of scaled operators build in-house while tier-2/3 operators worldwide *increase* reliance on specialist providers. Kambi's pivot to a modular product suite (Odds Feed+, Tzeract AI trading, Bet Builder) expands its TAM to operators who have *already built* proprietary sportsbooks but need best-in-class components — FDJ UNITED returned to Kambi for traded odds after departing to build in-house. AI prices nearly 50% of all bets on the network, customer concentration halved over five years (top-3 at 35%), and the 2026 World Cup is a near-term catalyst. The business model transition risk is priced in; the operating leverage from AI automation and modular distribution is not.

## Key Non-consensus Insights

- **The "boomerang" effect disproves the vertical integration death thesis.** FDJ UNITED acquired Kindred (Kambi's parent that left in 2022) for €2.45B, then by November 2025 signed an *expanded* Odds Feed+ deal across Unibet, 32Red Sports, and Parions Sports. B2B sportsbook is evolving from full outsourcing to modular component supply, expanding the TAM.

- **Kambi's AI liquidity flywheel is an underappreciated structural moat.** Tzeract prices ~50% of bets across €17B+ in liquidity from 45+ operators — a dataset no single operator (except Flutter) can replicate. AI-driven efficiency lifted operator trading margins from 10.0% (2024) to 10.8% (2025) while reducing manual trading costs. 2026 World Cup will be the first fully AI-traded major tournament.

- **Customer concentration de-risking was a deliberate strategy, not a symptom of decline.** Top-3 concentration dropped from 60%+ to 35% in five years. 15 new partnerships signed since Q4 2025 — including Hard Rock Digital Odds Feed+ covering 8 US states and OLG (contract through 2032). Pipeline velocity accelerating at the moment the market assumes ex-growth.

- **The Odds Feed+ product is a trojan horse into the in-house operator TAM.** Hard Rock Digital, FDJ UNITED, Superbet Group, Coolbet, and ComeOn Group have all signed — operators with proprietary sportsbooks buying Kambi's traded odds via a single API. Capital-light, higher-margin, priced differently from revenue-share turnkey. Management flagged "advanced discussions with tier-one operators" for 2026.

- **New CEO Werner Becher's Sportradar pedigree signals a distribution-and-data pivot.** Becher ran Sportradar's EMEA/LatAm business and was part of its $7B+ Nasdaq IPO team; appointed July 2024. Under Becher: OMEGA Systems PAM acquisition (unlocking Nevada), Abios esports expansion (Google partnership), accelerated Tzeract AI rollout. Executing Sportradar's modular data-distribution playbook inside Kambi's infrastructure.

## Outstanding Questions

- **How durable is operator trading margin expansion (10.0% → 10.8%) — and does it translate to Kambi pricing power?** If operators capture all the margin benefit while revenue-share rates continue compressing (20-25% a decade ago to 10-12% now), Kambi gives away its best innovation to sustain relationships. Can Odds Feed+ and Tzeract be priced to capture a share of the margin uplift, or does the value accrue entirely to operators?

- **What is the true unit economics profile of Odds Feed+ vs. Turnkey Sportsbook?** Kambi doesn't break out Odds Feed+ contribution. If Odds Feed+ carries higher margins but lower absolute revenue per operator, the mix shift could depress top-line while improving profitability — making the stock look worse on revenue metrics while underlying economics improve.

- **Can Kambi win tier-1 US operators beyond Hard Rock, or is the American market structurally capped?** The US is dominated by vertically integrated operators (Flutter/FanDuel, DraftKings, bet365). The TAM may be limited to mid-tier operators (Rush Street, Bally's, regional casinos). If US plateaus, Kambi becomes reliant on LatAm and smaller European markets with higher regulatory and tax risk.

- **How much of the 2025 revenue decline is truly one-off vs. structural?** Management attributes the €176.4M → €162.0M decline to absent transition fees, Colombian deposit taxes (€3.6M), Dutch limits, and FX. Ex-transition fees, the decline narrows to 1.2% — but operator turnover also declined 2.5%. The bull case requires genuine 2026 reacceleration; €20-25M EBITA guidance implies it, but execution risk remains.

- **Is the 2026 World Cup catalyst already priced in?** ~€5M incremental revenue (~3% lift) is material against €20-25M EBITA guidance. Sports betting stocks routinely rally into tournaments and sell off after. Does the World Cup provide a one-time spike or a permanent step-change in AI-traded product adoption?

- **What happens when Kindred's legacy turnkey contract with Kambi fully terminates?** FDJ UNITED is migrating Kindred brands to its in-house sportsbook and currently uses Kambi's Turnkey Sportsbook in markets that haven't yet transitioned. The Odds Feed+ expansion is a positive signal, but the net revenue impact of losing full-turnkey Kindred revenue while gaining partial Odds Feed+ revenue may still be negative. The transition timeline and net economics need closer scrutiny.

- **How does Kambi compete if Sportradar's ORAKO platform gains turnkey traction?** Sportradar has historically been a data/odds supplier, not a full sportsbook platform. But ORAKO is a modular turnkey platform serving 900+ operators. If Sportradar successfully converts data relationships into sportsbook platform contracts, it competes directly with Kambi's core turnkey business using a much larger operator base. Kambi's counter-argument — that its odds are traded in-house on live liquidity rather than algorithmic models — needs to hold under competitive pressure.

## Business Model & Product Description

Kambi is the "arms dealer" of the sports betting industry — a pure B2B technology infrastructure provider that enables licensed gambling operators to offer sports betting without building the technology stack themselves. The closest analogy is Shopify's relationship to e-commerce merchants: Kambi provides the core platform, trading engine, odds, and risk management while operators own the customer relationship, brand, and marketing.

**How Kambi makes money:** Historically through a revenue-share model — Kambi earns a percentage (now ~10-12%, down from ~20-25% a decade ago) of the Gross Gaming Revenue (GGR) generated by operators using its platform. GGR = total stakes wagered minus winnings returned. This means Kambi's revenue is directly tied to the volume and profitability of betting activity on its partners' platforms. Increasingly, revenue also comes from fixed-fee and subscription-based pricing for modular products (Odds Feed+, managed trading services), which de-links Kambi's revenue from operator GGR outcomes.

**Product Portfolio:**

- **Turnkey Sportsbook** — The flagship product. A fully managed, end-to-end sportsbook solution covering odds compilation, risk management, trading, player experience, and regulatory compliance. Currently deployed with 40+ partners in 50+ regulated markets. Think of this as the "full Shopify" — operators plug in and get a working sportsbook. This remains the majority of revenue but is growing more slowly as the model shifts toward modular components.

- **Odds Feed+** — The strategic growth engine. A premium odds feed delivered through a single API, providing pre-match and live odds including micro-markets and player props. Critically, these odds are *traded on Kambi's live liquidity* (€17B+ annually) rather than generated algorithmically from external data feeds, which Kambi argues produces sharper, more reliable pricing. Launched as a modular product in 2024, Odds Feed+ has signed deals with Hard Rock Digital (8 US states), FDJ UNITED, Superbet Group, Coolbet, and ComeOn Group. This product targets operators who have in-house sportsbooks but want best-in-class odds for specific markets or sports.

- **Bet Builder** — Kambi's same-game parlay (SGP) product, built natively within the turnkey platform rather than bolted on from a third party. Bet Builder accounted for 22% of pre-match bets during the 2022 World Cup, 31% during Euro 2024, and 57% during the 2024 Nations League finals — demonstrating explosive adoption. Almost 50% of all pre-match bets at Super Bowl LIX were placed via Bet Builder, with 88% containing a prop bet. This product is a key driver of operator trading margin expansion because multi-leg bets carry higher theoretical margins. Kambi was among the first sportsbooks to offer live Bet Builder with cash-out functionality.

- **Tzeract (AI Trading Division)** — Kambi's proprietary AI pricing engine, incubated internally and spun into a named division. By end-2025, Tzeract priced nearly 50% of all bets across the Kambi network, automating trading workflows, enabling faster repricing, higher bet acceptance rates, and longer market uptime. The 2026 World Cup will be the first major global tournament fully AI-traded. Tzeract's competitive advantage scales with liquidity volume — more operators feeding data into the system improve pricing accuracy for all operators, creating a genuine network effect.

- **Shape Games (Front End)** — Acquired for €38.5M in 2022. Provides customizable front-end interfaces for sportsbook operators, enabling differentiated user experiences without building UI from scratch.

- **Abios (Esports Data)** — Acquired in 2021. Esports data and odds supplier with a strategic partnership with Google. Provides live esports data feeds and odds for a rapidly growing vertical.

- **Player Account Management** — Kambi recently acquired PAM source code from OMEGA Systems, targeting Nevada and other markets where regulatory requirements mandate integrated account management capabilities. This unlocks access to casino-centric markets previously out of reach.

**Revenue Segmentation (FY2025):** Geographic turnover split is Americas 58.0%, Europe 38.6%, Rest of World 3.4%. Revenue concentration has been deliberately reduced: top-3 partners now represent 35% of revenue (down from 60%+ five years ago). The partner base includes ATG, Bally's Corporation, Corredor Empresarial, FDJ UNITED/Kindred, LeoVegas, LiveScore, Rush Street Interactive, Svenska Spel, OLG, Hard Rock Digital, Stake, and others. More than 1,000 employees across Malta (HQ), Australia, Denmark, Philippines, Romania, Sweden, UK, and USA.

## Industry Context

**Market structure: oligopoly at the top, fragmented middle and tail.** The global sports betting market is valued at ~$125B in 2026 with an 11.8% CAGR projection through 2033 driven by regulatory expansion, mobile-first adoption, and deeper betting content. The operator landscape has a clear power-law structure: 3-5 vertically integrated megacap operators (Flutter/FanDuel at ~$35B market cap, DraftKings at ~$20B, bet365 privately held, Entain/BetMGM) dominate the US and major European markets. Below them sits a long tail of hundreds of tier-2/3 operators — regional casino brands, state lotteries, emerging-market operators — who lack the engineering talent, trading liquidity, and regulatory expertise to build sportsbooks in-house. This long tail is Kambi's core market.

**The "build vs. buy" dynamic is the central industry tension.** The DraftKings migration (2020-2021) catalyzed a narrative that all successful operators would eventually vertically integrate. In practice, the economics are more nuanced. Building a world-class sportsbook requires: (a) a dedicated trading team of 200+ quantitative analysts and traders, (b) multi-year development cycles across dozens of regulated jurisdictions each with unique compliance requirements, (c) continuous R&D investment in live pricing, Bet Builder/SGP, and real-time risk management, and (d) liquidity — sharp pricing requires high bet volumes, which creates a chicken-and-egg problem for smaller operators. The industry has quietly settled into a hybrid model: even operators with proprietary sportsbooks (FDJ UNITED, Hard Rock) are buying components (Odds Feed+, managed trading) from specialists. This mirrors payments (Stripe/Adyen), cloud (AWS), and commerce (Shopify) — where build-vs-buy rhetoric gives way to "buy the components you can't differentiate on."

**Competitive landscape in B2B sportsbook technology:**

- **Sportradar (SRAD, ~$9B market cap)** — The dominant B2B data provider, serving 900+ operators in 120+ countries. Historically a data/odds feed supplier, now pushing into full sportsbook territory via its ORAKO modular platform. ORAKO combines data, odds, trading tools, and marketing services. Sportradar's competitive advantage is ubiquitous distribution and deep data relationships; its weakness is that ORAKO odds are algorithmically modeled rather than traded on live liquidity, which Kambi argues produces less sharp pricing. The Kambi-Sportradar dynamic is the key competitive axis: Sportradar moves down-stack from data into sportsbook, while Kambi moves up-stack from sportsbook into data and components.

- **OpenBet (Endeavor/IMG Arena)** — Enterprise-grade sportsbook platform powering some of the world's largest operators. Known for extreme reliability and scale during peak events. Targets the largest operators willing to pay premium prices for proven infrastructure. Less relevant to Kambi's tier-2/3 sweet spot.

- **IGT PlaySports** — US-focused sportsbook technology with 90+ locations across 30 jurisdictions. Strong in retail/land-based sports betting. Less competitive in digital-first environments.

- **Genius Sports (GENI)** — Primarily an official data provider (NFL, NCAA, EPL exclusives), now expanding into trading and betting content via BetVision (interactive in-stream betting). Competes with Sportradar on data; less directly competitive with Kambi on sportsbook platform.

- **Flutter/FanDuel, DraftKings, bet365** — These are the vertically integrated operators who have removed themselves from the B2B TAM. Their existence creates the "ceiling" narrative for Kambi bears, but their market share concentration (~70%+ in US) actually validates the long-tail opportunity: the remaining 30% of the market cannot justify $100M+ annual sportsbook development budgets.

**Regulatory tailwinds and headwinds are concurrent.** Kambi operates in 60+ regulated markets with 98% of revenue from regulated jurisdictions — a compliance moat. New market openings (Brazil day-1 in January 2025, Nevada license in January 2025, Ontario OLG launch in January 2026) expand the TAM. However, the regulatory environment is also creating headwinds: Colombia introduced deposit taxes (€3.6M 2025 impact), the Netherlands imposed deposit limits, and Illinois and other US states are increasing gaming taxes. High-tax jurisdictions compress operator margins, which puts pressure on B2B revenue-share arrangements — but paradoxically increases demand for efficient, AI-powered B2B solutions that maximize margin per bet.

**Value chain positioning:** Kambi sits between data providers (Sportradar, Genius Sports, Stats Perform) and consumer-facing operators. Its pricing model is essentially a tax on operator gross gaming revenue. The risk is that data providers move downstream (Sportradar → ORAKO) and operators move upstream (DraftKings, Kindred → in-house), squeezing the middle. The counter-argument is that the middle layer — live trading, risk management, and odds compilation on pooled liquidity — is the hardest and most capital-intensive layer to replicate, analogous to market-making in financial markets. You can build your own exchange (expensive, years of effort) or you can access NYSE/Nasdaq liquidity. Most operators are choosing the latter.

## Bull Case

- **Modular pivot expands TAM from "operators who fully outsource" to "every operator that needs best-in-class components."** Odds Feed+ makes Kambi relevant to operators with proprietary sportsbooks — the FDJ UNITED and Hard Rock Digital deals prove this. If Kambi captures even 10-15% of the in-house operator segment through component sales, the revenue opportunity exceeds the legacy turnkey business lost to vertical integration.
- **AI trading flywheel creates compounding moat.** Tzeract's pricing on €17B+ of liquidity across 45+ operators is a dataset advantage that compounds with scale. No standalone operator (except Flutter) can match this. The first fully AI-traded World Cup in 2026 is both a proof point and a catalyst — success would accelerate Odds Feed+ adoption across the industry.
- **Debt-free balance sheet + share buybacks signal management confidence at trough earnings.** Kambi is buying back stock at €15.5M adjusted EBITA while guiding to €20-25M in 2026. If the modular transition delivers operating leverage from AI cost reduction, normalized EBITA of €30-40M is achievable by 2027-2028, implying the stock trades at 8-10x forward EBITA at current levels.
- **Regulatory expansion provides organic growth runway.** Nevada license (January 2025), OLG Ontario launch (January 2026, contract through 2032), Brazil day-1 (January 2025), plus pipeline into additional US states and LatAm markets. Each new market adds incremental volume to the liquidity flywheel.
- **Sports betting industry growth (11.8% CAGR through 2033) provides a secular tailwind.** Even if Kambi merely maintains its market position, industry growth translates to mid-single-digit organic revenue growth — and the modular pivot positions Kambi to take market share from less-innovative B2B providers.

## Bear Case

- **Revenue-share compression is structural and ongoing.** Revenue-share rates have halved from 20-25% to 10-12% over a decade. If operators continue extracting pricing concessions — particularly as Sportradar's ORAKO provides a competitive alternative — Kambi's top-line may stagnate or decline even as industry volumes grow.
- **Sportradar's 900+ operator relationships dwarf Kambi's 45+.** If Sportradar successfully transitions ORAKO from data-feed to full sportsbook platform, Kambi faces competitive encirclement: losing both turnkey customers (to ORAKO) and Odds Feed+ customers (to Sportradar's native data). Sportradar's $9B market cap gives it vastly more resources for R&D and commercial investment.
- **The 2025 earnings deterioration may not be fully one-off.** Adjusted EBITA fell from €25.3M to €15.5M (-39%), with management attributing it to transition fees, Colombia taxes, and FX. But operating profit dropped even more severely (€18.8M → €8.1M, -57%). If 2026 reacceleration disappoints — missing the €20-25M EBITA guidance — the stock faces a de-rating as the market concludes the modular pivot is not compensating for turnkey erosion.
- **Geographic concentration in politically volatile jurisdictions.** Americas at 58% of turnover includes heavy exposure to Colombia (deposit tax uncertainty, possible new sports betting tax), Brazil (slower-than-expected market development), and Argentina (currency risk). European exposure includes Netherlands and UK markets facing regulatory tightening.
- **Werner Becher is unproven as Kambi CEO.** While his Sportradar pedigree is relevant, he was appointed in July 2024 and presided over a year of declining revenue and earnings. The strategic pivot is sound in theory, but execution under a new CEO with no tenure track record at Kambi adds risk. The simultaneous departure of founding CEO Nylén after 14 years is a cultural transition that could create execution gaps.

## Catalysts

- **2026 FIFA World Cup (June-July 2026)** — First fully AI-traded major tournament. ~€5M incremental revenue (~3% lift). A showcase for Tzeract's AI trading capabilities that could accelerate Odds Feed+ adoption.
- **Q1-Q2 2026 earnings** — Key test of management's €20-25M EBITA guidance and return-to-growth narrative. OLG Ontario ramp and Brazil market development will be visible in these results.
- **Tier-1 Odds Feed+ signings** — Management flagged "advanced discussions with tier-one operators." A signing with a major vertically integrated operator would be transformative for the narrative.
- **Nevada market entry** — License secured; operator partnerships pending. A US casino brand deploying Kambi in Nevada would validate the PAM acquisition strategy.
- **FDJ UNITED migration completion** — As FDJ UNITED finishes migrating Kindred brands to in-house and fully adopts Odds Feed+, the net revenue impact and margin profile will become clear.

## Risks

1. **Sportradar competitive escalation** — ORAKO platform gains traction as a cheaper, data-native alternative to Kambi's turnkey solution. Sportradar's 900+ operator relationships could convert to full sportsbook contracts faster than Kambi can win Odds Feed+ deals.
2. **Further customer concentration events** — Loss of any remaining top-3 customer (Bally's, Rush Street, ATG/Svenska Spel) would materially impact revenue and the de-concentration narrative.
3. **Regulatory/tax risk** — Colombia's potential new sports betting tax, expanding European gaming tax regimes, and US state-level tax increases compress operator margins, reducing revenue-share payments to Kambi and pressuring demand for B2B services.
4. **AI pricing commoditization** — If AI-driven odds compilation becomes commoditized (Sportradar, Genius Sports, and smaller providers all invest heavily in AI trading), Kambi's Tzeract division may lose its differentiation, and the liquidity flywheel argument weakens.
5. **Revenue-share rate compression continues** — Industry-wide trend toward lower revenue-share percentages erodes top-line even as volumes grow, requiring Kambi to run faster just to stay in place.
6. **Management transition risk** — New CEO Werner Becher's strategic pivot is sound but unproven. Cultural change from founder-led to professionally-managed company during a business model transition period creates execution risk.
7. **FX exposure** — Revenue denominated across EUR, USD, GBP, SEK, BRL, COP with limited natural hedging. Material FX movements can mask or amplify underlying performance trends.
