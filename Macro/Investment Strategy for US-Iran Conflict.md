## Hedging vulnerable sectors against drawdowns

**Volatility products** profit from market fear but require careful timing. **VIXY** offers 1x VIX exposure with moderate decay, while **UVXY** (1.5x) provides amplified short-term protection but lost 87% in 2020 despite elevated volatility. The April 2025 VIX spike to **65.73** delivered 36.94% single-day gains, but extended holders faced compounding losses. These are strictly **short-term tactical instruments**—budget 2-3% of portfolio maximum and exit on initial spike.

## Defense contractors positioned for sustained demand

**Cybersecurity** merits inclusion given Iran's proven cyber capabilities—attacks on US water utilities (2023), banking systems (2012), and petrochemical plants (2017). **CrowdStrike (CRWD)** leads with cloud-native threat detection, while **Palo Alto Networks (PANW)** is acquiring CyberArk to strengthen identity security. Both trade at premium valuations (~48x) but benefit from secular growth alongside geopolitical catalysts.

**RTX Corporation (RTX)** is the most direct Iran-conflict beneficiary as the sole Tomahawk cruise missile manufacturer ($1.1-1.9M per unit, 2,300+ fired since 1991) and primary producer of Patriot systems protecting Israel, Saudi Arabia, and UAE. Its $217B backlog provides multi-year visibility. **Lockheed Martin (LMT)** trades at 17x forward P/E—cheapest among peers—with 2.75% yield and F-35/THAAD exposure. **Northrop Grumman (NOC)** offers B-21 bomber and Global Hawk UAV exposure, with Q3 2025 defense systems sales up 14% YoY.

For drone/autonomous systems exposure, **AeroVironment (AVAV)** supplies Switchblade loitering munitions proven in Ukraine, with an $874M IDIQ contract secured December 2025 and regulatory moat from the FCC ban on Chinese drone competitors. **Kratos Defense (KTOS)** is the sole supplier of jet-powered sub-scale target drones to all US military branches, with exposure to the Golden Dome missile defense program.

## What makes Iran conflict uniquely dangerous

The Soleimani assassination (January 2020) saw brief oil spikes and gold jumping above $1,600, but markets stabilized within days as no actual supply disruption occurred. The June 2025 Israel-Iran conflict was more sustained but resolved with oil falling below pre-conflict levels following ceasefire. This pattern—brief spike, quick fade—reflects market "prove it" mentality requiring actual supply disruption.

## Safe haven assets showing record performance

**Uranium** presents a differentiated angle—Iran's nuclear program has been the focal point of strikes, potentially accelerating Western nuclear energy investment. Despite spot prices remaining rangebound ($63-83/lb), **Cameco (CCJ)** surged **114%** in 2025 as mining equities front-ran supply concerns. AI data center electricity demand provides secular tailwind, with global uranium production meeting only 90% of demand.

**Treasury ETFs** offer classic flight-to-safety exposure, though 2025 dynamics have been complicated by inflation concerns. **TLT** (20+ year Treasuries) yields approximately 5% with 16.5-year duration—meaning significant gains if Fed cuts accelerate during crisis-driven risk-off. However, oil-induced inflation could pressure long bonds, making **TIP** (inflation-protected) a hedge against that scenario. **SHY** provides near-cash stability.

# Investment strategies for US-Iran military conflict scenarios

A US military strike on Iran would activate multiple correlated market moves simultaneously—oil spikes, defense rallies, safe-haven flows, and sector-specific selloffs. The June 2025 Israel-Iran conflict provides a recent template: Brent crude surged **7-11%** while defense stocks jumped **5-10%** in single sessions. However, the critical differentiator for Iran versus other geopolitical events is the **Strait of Hormuz**—a chokepoint handling **20 million barrels daily** (20% of global crude, 27% of seaborne oil trade). A credible closure threat could push oil above **$100/barrel**, while actual disruption scenarios suggest **$120-130**. The strategies below position for prolonged tensions rather than short-term event trading.
