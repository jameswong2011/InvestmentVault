# Ten trades for the Hormuz crisis and beyond

**The Strait of Hormuz blockade has created the most asymmetric investment setup since the 2022 energy crisis — but with a structural twist.** Qatar's Ras Laffan destruction removes **~20% of global LNG supply** for 3–5 years, tanker rates have hit all-time records, and the Fed faces policy paralysis as oil at $112 Brent threatens both inflation and growth. The ten positions below exploit these dislocations across two time horizons: near-term trades that profit from Scenario A stagflation dynamics while offering escalation convexity, and longer-term structural positions that compound regardless of whether the conflict resolves into Scenario B reflation or Scenario A persists. The core investment insight is that markets are pricing a **short-lived disruption** — Brent December 2026 futures at just ~$80 — while the physical damage to Gulf infrastructure ensures supply tightness lasts years, not months.

## Bucket 1: five near-term positions for the next 90 days

These positions exploit the immediate stagflationary impulse — elevated oil, accelerating inflation, Fed paralysis, and equity fragility — while offering asymmetric upside if the crisis deepens further. The VIX at 31 signals fear but not panic; the market has not yet priced a prolonged Hormuz closure or conflict expansion.

# Iran war scenario analysis: stagflation vs. QE pivot

**The US-Iran war — now 30 days old — has triggered the largest oil supply disruption in history, creating a textbook stagflation shock that has boxed the Federal Reserve into its most consequential policy dilemma since 2022.** Brent crude has surged from ~$71 to **$112/barrel** since Operation Epic Fury launched on February 28, with Dubai physical crude hitting $126. The effective closure of the Strait of Hormuz has removed **~17.8 million bpd** from global oil transit, while Iranian missile strikes have inflicted billions in damage to Gulf energy infrastructure — including Qatar's Ras Laffan LNG complex (3–5 year repair timeline) and Saudi, Kuwaiti, and UAE refineries. Markets now price a **52% probability of a Fed rate hike** by year-end, the S&P 500 is down ~5% YTD, and the VIX has doubled to 31. The critical question for institutional portfolios is whether this shock resolves as 1970s-style stagflation (Scenario A) or triggers a recessionary demand destruction that forces a dovish pivot (Scenario B). Current market pricing — steep oil backwardation, elevated but contained breakevens, and still-tight credit spreads — suggests investors are leaning toward a temporary disruption that resolves within months. This consensus may be dangerously complacent.

## What the market is mispricing — and when it corrects

The positions above are structured around the thesis that this physical reality eventually forces the futures curve — and equity markets — to reprice. When it does, the near-term bucket captures the initial move while the longer-term bucket compounds the structural aftermath for years. The greatest risk is not being wrong about the direction, but being early — which is why defined-risk options structures (Positions 3 and 4) and trailing stops (Position 1) are essential risk management tools for the tactical portion of the portfolio.

The futures curve is the clearest signal of consensus error. **Brent December 2026 at ~$80** implies the market expects near-full resolution within 9 months. But the physical market tells a different story: Dubai physical crude trades at **$138–$140/bbl** — a $37–$40 premium over futures. This physical-futures disconnect is described by market participants as "futures prices separating from reality." Rystad Energy estimates the Gulf infrastructure repair bill exceeds **$25B**, with large-frame gas turbine backlogs of 2–4 years creating a binding constraint on LNG restoration.

### 1. Scorpio Tankers (STNG) — the shipping bottleneck play

The thesis is not just the rate spike itself but the **structural tonne-mile increase** from rerouting. Saudi and UAE crude now flows through bypass pipelines to Yanbu and Fujairah, then onto tankers for longer Atlantic Basin routes. This adds 5,000–8,000 nautical miles per voyage, creating a global vessel shortage that lifts rates even on non-Middle East routes. Insurance cancellations by major P&I clubs have further constrained effective fleet supply.

## Bucket 2: five structural positions into 2027

These positions are designed to compound across both scenarios. They exploit structural supply gaps (LNG, uranium), multi-year spending commitments (defense), or position for the eventual Scenario B transition to monetary accommodation. The key insight: even if the conflict resolves tomorrow, the physical damage to Gulf infrastructure creates investment themes that persist for 3–5 years minimum.

### 3. VIX 35/50 call spreads — asymmetric tail protection

With VIX at 31 and the term structure in backwardation, outright volatility purchases are expensive. But the market is pricing "significant but manageable" risk — not a full regional war or permanent supply disruption. Historically, VIX has only been in backwardation ~16–20% of the time, and the current level of 31 is well below true crisis extremes (COVID hit 80, 2008 touched 60+). A **VIX 35/50 call spread** with 1–2 month expiry provides **10:1 to 15:1 payoff** if the conflict escalates to scenarios the market hasn't priced: Hormuz mining, Iranian retaliation against Gulf state oil facilities, or a broader regional war drawing in Hezbollah.

The cost structure is approximately **$1.50–$2.50 per spread** depending on expiry. If VIX stays below 35, the spread expires worthless — but the loss is capped and small relative to portfolio size. If VIX spikes to 45–50 (plausible in a major escalation), the spread pays $10–$15, a 5–7x return. Historical precedent: the 1990 Gulf War pushed volatility analog to 35–40; the 2022 Ukraine invasion hit VIX 36. The current conflict is arguably more supply-disruptive than either.

**Risk factors:** VIX mean-reverts aggressively on any peace signal. Trump's April 6 pause deadline on Iranian energy infrastructure strikes could trigger rapid de-escalation.

## What markets are actually pricing right now

**Breakevens: stagflation priced but viewed as temporary.** The 5-year breakeven at **2.63%** versus the 10-year at **2.34%** reveals the market's crucial assumption: near-term inflation will be significantly higher, but long-term inflation expectations remain anchored near the Fed's target. This pricing implies the oil shock is transitory — a bet on conflict resolution within 6–12 months. If this assumption breaks, the 10-year breakeven repricing to 2.8%+ would trigger significant portfolio rebalancing.

**Credit: complacency.** HY OAS at **312 bps** is remarkably tight for a wartime environment. For context, HY spreads hit 500+ in the 2018 oil crash, 1,000+ during COVID, and 800+ in 2022. The market is pricing no recession and no credit event — the most vulnerable positioning in the current setup. The composition has improved (58% BB-rated, lowest CCC share ever), but airlines, shipping, and consumer-facing credits are under severe stress.

**Equities: moderately stressed but not panicked.** The S&P 500 at 6,369 and forward P/E of 19.9x represent a ~7% drawdown from highs — a correction, not a crash. The VIX at 31 is elevated but well below crisis levels (40+). The massive sector rotation into energy (+25% YTD) and defense stocks (RTX +22%, LMT +19%) while tech lags (-3.6%) shows portfolio repositioning, not liquidation. Goldman's year-end target of 7,600 implies 19% upside — the Street still leans bullish on resolution.

**Net assessment: markets are pricing a temporary shock that resolves by mid-2026**, with the Fed holding steady and growth recovering. Scenario B (dovish pivot) is more priced in than Scenario A (stagflation), but neither is fully discounted. The biggest mispricing appears to be in credit, where spreads are too tight for the range of outcomes.

**Oil curve: temporary disruption consensus.** The **steep backwardation** (WTI front month $99.64 vs. October $80.40, December Brent ~$79.70) is the market's strongest statement: this is a supply shock, not a permanent structural shift. The long end of the curve is only $10 above pre-conflict levels. This prices in Strait of Hormuz reopening by Q3 2026 — an assumption that looks increasingly fragile given the 3–5 year repair timeline for destroyed infrastructure.

The current cross-asset configuration reveals a market caught between scenarios, with a slight lean toward Scenario A (stagflation) in rates but Scenario B (resolution) in commodities.

**Rates market: leaning hawkish.** Fed funds futures show **52% probability of a rate hike** by year-end and zero probability of a cut — a dramatic reversal from early March, when two cuts were priced. The 2-year Treasury at **3.88%** above the effective fed funds rate (3.64%) is the clearest signal: the front end is pricing tighter policy. The 2s10s spread at **+56 bps** has solidly normalized from inversion, consistent with an inflation-driven bear steepening.

## The dashboard for tracking which scenario wins

**The highest-conviction view as of March 30, 2026:** The most likely path is a hybrid — Scenario A dynamics dominate for the next 2–3 months (inflation accelerates, Fed holds or signals hawkishness, equities grind lower, energy outperforms), followed by a Scenario B pivot in Q3 if the conflict resolves or demand destruction becomes undeniable. The market's pricing of this as a temporary shock may prove correct on a 12-month horizon but dangerously wrong on a 3-month horizon. The **key asymmetric risk** is that infrastructure destruction (3–5 year Ras Laffan repair, Hormuz mine-clearing timeline, damaged Gulf refineries) creates a permanent supply deficit that persists well beyond any ceasefire — in which case Scenario A becomes structural rather than cyclical, and the 10-year breakeven repricing to 3%+ triggers a genuine bond bear market.

**Tier 3: structural signals (check monthly).** PMI employment sub-indices falling below 45 would indicate corporations are responding to the shock with layoffs, accelerating the Scenario B path. The **Senior Loan Officer Survey** (next release April/May covering Q1) will reveal whether banks are tightening lending standards in response to the conflict — a leading indicator of credit crunch dynamics. **China crude import data** and strategic petroleum reserve drawdown estimates will indicate whether the world's largest importer is absorbing or amplifying the supply shock. **OPEC+ compliance data** and any emergency production decisions signal whether marginal barrels can reach market through alternative routes.

Positions should reflect this sequencing: overweight energy, defense, and real assets near-term; maintain optionality through long volatility exposure (VIX at 31 still offers reasonable entry for tail hedges); underweight consumer discretionary and highly leveraged credits; and prepare to rotate aggressively into duration and risk assets at the first credible signal of diplomatic resolution or Fed capitulation.

### 7. Northrop Grumman (NOC) — cheapest defense prime with unpriced upside

Among the four US defense primes, Northrop offers the **best risk-adjusted entry**. At 23–25x trailing earnings, it trades at a significant discount to RTX (37–42x), LMT (29–30x), and the sector average (44x). This discount exists partly because of Sentinel ICBM execution concerns and a prior B-21 loss provision — but both overhangs are clearing. The Air Force signed a contract in February 2026 to **accelerate B-21 Raider production**, with plans to lift capacity by 25% using $4.5B in FY2025 funds. This acceleration is **not yet reflected in 2026 guidance** — it's a pure upside catalyst.

## Scenario B: dovish Fed, QE pivot, and asset price reflation

**Equity multiple expansion.** The forward P/E at **19.9x** has compressed from 22.0x at year-end 2025. If the Fed floods liquidity while earnings hold (energy sector offsets consumer weakness), the multiple re-rates toward 22–24x as the "TINA" (There Is No Alternative) trade reasserts. This is the 2020 playbook: massive monetary expansion offsetting real economic weakness, generating nominal asset price gains despite fundamental deterioration.

## Scenario A: hawkish Fed, stagflation, and falling asset prices

The wage-price spiral risk is real but contained for now. Average hourly earnings growth of ~3.7% exceeds the Fed's comfort zone, but the labor market shows a "low-hire, low-fire" pattern — February nonfarm payrolls printed -92,000 while initial claims remain historically low at 210,000. The danger lies in the second-round effects: gasoline at $3.98/gallon (up $1.00 in one month, with California cities hitting $8) feeds directly into transportation costs, food prices (+3.1% YoY already), and services inflation through input cost pass-through. S&P Global's flash PMI explicitly warns that price gauges "point to consumer price inflation accelerating back to around 4%."

**Corporate earnings compression.** Q1 2026 earnings estimates assume 13% growth, but every $10/barrel increase in oil reduces S&P 500 EPS by roughly $2–3 through input costs and consumer spending drag. At $112 Brent, the energy windfall for XLE is more than offset by margin pressure across consumer discretionary, industrials, and transportation. Goldman Sachs warns a severe scenario could push the S&P to **5,400** — a 15% drawdown from current levels.

## Private Credit Systemic Risk — Key Scenario B Trigger (from Gemini research)

**The private credit market ($1.7 trillion) is the most likely transmission mechanism for a Scenario B pivot.** Default rates have hit 9.2% (vs 2008 bank loan defaults), up from 3.5% average in 2025. Several major private credit funds have implemented redemption restrictions (gating). If defaults reach 15%, a "Lehman-style" event in private credit would be the tipping point forcing central banks from hawkish inflation-fighting to emergency QE. This is the highest-conviction leading indicator for timing the shift from Scenario A to Scenario B.
