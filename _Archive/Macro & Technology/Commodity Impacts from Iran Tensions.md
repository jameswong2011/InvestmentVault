## The insurance mechanism matters more than navies

The most important structural lesson from this crisis is that **modern maritime insurance killed Hormuz transit before the IRGC navy did**. War risk premiums jumped from 0.125% to 1–3% of hull value within 48 hours — a single transit of a $100 million tanker went from ~$200,000 to over $1 million. Lloyd's issued 72-hour cancellation notices and redesignated the entire Persian Gulf as a conflict zone. Every major container line suspended service autonomously, without waiting for government directives. This mechanism is fundamentally different from the 1980s Tanker War, when insurance remained available at elevated but manageable premiums and shipping continued despite hundreds of attacks. The financialization of maritime risk means that **even partial reopening of the Strait may not immediately restore commercial flows** — insurers will demand sustained proof of safety before repricing. The implication for commodity markets is that the post-ceasefire normalization could take weeks to months for energy flows, and years for LNG given the physical infrastructure damage. Traders should not assume a ceasefire equals an immediate return to pre-war commodity prices.

## Base metals: aluminium is the most exposed industrial commodity

**Iron ore** at **~$100–107/t** is the notable outlier — likely to underperform in this environment. China's property sector continues declining, Simandou (Guinea) shipped first ore in December 2025 with 15–20 million MT expected in 2026, and higher energy costs squeeze steel margins without translating into higher iron ore prices. The World Bank projects iron ore falling **4% in both 2026 and 2027**. **Nickel** at $15,500–17,800/t remains structurally oversupplied (120,000–275,000t surplus) despite Indonesian ore production cuts; **zinc** at $3,115/t is transitioning to oversupply despite Iran's significant reserves (world's largest); **tin** at $45,788/t operates largely independent of Iran dynamics, driven instead by AI/electronics demand and Myanmar/Indonesia supply constraints; **lithium** (CNY 138,000–145,000/t) has minimal Hormuz exposure, with price driven by battery demand cycles rather than geopolitics.

**Copper** at **$12,195/t** faces compounding tightness. The market was already flipping from surplus to a projected **150,000–600,000t deficit** in 2026 (estimates vary by source) before the crisis removed Iranian production (world's 8th largest producer) and raised energy costs across the mining sector. Energy represents **~30% of extraction costs and up to 50% of smelting costs**. Strong structural demand from AI data centers (~475,000t in 2026), EVs (80–100 kg per vehicle), and grid modernization keeps the demand side robust. The 20-year copper-oil correlation of ~0.94 remains the highest among base metals.

### Trade 5: Long VanEck Gold Miners ETF (GDX)

**Rationale:** Gold miners offer **leveraged exposure to gold** at a point of maximum pessimism. Gold's 20% selloff from its $5,595 all-time high — during an actual war — represents the deepest wartime gold decline in BullionVault's 50-year dataset. The selloff was driven by hawkish Fed expectations (holding 3.5–3.75%), dollar strength, and forced liquidation — NOT by fundamental weakness. Central banks are expected to purchase **755–850 tonnes** in 2026, providing a structural demand floor. JP Morgan targets **$6,300/oz** by year-end; Deutsche Bank targets $6,000. Miners at current levels are pricing gold at roughly $4,000–4,200/oz, well below spot. The trade thesis works under two different paths: (a) if the crisis persists and the Fed eventually cuts to combat recession, gold rallies sharply and miners outperform by 2–3x; (b) if the crisis resolves rapidly, the inflation scare fades, the Fed cuts, and gold rallies on easier monetary conditions. GDX's top holdings (Newmont +21% in March, Barrick +18%) demonstrate the operational leverage. Gold mining costs rise with oil (energy is 20–30% of production costs), but at $4,400/oz spot, margins remain extraordinarily wide even with oil at $115.

**Best scenario:** Tier 2 or Tier 1 (de-escalation) — paradoxically, gold and miners may perform better if the crisis eases, because the removal of the inflation threat allows the Fed to cut, which is the primary bullish catalyst for gold. Under Tier 3 (sustained crisis), the thesis depends on the eventual pivot from inflation-fighting to recession-fighting.

## Precious metals: gold's counterintuitive crash and silver's volatility

Gold's performance has defied safe-haven orthodoxy. After hitting an all-time high of **$5,595/oz** in January 2026, gold has fallen ~20% to **$4,430–4,450/oz**, including a $2 trillion flash crash on March 23. The explanation lies in the transmission mechanism: oil-driven inflation → hawkish Fed holding at 3.5–3.75% → stronger dollar → pressure on non-yielding assets → margin calls → forced liquidation. This mirrors the early stages of 1970s oil shocks, when gold initially sold off before ultimately rallying dramatically. Central bank buying of **~755–850 tonnes** expected in 2026 provides a structural floor. JP Morgan targets **$6,300/oz** by year-end; Deutsche Bank targets $6,000.

**Silver** at **$62–70/oz** has been the most volatile precious metal, losing nearly half its value from January's $121 record high. Its dual industrial/monetary nature creates a tug-of-war: safe-haven buying versus demand destruction fears for its **59% industrial use** (solar PV alone accounts for 29% of industrial demand). Silver's seventh consecutive year of supply deficit and structurally constrained mine supply (70% produced as byproduct) suggest the selloff has overshot fundamentals.

### Trade 3: Long Cheniere Energy (LNG) equity

**Entry considerations:** The stock received an "abrupt tailwind" from the Ras Laffan strike. Entry on any dip related to ceasefire optimism — the structural LNG supply gap from Ras Laffan damage persists for years regardless of conflict outcome. This is the single best structural long in the commodity space because the damage is already done and cannot be quickly reversed.

**Rationale:** The Iranian drone strike on Qatar's Ras Laffan eliminated **~17% of global LNG capacity**, with QatarEnergy's CEO warning repairs could take up to five years. Asian spot LNG prices have surged **140%** from pre-war levels. There is no pipeline bypass for LNG — unlike oil, where Saudi and UAE pipelines provide partial relief. Cheniere, as the largest US LNG exporter, operates facilities entirely outside the conflict zone and directly fills the supply gap. US LNG feed gas is running near-record at >17 Bcf/day. New US capacity (Plaquemines, Golden Pass) is ramping, providing volume growth. European storage at 29% (vs. 59% five-year average) guarantees strong pull for US LNG cargoes through the next heating season.

## Energy: the epicenter of disruption

**Crude oil** sits at the center of this crisis. Brent peaked at **$126/bbl** intraday, while Dubai physical crude traded at $126 — a wider premium to paper futures that signals physical tightness exceeding what derivatives markets have priced. WTI briefly crossed $100 for the first time since 2022. Goldman Sachs estimates an **$14–18/bbl risk premium** embedded in current prices, with analyst forecasts ranging from $120 (Citi near-term) to $153–200/bbl if Kharg Island infrastructure is damaged or disruption extends past April. The Dallas Fed models WTI averaging $98 in Q2, with global GDP reduced by **2.9 percentage points annualized** at current levels. OPEC+ spare capacity of ~3.5 million bpd is largely **locked behind Hormuz** — Saudi Arabia and UAE can access some via pipeline bypasses, but the effective spare capacity accessible to global markets is under 2 million bpd.

**Natural gas** has bifurcated dramatically by region. Henry Hub remains relatively insulated at **~$3.05/MMBtu** due to US self-sufficiency, though LNG export demand provides a tailwind. European TTF has surged to **€54.52/MWh** (~$16–17/MMBtu), doubling from pre-war levels, with EU storage at a critically low **29%** versus a five-year average of 59%. Asian JKM spiked to the mid-$20s/MMBtu, up **140%** from pre-war levels, after the Ras Laffan strike. Goldman Sachs warns a month-long disruption could push TTF/JKM to **€74/MWh** — the demand-destruction threshold from the 2022 energy crisis. The LNG situation is structurally worse than oil: **no pipeline bypass exists** for Qatari LNG, and damage to Ras Laffan may reduce global LNG supply by 17% for years, not months.

**Coal** has rallied to **$135.60/ton** (Newcastle thermal), up 40% year-over-year, as utilities substitute coal for disrupted LNG supplies. **Uranium** trades at **~$92/lb**, supported by nuclear renaissance narratives (AI data center demand, reactor life extensions) and the structural supply deficit that Russia's nuclear fuel export ban (effective August 2024) has created. The Iran crisis reinforces nuclear energy's strategic value as a non-hydrocarbon baseload source, with Cameco (CCJ) attracting strong institutional inflows.

## Agriculture and fertilizers: the food security transmission channel

The **fertilizer nexus** is the critical transmission mechanism from energy disruption to global food prices. Middle East countries account for **~49% of global urea exports, ~30% of ammonia exports, and ~50% of sulfur exports** — virtually all transiting Hormuz. Qatar's LNG shutdown has halted downstream urea production. Iran itself controls **10–12% of global urea trade**. The result: urea has surged from ~$490/MT to **$700/MT** (+50%), ammonia is approaching $600/MT, and spring planting season in the Northern Hemisphere is underway with **30% of global exportable fertilizer supply offline**. This echoes the 2022 Russia-Ukraine fertilizer crisis but is potentially worse because it affects multiple producers simultaneously.

**Palm oil** (MYR 4,631/MT) has the **strongest direct correlation with crude oil** among agricultural commodities via Indonesia's B40 biodiesel mandate, which consumes 23% of annual CPO output. Higher crude makes biodiesel more competitive, tightening edible oil supply. **Sugar** (15.30–15.80¢/lb) benefits from the same biofuel mechanism: higher gasoline prices redirect Brazilian sugarcane crushing toward ethanol production (Brazil plans **4 billion liters** additional ethanol in 2026/27), reducing sugar supply despite a fundamental global surplus of 8.3 million MT.

## The three tiers are no longer hypothetical

**Tier 2 (Direct strikes on Iran)** was triggered on February 28. The US and Israel launched coordinated strikes destroying nuclear facilities, military installations, and senior leadership. Iran's retaliatory missile strikes hit US bases across five Gulf states, killing at least one civilian in Abu Dhabi. Oil jumped 8% on the first trading day. Historical precedent from the 2020 Soleimani strike and June 2025 conflict suggested risk premiums of 5–13% that would fade within days — **but this time was different**.

## Livestock, rubber, lumber, and the least-exposed commodities

**Live cattle** ($233–242/cwt) and **feeder cattle** ($362–370/cwt) are at the intersection of a 75-year herd low (smallest US calf crop since 1941) and rising feed costs from the fertilizer/energy shock. The supply-side bullish case remains intact — beef production is forecast down, the Mexico border remains closed to cattle imports due to screwworm, and herd rebuilding is a multi-year process. However, rising corn and feed costs compress cow-calf margins, potentially **slowing herd expansion** and extending the price cycle. **Lean hogs** ($95–98/cwt) face similar feed cost pressures with less supply-side support.

**Rubber** (~188¢/kg) is in its fifth consecutive year of supply deficit (665,000 MT in 2025), and higher oil prices make natural rubber more competitive versus petroleum-derived synthetic rubber. **Lumber** ($590–614/MBF) faces housing demand destruction risk from higher interest rates but benefits from a 3.8 million unit US housing shortage and Canadian tariffs of ~35%. **Potash** ($487/ton) is the most stable fertilizer, produced primarily in Canada (97% of US imports) with minimal Gulf exposure.
