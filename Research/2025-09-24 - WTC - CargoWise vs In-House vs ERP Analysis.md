# WiseTech CargoWise vs In-House vs ERP: Cost, Adoption & Growth

## 1. Total Cost of Ownership: CargoWise vs Internal or ERP Systems  
**Build vs Buy – Cost Drivers:** Developing a global freight management system in-house or via a general ERP (e.g. SAP TM or Oracle) entails significant ongoing investment in IT staff, hardware, and updates. As one mid-sized forwarder (Clasquin) found after 30+ years on a custom TMS, *“each new layer and request required more time and resources,”* often forcing redevelopment of entire system parts. This complexity (especially for multi-country compliance) drove them to adopt CargoWise. In contrast, CargoWise’s multi-tenant cloud platform spreads R&D and infrastructure costs across many customers, yielding *“staggering cost advantages”* over maintaining private data centers. Even intelligence agencies outsource to cloud providers for cost – a scale individual firms *“cannot match”*. 

**Licensing & Implementation:** CargoWise is offered on a subscription/usage model, avoiding the large upfront capex of building from scratch. However, initial setup and integration are still substantial projects – often done with CargoWise certified partners. Industry observers note *“set-up costs can be initially quite intense,”* with top consultants in high demand (and commanding premium fees). Large forwarders like DHL have taken a phased rollout approach to manage change and costs, deploying CargoWise gradually across regions. By choosing CargoWise, DHL *“realized we don’t have to do everything by ourselves… we looked for the best technical solution… and decided to go with CargoWise”*. This saved DHL from the expense of modernizing legacy mainframes and coordinating 4,000+ IT staff internally. In essence, CargoWise shifts much of the development burden (and cost) to WiseTech, while the forwarder pays license fees and a one-time implementation cost.

**Maintenance & Upgrades:** Using CargoWise also offloads the ongoing cost of updates (e.g. regulatory changes) to WiseTech. For example, customs compliance updates are continuous – WiseTech has teams updating the software for new laws and e-invoicing rules, which gave Clasquin *“peace of mind”* that they stay compliant without constant in-house redevelopment. By comparison, an internally built or heavily customized ERP solution requires the company to bear these maintenance costs (or pay vendors for upgrades). Panalpina’s experience is illustrative: it invested in an SAP-based TMS and argued it would leverage existing capex and yield returns. But this strategy carried risks – Panalpina had to manage all enhancements and *“hoped for rising returns”* on that investment. After Panalpina was acquired by DSV in 2019, the combined entity moved onto CargoWise, underscoring the difficulty of keeping pace with a bespoke ERP approach. As a logistics tech analyst noted, even if upfront costs are sunk, *“what is the annual cost to maintain and augment legacy systems?”* – including hardware, data centers, and patching – these **must** be weighed against SaaS fees. CargoWise’s cloud model also scales capacity on demand, whereas self-hosted systems might require costly infrastructure expansion for growth.

**Real-World Comparison – Kuehne+Nagel:** K+N, the world’s largest forwarder, long relied on proprietary systems for freight operations. Recently, it chose CargoWise for a **global customs and compliance rollout**, rather than attempt an internal build for each country. This “watershed” deal suggests that even industry giants see a third-party platform as more cost-effective for complex, multi-country functions. WiseTech had been expanding its single global customs engine since 2017, now covering all major English-speaking economies, the largest EU economies, big Asian export markets, and Brazil. Achieving ~90% coverage of world trade flows is WiseTech’s goal – a level of breadth that would be enormously expensive and time-consuming for one forwarder to develop alone. By leveraging CargoWise, K+N avoids the *“significant resources”* and years of development an in-house global customs system would require, and instead pays license fees for a ready-made solution continuously updated by WiseTech. While exact dollar figures are proprietary, analysts estimate large global 3PLs could easily spend **hundreds of millions** to build and maintain an internal TMS over time. In contrast, CargoWise licensing for a top-25 forwarder might be on the order of **tens of millions per year**, plus implementation – an expense, but one that includes ongoing improvements and scalability. Importantly, CargoWise’s unified platform can drive productivity gains (e.g. automation reducing manual work). DSV and Geodis, early CargoWise adopters, were able to **reduce headcount** by thousands during COVID while keeping operations flowing – implying labor cost savings that offset licensing fees. In short, **the TCO of CargoWise is often lower** when factoring in reduced IT overhead and higher efficiency, despite annual subscription costs. 

**Scalability & Future-Proofing:** Internally developed systems can become brittle or costly to modernize (as K+N experienced with its aging core system). ERP solutions like SAP/Oracle offer scalability, but they often lack freight-specific features out-of-the-box, leading to **expensive customizations**. By contrast, CargoWise is purpose-built for global logistics and continuously enhanced. Users benefit from new modules and updates without a big version upgrade project. This helps avoid the **“technical debt”** that in-house systems accrue. However, one downside of outsourcing to CargoWise is **vendor lock-in** and pricing power. WiseTech has imposed regular price increases (about 5% annually in recent years) on CargoWise users. While WiseTech often justifies these hikes by *“inflation and new functionality”* added at no extra cost, it means customers must absorb rising fees over time. Still, most large forwarders judge that the benefits (global scope, faster innovation, less IT headache) outweigh the risk of these “golden handcuffs.” DHL’s CIO noted that at their scale, outsourcing wasn’t automatically cheaper *“1-for-1”*, given existing IT capacity. But for many logistics providers, **CargoWise provides a faster ROI** by avoiding multi-year development cycles and delivering productivity improvements sooner. In summary, **CargoWise tends to have a lower TCO over the long run** when considering full lifecycle costs – especially for achieving global, integrated capabilities – although upfront implementation spend and ongoing fees must be planned for. Companies like DHL, DSV, Toll, Geodis, **and 24 of the top 25 forwarders overall** have evidently reached that conclusion, as nearly all now use CargoWise in some form.

## 2. CargoWise Module Penetration and Adoption  
WiseTech’s CargoWise platform is modular, and adoption varies by module. The **core Freight Forwarding** module (global TMS for air/sea/road forwarding) has the deepest penetration. This is CargoWise’s flagship functionality and essentially all CargoWise enterprise customers use it. In fact, 11 of the Top 25 global forwarders have signed on for full CargoWise **global forwarding rollouts**, contributing to 47 large forwarder rollouts in total. By WiseTech’s estimates, CargoWise now handles **over half of global manufactured trade volume** through these forwarders. In practical terms, this suggests that millions of shipments and bookings – roughly ~50% of world trade flows – are managed via the CargoWise forwarding module. Nearly every major 3PL (except a rare holdout) is leveraging CargoWise as a backbone for freight operations. Over *“90% of the top players in the industry”* are CargoWise customers. Furthermore, more than **40,000 logistics professionals** have become CargoWise Certified users – underscoring how widespread the core platform’s use is in the industry.

**Customs & Compliance:** The customs clearance module is a critical complement to forwarding, and its adoption has ramped up as WiseTech expanded country coverage. CargoWise’s single global customs engine now covers the major trading economies (all key English-speaking and EU markets, top Asian export countries, etc.), with a stated goal of reaching 90% of world trade coverage. Many large forwarders that use CargoWise for freight also use it for customs in countries where available. For example, DSV and DHL Global Forwarding run integrated customs processes on CargoWise in multiple countries. Until recently, some giants like Kuehne+Nagel used in-house tools for customs; however, in FY2023 K+N became the **first top-tier forwarder to commit to a *global*** CargoWise customs rollout. This was followed post-year-end by FedEx Logistics planning to do the same. With these wins, **customs module penetration** among CargoWise’s big customers is set to deepen further. We can infer that a majority of CargoWise seats (especially at global 3PLs) now include customs functionality – at least in core trade lanes – since forwarders want a single system for end-to-end shipment execution and compliance. In terms of transaction share, WiseTech hasn’t disclosed exact declaration counts, but it likely processes **millions of customs entries annually**. CargoWise’s customs adoption is also global: the platform is used for entries across North America, Europe, Oceania, and parts of Asia/LatAm, with more countries being added via acquisitions (e.g. recent moves into Colombia, Finland, Chile/Mexico). As of 2024, WiseTech’s CEO noted the *“global customs rollout with [K+N]… is a testament”* to their customs product and foothold strategy. **Bottom line:** the customs module is becoming nearly as indispensable as the forwarding module for CargoWise customers, approaching a penetration of **~80%+** of those customers’ shipments (and growing toward the 90% trade flow target).

**Warehouse Management (WMS):** CargoWise offers an integrated Warehouse suite, including modules for **transit warehouses**, bonded warehouses, omnichannel e-commerce warehouses, etc. Historically, adoption of CargoWise WMS lagged the core modules, as many 3PLs used specialized WMS solutions. However, WiseTech has invested heavily to broaden its warehouse capabilities – launching a new CargoWise Warehouse Suite in 2023 with five “modalities” (Transit, Short-term, eCommerce fulfillment, Long-term, Bonded) under a single application. This unified WMS is **designed for both international and landside logistics** and is tightly integrated with the forwarding and customs modules. The ease of having one platform is driving higher uptake. Many forwarders now implement the **transit warehouse** module for their cross-dock operations and consolidation warehouses (where shipments are staged briefly). For instance, a freight forwarder can receive cargo, break bulk, and dispatch from a transit warehouse all within CargoWise – inventory visibility links straight into the forwarding job. Adoption of the broader Warehouse module (for contract logistics or long-term storage) is on the rise but still moderate. Some large logistics providers (with sizable contract logistics businesses) continue to run legacy WMS systems in their warehouses and have not fully switched those to CargoWise. Nonetheless, midsize forwarders and those without a heavy bespoke WMS have been rolling out CargoWise Warehouse to eliminate interfaces between forwarding and warehousing. WiseTech has highlighted that the Warehouse Suite *“easily scales to global, multisite, high-volume”* operations and reduces total cost of ownership for warehouse IT. We estimate that **a few hundred warehouses worldwide** are now managed on CargoWise (ranging from transit hubs to 3PL distribution centers), and this number is growing as the new suite gains traction. In percentage terms, perhaps ~20–30% of CargoWise’s large customer base is actively using its WMS modules today – a figure that could climb as the value of a single-system solution prompts more module adoption.

**CargoWise Neo (Customer Portal):** Neo is a relatively new addition (announced around 2020) – a digital **shipper portal** that allows forwarders’ end-customers (the BCOs) to get quotes, place bookings, track shipments, and manage documents in real time. It’s essentially a self-service interface layered on the CargoWise platform. Neo’s penetration is in early stages but promising. Large forwarders are beginning to offer Neo to their clients as a value-added service. For example, during DHL’s rollout, the plan has been to use CargoWise’s customer-facing capabilities (which likely includes Neo) for a unified experience. Likewise, mid-tier forwarders like Seko Logistics have leveraged CargoWise to build customer portals. WiseTech has not published user counts for Neo, but anecdotal evidence suggests adoption is building: by late 2024, forwarders were actively rolling it out after initial delays in the product’s release. Neo essentially replaces older web-tracker tools that forwarders had, so its *“command center”* dashboard appeals to any forwarder who wants to modernize client interaction. We estimate only a **small percentage (perhaps <10%) of CargoWise-using forwarders** had fully deployed Neo to their customers by 2023, but this is expected to accelerate. Even with that limited penetration, Neo can have a broad reach – one forwarder’s portal can serve hundreds of BCO customers. As WiseTech refines Neo, we anticipate thousands of shipper-users on the platform in the near term. In summary, Neo is **not yet as ubiquitous** as the core operational modules, but it represents a strategic growth area to deepen CargoWise’s footprint (by extending directly to shippers). WiseTech itself views **Neo and customs** as two big opportunities to expand usage in coming years.

**International eCommerce:** WiseTech introduced the International eCommerce module to help logistics providers handle cross-border e-commerce flows (from origin through last-mile). This module covers order fulfillment, customs clearance for parcels, international line-haul, and injection into last-mile networks – effectively an end-to-end solution for cross-border B2C shipments. Adoption here is currently limited to forwarders and 3PLs who have significant e-commerce logistics businesses. Many traditional freight forwarders are only just entering the e-commerce parcel space, so penetration is relatively **low** (likely a single-digit percentage of CargoWise customers use it so far). However, interest is growing as global e-commerce volumes remain high. CargoWise’s value proposition is that a forwarder can manage an e-commerce order alongside its regular freight in one system – for example, using the *International Ecommerce* module to orchestrate online orders from a warehouse in China to a consumer in Europe, with automated customs and parcel label generation. WiseTech’s acquisitions of parcel tech (like SmartFreight/Pierbridge) have been folded into this offering. Quantitatively, there aren’t public metrics on e-commerce transactions in CargoWise, but we know e-commerce is a minority of the business today. WiseTech’s goal is to position CargoWise for the **rapid growth** in cross-border B2C trade. Thus, while **module penetration for eCommerce is currently the smallest of those listed**, the module is available globally and ready as more logistics providers diversify into omnichannel fulfillment. For instance, a forwarder like DB Schenker or Geodis could start using CargoWise eCommerce to serve retail clients – and when they do, that could bring large transaction volumes onto the platform. In sum, **Freight Forwarding and Customs** have the highest adoption (core to most users), **Warehouse and Landside** modules have moderate and growing use (especially for transit and bonded facilities), and newer offerings like **Neo and International eCommerce** are in early adoption phases with significant headroom to expand. WiseTech often notes that its strategy is to get existing customers to adopt more modules (“deepen penetration”) – so each of these modules’ uptake is expected to increase over time as CargoWise becomes an all-in-one solution.

## 3. CargoWise Revenue Growth Breakdown: Pricing vs Volume  
WiseTech’s CargoWise platform has enjoyed rapid revenue growth over the past decade. To understand the drivers, it’s important to separate **price effects** from **volume (usage) effects**. CargoWise revenue growth has come from two main sources: **(a)** higher revenue per transaction/user (through price rises and upselling more modules), and **(b)** greater transaction volume (more users, more transactions, new customers). According to WiseTech’s financial disclosures, CargoWise achieved a ~33% compound annual growth in recurring revenue from FY2016 to FY2023. The **majority** of this growth was driven by volume expansion rather than pure price increases.

- **Pricing Increases:** WiseTech has implemented annual price uplifts (often around inflation levels). In mid-2024, for instance, it enacted a ~5% general price increase for CargoWise licenses. Over FY16–FY23, price rises (including inflation-linked uplifts and charging for product enhancements) contributed roughly **5 percentage points** of the annual growth on average. This equates to roughly **15% of total growth** coming from higher pricing. WiseTech explicitly notes *“price increases to offset inflation and generate returns on product investment”* as a key growth factor. In addition, the company has periodically monetized new features by folding them into the core product and uplifting subscription rates (often justified by added functionality). Some of WiseTech’s pricing power comes from its dominant position – as one observer put it, *“the price increases happen simply because they can”*. Nonetheless, the data shows pricing has been a smaller contributor relative to volume.

- **Volume Growth – Existing and New Customers:** The bulk of CargoWise’s growth (~60–70% of it) has come from **more usage** of the platform. This includes (1) **Global customer rollouts and seat expansion** at large forwarders, and (2) **new customer acquisitions and broader module adoption** in the existing customer base. WiseTech’s investor presentation delineated about **10 percentage points** of annual growth from the continued rollout at large global forwarders (i.e. as big customers add users, branches, and transactions). Another **~7 pp** came from other new and existing customers expanding their use of CargoWise (for example, a mid-tier forwarder signing on, or a current customer deploying CargoWise in a new country). Combined, this indicates roughly **17 pp of growth (~51% of total)** was due to higher transaction volumes/user counts from customer expansion. A further **~3 pp** of growth was attributed to general **market growth** (i.e. the rise in global freight volumes over time). This means that as world trade and shipment counts grew, CargoWise’s usage naturally rose even without adding new customers – contributing about 3 points (~9%) to growth, on average. In summary, **organically increasing volume (seats and transactions)** – through winning new customers and growing with existing ones – has been the primary engine of CargoWise revenue expansion.

- **New Modules & Upsell:** An important component bridging price and volume is the introduction of **new modules (products)** which existing customers then adopt (effectively an upsell, but also an increase in what they pay). WiseTech identifies “major new product releases” as adding about **5 percentage points** of annual growth over FY16–23. Examples include the launch of CargoWise Neo, the Warehouse suite, Landside Transport, and other modules that created new revenue streams. When a customer that was only using, say, forwarding and customs decides to start using a new module like Warehouse or Rates, their subscription spend increases (which is akin to volume growth in terms of usage breadth). This ~5 pp (~15% of growth) from new modules can be seen as **cross-selling effect** – it’s not an across-the-board price hike, but rather customers buying more CargoWise functionality. It’s a testament to WiseTech’s strategy of expanding the platform’s scope (and thus wallet share per customer). For instance, when CargoWise released its Warehouse module and large clients adopted it, that generated incremental revenue on top of existing seat licenses. Similarly, the rollout of customs modules in new countries or the addition of regulatory tools (Compliance modules) contributed to growth in this category. 

- **Inorganic Growth:** Although not “organic,” it’s worth noting WiseTech also grew via acquisitions (which are then often integrated into CargoWise). Over the past decade, **acquired revenue** accounted for roughly **3 percentage points** of the annual growth on average. These are “tuck-in” deals (e.g. for rate management, domestic transport systems, etc.) that immediately add to revenue. However, even excluding M&A, CargoWise’s **organic recurring revenue** growth remained very strong (e.g. +37% organically in FY23). For the purpose of this breakdown, we focus on organic drivers (price vs volume), but one can consider that a small single-digit percentage of growth came from expanding the platform via acquisitions (e.g. the Blume Global and Envase deals in 2023 added some revenue).

From the above, we see that roughly **20–22 pp (about two-thirds)** of CargoWise’s growth was volume-driven (including new customers, usage expansion, and general freight market trends). Another **5 pp (~15%)** was pure price increase, and **5 pp (~15%)** was effectively upselling new modules (which can be viewed as a mix of volume and pricing – new functionality leading customers to spend more). The remaining small portion (~9%) came from acquisitions. In simpler terms, **WiseTech’s growth has been fueled more by ‘more seats and more modules’ than by charging higher prices**. Even in the latest year reported, this pattern holds: In FY2023, CargoWise’s organic growth of +37% was attributed to new product releases and price increases (together contributing a part of that), but principally to existing customer growth and rollouts. WiseTech’s ability to land large global customers and then expand their usage (adding departments, geographies, and modules) has been the core driver of sustained high growth. Pricing actions – while not insignificant – have been supplementary. This balance is important for investors and customers alike: it indicates that CargoWise’s revenue expansion is coming from a larger user base and deeper penetration (a healthy, sustainable trend) rather than over-reliance on price hikes. Going forward, WiseTech has indicated it will continue modest annual price rises (often pegged to inflation), but the lion’s share of growth is expected to come from **volume**: signing more global rollouts, selling more modules (like Landside, Neo, customs in new regions), and underlying logistics activity growth.

In conclusion, WiseTech’s CargoWise platform has achieved strong growth by **delivering more value (and hence capturing more usage)** from the logistics industry, while also leveraging its pricing power judiciously. Logistics providers adopting CargoWise are seeing productivity and scope benefits that encourage them to expand usage to new modules and geographies – this is the engine of volume growth. Meanwhile, WiseTech has maintained a pricing strategy that increases revenues per user in line with added functionality. The result is a compound growth profile with ~*20–25%/yr from volume* and ~*5–10%/yr from price* on average, combining to deliver 30%+ annual revenue growth. This mix underscores the robustness of CargoWise’s model: growth is not dependent on just raising prices, but largely on the platform becoming more and more embedded in the global supply chain.
