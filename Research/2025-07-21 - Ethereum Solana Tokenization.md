# Asset Tokenization on Ethereum vs. Solana (2024–2025) – A Comprehensive Analysis

## Introduction and Overview of Asset Tokenization (2024–2025)

Asset tokenization – the process of creating blockchain-based tokens that represent ownership of real-world assets (RWAs) – has emerged as one of the fastest-growing use cases in crypto over the past 12–18 months. In 2024 alone, the on-chain tokenized RWA market (excluding stablecoins) nearly doubled, reaching about **$15.2 billion by December 2024**. By mid-2025, that figure surpassed **$24 billion** (excluding stablecoins), marking a **380% increase from 2022 levels**. (Including fiat-backed stablecoins – essentially tokenized cash – the total tokenized asset market is over $200B, though this analysis will focus on non-cash assets like securities, real estate, and commodities.) Industry projections are optimistic: **10–30% of global assets could be tokenized by 2030–2034**, bridging trillions of dollars from traditional finance into blockchain networks. This trend represents a fundamental shift, positioning RWAs as a bridge between the ~$400 trillion traditional asset markets and the on-chain economy. 

Several factors are driving this tokenization boom. High interest rates in 2024–2025 made yield-bearing RWAs (e.g. tokenized **U.S. Treasuries**) especially attractive on-chain, while blockchain’s 24/7 markets and fractional ownership broadened access to traditionally illiquid assets like **real estate** and **private credit**. Major institutions have moved beyond pilot projects and are now launching production-scale offerings: for example, **BlackRock, JPMorgan, Franklin Templeton, Apollo Global Management**, and **UBS** all rolled out tokenized asset initiatives in 2024–2025. Governments and regulators are also increasingly supportive, seeing tokenization as a way to modernize financial markets. The past year has thus seen convergence of technological maturity, institutional adoption, and regulatory clarity that bolsters the fundamental investment case for blockchain networks enabling asset tokenization. 

**Ethereum and Solana** have emerged as two prominent public blockchains hosting tokenized assets, each with distinct strengths. **Ethereum** is currently the dominant RWA platform by value, **hosting ~$7.5B (≈59% market share) across 335 tokenized products as of mid-2025**. It enjoys deep liquidity (over $130B in stablecoins on Ethereum) and a decade-long security track record that inspires institutional confidence. **Solana**, while a later entrant, is a high-performance challenger that gained momentum in 2024–2025 – its RWA market grew over **200% in the first half of 2025**. Solana now hosts around **$350M in tokenized assets** (≈3% market share) across a smaller number of products, but it offers ultra-fast settlement and negligible fees, appealing to use cases that demand speed and low cost. In the sections below, we provide a structured analysis of Ethereum vs. Solana in the asset tokenization space – examining institutional and retail perspectives, regulatory and technological developments, market adoption trends, and key tokenization projects on each network.

## Ethereum: The Institutional Standard for Tokenization

Ethereum has positioned itself as the **“institutional standard”** for asset tokenization. As the most mature smart contract platform, it benefits from network effects: a large base of users and developers, hundreds of DeFi applications, and integration into enterprise workflows. **Major financial institutions consistently choose Ethereum** for issuing tokenized assets, leveraging its broad user network and liquidity. For example, in March 2024 **BlackRock launched its inaugural tokenized fund (the BUIDL fund) on Ethereum**, offering exposure to short-term U.S. Treasuries. Within weeks, BUIDL became the world’s largest on-chain U.S. Treasury product – reaching about **$657M** AUM in its first six weeks and later growing past **$1–2.7B** as more investors poured in. BlackRock’s choice of Ethereum underscores the trust in its security and operational stability. As *RedStone* research notes, Ethereum’s **decade-long track record and deep liquidity (e.g. ~$131B in stablecoins)** give it a “durable moat” for tokenized finance despite newer alternatives.

From a **technological perspective**, Ethereum’s recent and upcoming upgrades aim to support its growing RWA ecosystem. The move to Proof-of-Stake in 2022 (the Merge) improved energy efficiency and set the stage for scalability upgrades. In late 2023, the network implemented proto-danksharding (EIP-4844, part of the “Surge” roadmap) to eventually enable **lower fees via Layer-2 rollups** – an important development, as Ethereum’s on-chain gas fees remain relatively high for retail users. While Ethereum can currently process ~15–30 transactions per second, scaling efforts and Layer-2 networks (Arbitrum, Optimism, zkSync, etc.) are gradually increasing throughput and reducing costs. Additionally, Ethereum’s open development culture has produced token standards to facilitate compliant asset tokens – for instance, **ERC-3643**, an Ethereum standard tailored for securities/RWAs that supports regulated transfers and identity checks. Compliance-focused frameworks by companies like **Securitize** build on Ethereum’s smart contracts to embed KYC/AML rules at the token level (using protocols like ERC-1404/1400 or Securitize’s DS Protocol). These features allow Ethereum-based RWA tokens to enforce whitelisted investors and geographic restrictions automatically, satisfying regulatory requirements while remaining interoperable with DeFi.

### Institutional Adoption on Ethereum

Over the past year, Ethereum has seen **accelerating institutional adoption** in tokenization. Beyond BlackRock’s fund, other marquee examples include: 

- **UBS** – In November 2024, UBS Asset Management launched *“UBS Tokenize”* and issued **uMINT**, a tokenized money market fund built on Ethereum. This lets clients access a conservative USD cash management fund via Ethereum tokens. UBS developed uMINT as part of Singapore’s **Project Guardian** pilot, working with regulators to ensure compliance. UBS noted growing investor appetite for tokenized assets across asset classes and collaborated with MAS (Monetary Authority of Singapore) to pioneer this regulated offering.

- **Franklin Templeton** – The $1.4T asset manager expanded its *Franklin OnChain U.S. Government Money Fund* (FOBXX) in 2024 by making its tokenized shares (BENJI tokens) available on multiple networks, including Ethereum and Polygon. By October 2024 the fund reached **$410M** AUM. This SEC-registered fund invests in U.S. Treasuries and allows qualified holders to transfer shares peer-to-peer on-chain. Franklin’s initiative – begun on Stellar and bridged to EVM chains – demonstrates cross-chain flexibility but highlights that **Ethereum’s ecosystem (e.g. Polygon, Base L2)** is crucial for distribution.

- **MakerDAO’s RWA Program** – MakerDAO (issuer of the DAI stablecoin on Ethereum) has incorporated real-world asset exposure into its reserves at significant scale. By mid-2025, Maker (renamed as *“Sky”* in its endgame plan) had deployed **over $3.5B** from its DAI treasury into RWA holdings, including short-term bonds and private credit, in order to back DAI with yield-generating traditional assets. In 2022–2023 Maker famously onboarded a **$40M tokenized bond from Société Générale** and invested in pools of loans via partners like **Centrifuge**. This trend continued into 2024, with Maker’s on-chain lending arm (Spark) tapping tokenized Treasuries and loans. Maker’s RWA strategy shows how **Ethereum’s DeFi protocols are integrating tokenized assets**: regulated trustees hold real assets off-chain while ERC-20 tokens representing those assets are used as collateral on-chain. This blend of DeFi and TradFi on Ethereum is unlocking new sources of liquidity and yield.

- **Other Institutional Highlights** – *BlackRock’s* BUIDL fund on Ethereum (noted above) crossed **$1 billion in assets within months**. *JPMorgan* executed a pilot in late 2022 for tokenized yen and Singapore dollar deposits on a modified Ethereum Layer-2 (Project Guardian Phase I), and in 2024 it continued exploring on-chain bonds and collateral. *Fidelity, WisdomTree, and VanEck* have each launched tokenized funds or ETFs on Ethereum or Ethereum-based networks – e.g. WisdomTree’s **tokenized gold (WTGOLD)** on Ethereum and VanEck’s **tokenized Treasury fund (VBILL)**. Even traditional exchanges began experiments: **Switzerland’s SIX** and Germany’s **Deutsche Börse** have utilized Ethereum or Ethereum-based permissioned chains for bond issuance and trading pilots. These examples underscore that Ethereum is often the **default choice for institutions** due to its robust infrastructure and acceptance. Importantly, a broad range of asset types are now on Ethereum: from **tokenized private credit** (the largest segment of RWAs, ~$14B by mid-2025) to **commodity tokens** like gold (e.g. Paxos’ PAXG, market cap $500M+) and even **tokenized equities** (e.g. **Securitize’s** tokens for private equity funds by KKR and Hamilton Lane issued on Ethereum in 2023). 

Ethereum’s credibility has even led to new initiatives to maintain its lead. In January 2025, a consortium led by former Wall Street veterans launched **“Etherealize,”** an outreach initiative to position Ethereum as the “**digital oil**” powering the tokenized financial system. This represents a strategic push to coordinate Ethereum’s traditionally diffuse community towards meeting institutional needs. While Ethereum’s decentralized governance can make rapid business development challenging, its vast community of developers and **largest DeFi liquidity pools** give it an entrenched advantage. At ~**76,000 RWA holders** (mid-2025), Ethereum also has the widest distribution of tokenized asset investors – ranging from large institutions to crypto-savvy retail users around the world.

### Retail Adoption and Use Cases on Ethereum

From the retail investor perspective, Ethereum offers access to many tokenized assets, though often with some barriers (like KYC or high gas fees). Notably, **fractional real estate** has been a pioneering retail use case on Ethereum. **RealT**, a platform launched in 2019, uses Ethereum to sell tokenized equity in rental properties (primarily in the U.S.), enabling investors globally to buy fractions of houses for as little as ~$50. As of 2024, RealT had tokenized **200+ properties** on Ethereum, with token holders earning proportional rental income. This democratizes real estate investing – a user can own 0.1% of a house and trade that ownership on secondary markets. RealT’s tokens adhere to U.S. securities exemptions (Reg D/Reg S), meaning investors undergo KYC and non-US retail can participate, showing how Ethereum’s public network can handle compliance for retail-facing RWAs. 

Another retail-friendly RWA on Ethereum is **Pax Gold (PAXG)**. Each PAXG token represents one troy ounce of physical gold stored in London vaults, and holders can redeem tokens for real gold bars. Launched in 2019, PAXG grew to be the largest tokenized commodity with **over $500M market cap in 2024**, held by a mix of retail users and institutions as a convenient way to get gold exposure on-chain. Its success highlighted retail appetite for asset-backed tokens that are easier to transfer than the underlying asset (in this case, gold bullion). Other examples of **commodity tokenization** on Ethereum include Digix’s gold token and tokenized **diamonds** (e.g. Diamond Standard’s tokens).

Despite these opportunities, **Ethereum’s high transaction fees have been a pain point for retail**. During busy periods in 2024, a single ERC-20 token transfer could cost a few dollars in gas – a significant cost if one is investing only $100. This has driven some retail-focused RWA projects to seek scaling solutions. RealT, for instance, migrated a lot of activity to an Ethereum sidechain (xDai/Gnosis Chain) to facilitate cheaper transactions for its users while still using Ethereum’s security model for finality. In the broader ecosystem, many tokenized assets targeting retail are launching on Ethereum **Layer-2 networks**. For example, in 2023–24, multiple startups (like **Backed Finance and Matrixdock**) issued tokenized securities (stocks or bonds) on **Polygon or Arbitrum**, which inherit Ethereum’s security but offer lower fees for end-users. 

Overall, Ethereum’s retail RWA adoption is growing but tends to involve **niche platforms** and early adopters. The average retail user still faces user-experience challenges (managing wallets, paying gas, passing KYC on multiple portals). However, progress is being made: **account abstraction (ERC-4337)** is enabling more user-friendly “smart wallets,” and major fintechs like **PayPal** have integrated Ethereum for their own stablecoin (PYUSD) – signaling a future where users may interact with tokenized assets on Ethereum via familiar interfaces. As infrastructure improves, Ethereum’s enormous DeFi ecosystem could become readily accessible to retail participants in RWAs. Already, once a tokenized asset is issued on Ethereum, **DeFi protocols can integrate it** – for example, RWA tokens (with proper permissions) can be used in lending platforms or AMMs, providing liquidity and yield opportunities that benefit retail investors. This composability is a unique strength of Ethereum: even regulated assets can potentially plug into on-chain money markets (under whitelisted conditions) to amplify their utility. 

In summary, **Ethereum’s fundamental investment case is bolstered by its role as the primary platform for tokenizing real-world assets**. High-profile institutional adoption reinforces Ethereum’s position at the center of on-chain finance, which in turn drives demand for Ether (ETH) for transaction fees and staking. At the same time, Ethereum’s community continues to address its challenges (scalability, cost) through technical upgrades and Layer-2 expansion. If even a single-digit percentage of the $400T traditional asset market eventually moves on-chain, Ethereum stands to capture a substantial portion given its head start – a prospect that underpins a bullish long-term outlook (absent any explicit price predictions). However, Ethereum is no longer the only game in town for RWAs, as we’ll see with Solana’s rise.

## Solana: The High-Performance Challenger for Tokenization

Solana has reemerged as a serious challenger in the tokenization arena, particularly in 2024 and 2025. After navigating a difficult 2022 (which saw network outages and the FTX collapse impacting ecosystem sentiment), Solana refocused on its core strengths: **high throughput, low latency, and ultra-low fees**. The network’s capability to process **1,000+ transactions per second in real conditions** with **400 millisecond block times** and gas fees around **$0.0001–$0.01** is a key differentiator. These features make Solana naturally suited for tokenized asset use cases that involve **high-volume trading or real-time settlement**, such as **foreign exchange, money markets, or retail equity trading**. By contrast, executing such high-frequency transactions on Ethereum would be prohibitively expensive and slow for most users. Solana’s technical architecture (a single global state without sharding) ensures that all assets and programs reside on one shard, simplifying composability and **avoiding the fragmentation** that can occur in multi-chain or sharded environments. This “single source of truth” design is attractive for financial institutions who require consistent, fast execution and prefer not to deal with complex cross-chain bridges for liquidity. 

### Technology and Network Upgrades

Several **technological developments** have strengthened Solana’s appeal for tokenization. First, the network dramatically improved its stability in 2023–2024. Software upgrades (like QUIC-based networking and localized fee markets) and better node management reduced the outage incidents that plagued Solana in early 2022. By mid-2024, Solana demonstrated much **improved reliability** – a necessary condition to win back institutional trust. Furthermore, **Jump Crypto’s “Fire Dancer”** project is set to deliver a second independent Solana validator client (expected in 2024–2025), which will not only bolster decentralization but also significantly boost performance (preliminary tests demonstrated up to 600k TPS in a lab setting). Having multiple node implementations reduces the risk of single points of failure in Solana’s software, addressing prior concerns about network halts.

Crucially for RWAs, Solana’s developers have introduced **built-in compliance and custody features** at the protocol level. The **Solana Program Library (SPL) token standard** now supports *“token extensions”* that can enforce **transfer restrictions, freezes, and role-based controls** on assets. For example, an issuer can mint an SPL token with an authority that can **freeze or claw back tokens**, enabling compliance with court orders or regulatory requirements. Solana also supports *“confidential tokens”* where transaction amounts can be hidden (using encryption via ElGamal), potentially useful for privacy in financial trades. Additionally, Solana’s single-shard execution allows smart contracts to easily check identities or whitelists on-chain before allowing a token transfer – thus automating **“programmable compliance”** for securities. As a result, Solana can offer institutions a robust **RWA protocol** for secure and compliant tokenization, where asset legitimacy and investor qualifications are verified within the system. This contrasts with Ethereum’s approach where such compliance is usually handled by external smart contract logic or off-chain services. Solana’s more **vertically integrated approach** (layer-1 support for permissioned environments and identity) has started to resonate with enterprises looking for turnkey solutions.

To cater to enterprise users, the Solana ecosystem has also embraced **“permissioned instances”** of its technology. For instance, projects like **Sphere** and others (with Solana Foundation support) are exploring **Solana Enterprise (SPE)** chains – essentially private or permissioned Solana networks that can connect to the public Solana for liquidity. In 2025, Solana announced a partnership with **R3 Corda**, a leading enterprise DLT platform used by banks. This integration aims to let institutions use Corda’s private ledgers for internal processes, while **tapping Solana’s public network for broader liquidity or settlement**. It’s a hybrid model offering “the best of both worlds” – banks keep sensitive workflows in a permissioned environment but settle or list assets on Solana’s open market for wider access. Such innovations signal to institutional clients that Solana is willing to meet them halfway on regulatory and operational needs.
