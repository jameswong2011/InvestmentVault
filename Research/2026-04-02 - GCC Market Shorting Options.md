# GCC Market Shorting Options

I’m checking what is actually tradable now, focusing on listed GCC-wide equity or bond instruments that can truly be shorted, and separating those from rougher country-level or OTC proxies.

---

I’ve confirmed at least one direct GCC-wide equity future—ICE’s Micro MSCI GCC NTR USD contract—while the bond side still looks weaker, likely limited to ETFs or OTC credit hedges.

---

The remaining questions are practical rather than structural: the broad-GCC ETF and GCC government-bond ETF I found both look small, and country-level vehicles may be easier to short than the purer regional basket. I’m also checking whether a “short GCC” hedge is even directionally clean in a war scenario, since an oil spike could offset losses in parts of the Gulf.

---

Yes, but the menu is limited and the direct GCC-wide products are thinner than you might expect.

**Best direct GCC-wide future:** ICE Futures Abu Dhabi lists the **Micro MSCI GCC NTR USD Index Future (GAC)**. It is cash-settled on the MSCI GCC Countries Net Total Return USD Index, with contract size **US$10 × index**. ICE also lists micro **Qatar** and **UAE** index futures. It tracks the **MSCI GCC Countries ex Select Securities Index**, covering **Bahrain, Kuwait, Oman, Qatar, Saudi Arabia and the UAE**. DWS shows **ISIN IE00BQXKVQ19** and **TER 0.65%**, and MSCI lists **XGLF** as an index-linked ETP for that benchmark. **Bond side:** the GCC-wide listed fixed-income product I found is **Janus Henderson GCC Sovereign USD Bond Core UCITS ETF (TGCC/TGGT)**, which tracks the **ICE Gulf Cooperation Council Government Bond ex-144a Index**. Janus showed about **$18.2 million AUM** on March 19, 2026, and explicitly flags **liquidity risk**. If Saudi-only duration/credit is acceptable, **SPDR J.P. Morgan Saudi Arabia Aggregate Bond UCITS ETF (KSAB)** is much larger at about **$223.9 million** total fund assets. The main caution is that **“short GCC” is not a pure “regional war destroys everything” trade**. The **MSCI GCC Countries Combined Index** is about **55.63% Saudi Arabia**, **23.03% UAE**, **9.7% Qatar**, and **58.54% financials**, with only **9.14% energy**. So you are mostly short Saudi/UAE banks plus some oil-linked names, not a clean disaster basket. Recent market action also shows mixed behavior rather than straight-line collapse: Reuters reported Saudi Arabia’s benchmark **rose 0.8%** during a recent regional escalation even as Dubai and Abu Dhabi fell, with higher oil prices and export rerouting cited as support. citeturn168456view0turn887324view1turn279452view0
