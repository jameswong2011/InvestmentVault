# Silicon Photonics Supply Chain: 2026–2030 Investment Outlook

## Executive Summary  
Silicon photonics is on the cusp of a significant growth surge as data centers and AI infrastructure shift from copper to optical interconnects. The driving force is the so-called “copper wall” – physical limits of electrical links (resistance, heat, bandwidth) that waste energy and bottleneck modern AI clusters. Optical links, using light instead of electrons, can carry far more data with lower latency and power, making them essential for scaling next-generation networks. By 2026, over half of all optical transceiver sales are expected to use silicon photonics, up from just one-third in 2024. Industry forecasts project the global silicon photonics market to continue its ~20–25% CAGR, reaching the multi-billion dollar range by the late 2020s. This boom is underpinned by the exponential bandwidth demand of AI and cloud applications, with 200G per lane optical channels becoming mainstream by 2026–27 and paving the way for 800G and 1.6T transceivers. Co-packaged optics (CPO) – integrating photonics directly with switch/processor ASICs – is targeted for large-scale adoption around 2028–2030 to overcome the performance and energy limits of pluggable modules. 

Every layer of the silicon photonics supply chain stands to benefit. **Photonic chip design** houses are delivering the intellectual property and specialized circuits that drive these optical engines. **Fabrication (foundries)** are rapidly expanding capacity on mature nodes optimized for photonic integrated circuits (PICs). **Packaging & assembly** specialists (OSATs) are reinventing how chips and lasers are aligned and integrated at scale. **Testing** equipment providers are addressing new challenges in optical alignment and high-volume photonics test to ensure yield and reliability. **Transceiver module developers** – both established optical component firms and startups – are racing to supply 800G/1600G modules for cloud providers and to prototype CPO engines. Finally, **systems integrators** (network equipment and AI system vendors) are forging partnerships across the supply chain to embed silicon photonics in switches, servers, and HPC clusters, seeking competitive advantage in performance per watt. ## Photonic Chip Design & IP Innovation  
Silicon photonics design is a critical upstream segment, providing the **intellectual property and specialized chip designs** that fuel the entire supply chain. This layer includes companies designing photonic integrated circuits (PICs) – such as optical modulators, switches, and light-based computing elements – often in fabless models. With Moore’s Law slowing, the industry is investing heavily in novel photonic architectures to break through the bandwidth and energy limits of electrical chips. From optical I/O chiplets that replace traditional copper interconnects, to photonic computing processors that use light for AI computations, these innovators are poised for rapid growth as silicon photonics moves from niche to mainstream.

**Leading Companies:**  
- **Ayar Labs (Private)** – A Silicon Valley startup focusing on optical I/O chiplets (TeraPHY) that enable chips to communicate using light instead of copper. Ayar’s technology eliminates electrical I/O bottlenecks by integrating optical links directly onto packages. The company has strong strategic backing – its recent $155 million Series D was led by semiconductor incumbents (AMD, Intel, NVIDIA) and others, valuing Ayar above $1 billion. This reflects industry confidence that optical chiplet interconnects will be *essential* for large-scale AI and HPC systems. Ayar has partnered with Intel and Hewlett Packard Enterprise on demonstrations of optical-enabled processors, and its chips are fabricated by GlobalFoundries in the U.S., highlighting an advantage in supply chain integration. As generative AI drives “AI-native” datacenter designs, Ayar Labs is well positioned with a unique technology to relieve networking bottlenecks. Investors view Ayar as a pure-play bet on the silicon photonics adoption curve in data centers.  

- **Lightmatter (Private)** – A leading photonic computing startup building photonic processors and interconnects for AI datacenters. Lightmatter’s photonic chips use light to perform matrix multiplication and to move data between chips at ultra-high bandwidth, dramatically improving performance-per-watt for AI workloads. The company’s promise and traction are evidenced by a massive **$400 million Series D raise in late 2024 at a $4.4 billion valuation**, backed by investors like T. Rowe Price, Fidelity, and GV (Alphabet). This brought total funding to ~$850 million, aimed at scaling manufacturing and deployment of its photonic chips in customer data centers. Lightmatter’s first large clusters using its photonic interconnected systems are expected online by 2025. With a former NVIDIA networking executive as its CFO and an IPO envisioned, Lightmatter is seen as an emerging **market leader in photonic AI accelerators and networking**. Its strategy is to be vendor-agnostic – providing optical interconnect tech that can plug into any vendor’s AI chips (NVIDIA, Intel, AMD, etc.). If successful, Lightmatter could become a core supplier of photonic engines to hyperscalers racing to deploy bigger and more efficient AI infrastructure.

- **Celestial AI (Private)** – A fast-rising startup behind the *Photonic Fabric™* optical interconnect platform. Celestial’s technology targets the “memory wall” and “network wall” in AI computing by using light to move data between processors and memory with extremely high throughput. The company has attracted *very large funding* – over **$250 million in Series C** rounds (C and C1) in 2023–24, including investments from BlackRock, Fidelity, Koch Industries, and strategic backers like AMD. Its post-money valuation reportedly reached $2.5 billion, reflecting high expectations. Celestial AI’s solution can be integrated into AI servers to optically connect GPUs/TPUs and memory, reducing communication latency and boosting utilization of expensive AI chips. With this capital, the company is accelerating product development and go-to-market to address hyperscale data center needs. Celestial AI is noteworthy for investors due to its deep bench of backers and its focus on interconnects – a critical enabler as AI models scale and require distributed compute across optical fabrics.

- **OpenLight (Private)** – A unique player offering an *open silicon photonics platform* with integrated lasers. Spun out of Synopsys in 2022, OpenLight provides a process design kit (PDK) and manufacturing capability for photonic ASICs (“PASICs”), allowing customers to design custom silicon photonic chips with **heterogeneously integrated indium phosphide lasers and amplifiers on silicon**. In August 2025 OpenLight raised $34 million in Series A funding from investors including HPE (via its Juniper Networks unit) and others. Its model is part design-house and part foundry-enabler: OpenLight works with Tower Semiconductor as the fab partner and has validated its laser-integrated PDK on Tower’s process. This gives smaller companies a fast track to build photonic chips with lasers, modulators, detectors, etc., without building their own fabs. OpenLight’s early customers span networking equipment firms and even hyperscalers, and production chips are expected by end of 2025. For the supply chain, OpenLight fills an important niche by **democratizing photonics design**, and for investors it represents an enabling pick-and-shovel play as more companies seek to develop custom optical engines.

- **Intel (Public: INTC)** – As a pioneering giant in silicon photonics, Intel continues to be highly relevant at the design stage. Intel was one of the first to commercialize silicon photonics (launching 100G pluggable modules years ago) and has developed a rich IP portfolio (high-speed modulators, germanium photodetectors, etc.). Despite corporate restructuring, Intel maintains its internal silicon photonics fabs and R&D, focusing on both data center optics and new areas like co-packaged optics for its switches and future server CPUs. Intel’s approach is vertically integrated – designing and **manufacturing its own photonic transceiver chips**, which it uses in products and sometimes sells on the merchant market. For example, Intel’s 800G pluggable modules and its future on-package optical I/O solutions stem from this in-house design capability. Intel’s market positioning is both as a supplier and a consumer of silicon photonics: any investor evaluating the ecosystem should note that Intel’s commitment signals confidence in the technology’s necessity for scaling compute clusters. While not a pure-play photonics stock, Intel’s moves (and partnerships – e.g. with Ayar Labs for optical chiplets) often validate the broader trend and can create opportunities for its partners and suppliers.

*Other notable design-stage players include **IBM Research**, which has long innovated in silicon photonic circuitry (e.g. optical interconnects for mainframes and quantum computing networks), and startups like **Luminous Computing** and **Lightelligence**, also developing photonic AI chips. These firms, along with university spin-offs, contribute to a healthy pipeline of photonic design innovation, though the ones highlighted above are among those best positioned for 2026–2030 growth.* 

## Silicon Photonics Fabrication (Foundries)  
High-volume manufacturing of photonic chips is a cornerstone of the supply chain. Unlike traditional logic chips, silicon photonics leverages mature CMOS nodes (often 45nm to 90nm processes on silicon-on-insulator) with added process steps for optical structures. This means **specialized foundry capacity** is needed – and leading semiconductor foundries worldwide are now racing to add that capacity, eyeing the coming wave of optical transceiver and CPO demand. The competitiveness of each foundry lies in its process IP (low-loss waveguides, efficient modulators, laser integration techniques) and its ability to scale production with high yield. Investors are focusing on which foundries will capture the surge in volume for optics, similar to how TSMC captured mobile chip growth in the last decade.

**Leading Companies:**  
- **GlobalFoundries (NYSE: GFS)** – GF has made one of the **boldest commitments to silicon photonics**, aiming to corner this market. In November 2025, GlobalFoundries announced the acquisition of Advanced Micro Foundry (AMF) in Singapore, a pure-play photonics foundry, propelling GF to the #1 position globally for silicon photonics manufacturing. AMF had built a successful business with 300+ photonics customers and advanced capabilities (including integrating indium phosphide lasers onto silicon). With AMF, GF gains a proven 200 mm production line (to be upgraded to 300 mm) and a wealth of photonics IP, which GF will leverage alongside its existing 300 mm photonics line in New York. GF’s “Fotonix” platform – a monolithic photonics+RF CMOS process – is already an industry standard for fabless firms that lack their own fabs. The company expects **over $1 billion in silicon photonics revenue by 2030**, and is marketing itself as a U.S.-friendly, end-to-end source for optical chip production. With geopolitical factors making supply chain security important, GF’s expansion (offering a “China-free” photonics supply chain) could attract North American and European customers. For investors, GF’s photonics push represents a growth engine on top of its existing specialty semiconductor business – they are effectively positioning to be the TSMC of photonics in high-volume “feature-rich” nodes.

- **TSMC (TWSE: 2330 / NYSE: TSM)** – The world’s largest semiconductor foundry is also actively involved in silicon photonics, albeit more quietly. TSMC’s core business is leading-edge logic, but it has been investing in photonics as a complementary offering. Notably, TSMC has taken a lead in **co-packaged optics (CPO) development**, working with networking leaders like NVIDIA and Broadcom on optical integration for next-gen switches. TSMC views CPO as “another packaging technology” to offer clients, aligning with its advanced packaging services (e.g. TSMC’s InFO and CoWoS are analogous in chiplet integration). In practice, TSMC can provide front-end fabrication of silicon photonic engines as well as back-end integration of those engines with ASICs. For example, TSMC’s involvement in NVIDIA’s 2025 CPO demonstrations suggests it fabricated the optical engines or at least handled the packaging substrate. TSMC is also rumored to offer photonic process nodes (e.g. a 300 mm silicon photonics platform) to high-volume customers, though details are not public. Given TSMC’s manufacturing prowess, if demand for optical I/O on chips explodes, TSMC will capture a significant share – especially from customers who already use TSMC for logic and want tight integration. From an investment perspective, TSMC’s photonics foray reinforces its role as a one-stop-shop for high-performance chip needs, though photonics remains a small part of its revenue in the near term.

- **Tower Semiconductor (NASDAQ: TSEM)** – A specialty foundry (based in Israel, recently courted by Intel for acquisition) that has *quietly become a significant silicon photonics fab*. Tower operates multiple 200 mm fabs (in California, Texas, Israel) and a 300 mm fab in Japan, all supporting silicon photonics processes. The company is expanding aggressively: Tower plans to **double SiPho capacity by end of 2025 and triple it by mid-2026** as part of a $300 million investment. It even expects to double its photonics revenue in 2026, challenging GF for the top spot. Tower’s differentiator is its **open-process model** and materials expertise. It allows customers to integrate high-speed SiGe (silicon germanium) BiCMOS electronics with silicon photonic waveguides on the same chip. This is crucial because at 1.6 Tb/s and beyond, pure silicon modulators struggle with speed, whereas SiGe can drive modulators at 100+ GHz. Tower recently announced a breakthrough in *wafer-scale laser bonding*, enabling lasers to be attached to photonic wafers in mass (reducing the cost of integrating lasers for 1.6T modules). With these innovations, Tower is seen as an **“artisan” foundry** offering flexible, high-performance photonics processes for customers who need more customization than GF or TSMC provide. For investors, Tower’s photonics business could be a strong growth story (even as its core CMOS business is steady) – it’s effectively carving out a high-value niche in the broader foundry market. News of any major Tier-1 customer wins (as hinted for its new CPO process) could be catalysts for Tower’s stock.

- **STMicroelectronics (NYSE: STM)** – Europe’s large semiconductor firm re-entered the silicon photonics market in early 2025 after having exited some years prior. STMicro has a history in photonics (it previously partnered on photonic projects) and now sees renewed opportunity as optical interconnects go mainstream. The company is reportedly leveraging its 300 mm fab in Crolles, France, to offer silicon photonics manufacturing, possibly targeting applications like optical networking and even sensors (ST is strong in automotive/industrial, where LiDAR and sensors using Si photonics could emerge). In 2025, STMicro announced partnerships to commercialize photonic technology (one example: teaming with Ayar Labs to evaluate optical I/O for European customers). While details are sparse, ST’s re-entry indicates **no major region wants to be left out** of photonics production – and the company could become a key supplier in the EU, supported by initiatives to build a European photonics supply chain. Investment-wise, STMicro’s photonics activity is one facet of its diversification (the company is also in power electronics, microcontrollers, etc.), but any significant contracts or progress in this area could add upside to its growth projections for late-decade.

- **Intel Foundry Services (IFS)** – In addition to Intel’s in-house use mentioned earlier, Intel’s fabs (especially its D1 facility in Oregon) have dedicated lines for silicon photonics. Intel historically built all its own transceiver chips, making it somewhat vertically integrated. As Intel opens up foundry services, it’s conceivable it may offer photonic manufacturing to select customers (particularly those in the U.S. requiring a domestic source). In any case, Intel’s onshore photonics fab capacity is a strategic asset. The Reuters report on Ayar Labs highlights that *GlobalFoundries’ New York fab and Intel are both strategic investors*, and Ayar’s chips are made at GF. This implies Intel’s photonics fab is currently more for internal use, with GF picking up external fabless business like Ayar. Going forward, if Intel’s own photonic product needs fluctuate, IFS could potentially take on foundry contracts in this domain (especially given government interest in U.S.-based photonics for defense and HPC). For now, Intel’s photonic fab presence mainly underscores the **demand for capacity** – multiple players are needed to meet industry volume requirements in coming years.

*In addition to these, regional foundries such as **SilTerra** (Malaysia, 200 mm photonics line, now part of DNeX/Foxconn), **CompoundTek** (Singapore, a smaller pure-play photonics foundry), and **SMIC** in China (which is reportedly developing indigenous photonic processes) are part of the global supply base. As Yole’s analysts note, China is scaling up domestic capabilities aggressively – with companies like HiSilicon, Shawcor (Hefei), and others building out photonic fabs – aiming to reduce reliance on Western foundries. This global competition will likely intensify, but from an investment perspective, the *leading foundry players (GF, TSMC, Tower, etc.) with proven technology and capacity are expected to capture the bulk of the high-value photonics manufacturing growth from 2026–2030.**

## Advanced Packaging & Assembly  
Packaging is often considered the **Achilles’ heel** of silicon photonics scaling. Attaching delicate optical fibers, aligning lasers to waveguides, and integrating photonic chips with electronic drivers require precision assembly techniques far beyond conventional semiconductor packaging. Historically, optical packaging was a manual, labor-intensive process (often “paying a PhD to glue fibers under a microscope” as one analyst quipped). To meet data center volumes, the industry needs automated, high-throughput packaging solutions – and this challenge is giving rise to both **innovations in packaging technology and the rise of specialist contractors**. The strategic importance of this layer cannot be overstated: if packaging yields or throughput falter, it becomes a bottleneck that slows product shipments. Investors are therefore keen on companies that can solve photonic packaging at scale, as they are linchpins for the entire supply chain’s growth.

**Leading Companies:**  
- **ASE Technology (NYSE: ASX)** – The world’s largest semiconductor assembly and test provider (OSAT), ASE has been at the forefront of adapting mainstream packaging for silicon photonics. ASE has developed processes for co-packaged optics (CPO) and optical *System-in-Package* solutions, leveraging its expertise in 2.5D/3D integration. In 2023, ASE demonstrated a CPO platform that integrates optical engines closely around an AI chip, significantly improving energy efficiency and bandwidth density for AI systems. The company highlights that shortening electrical paths via on-package optics can dramatically improve pJ/bit and $/Gbps metrics compared to pluggable modules. Key building blocks provided by ASE include high-accuracy flip-chip bonding for lasers and photonic dies, wafer-level optical testing (to ensure known-good die before assembly), and advanced substrates that accommodate optical fiber attach points. ASE’s involvement gives silicon photonics customers a high-volume assembly partner who can implement novel designs in a manufacturing-friendly way. As the industry moves toward CPO around 2028, ASE stands to gain from packaging those optical-chiplet-on-ASIC solutions. From an investment perspective, ASE’s diversification into photonics packaging adds to its growth drive
