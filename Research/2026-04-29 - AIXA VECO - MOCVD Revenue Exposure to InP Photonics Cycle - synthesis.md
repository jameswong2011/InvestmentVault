# 2026-04-29 — AIXA / VECO MOCVD Revenue Exposure to the InP Photonics Cycle

## Thesis Delta

Sizing exercise extending the equipment supply chain subsection added to [[Theses/LITE - Lumentum.md]] on 2026-04-28. **Aixtron's optoelectronics segment grew from 12% of equipment revenue (2024) to 23% (2025), with 65% of Q1 2026 order intake from the same segment** — the photonics cycle is happening in real time, not as a 2027-28 forward expectation. Aixtron is becoming a CPO/AI-datacom pure play. Veeco's MOCVD (Compound Semiconductor segment) was 9% of 2025 revenue, projected 12% in 2026 — the InP/CPO leverage exists but is dilutive within Veeco's broader mix (laser annealing for TSMC 2nm GAA and ion beam deposition for advanced packaging dominate).

**Implications**:
1. [[Theses/LITE - Lumentum.md]] equipment chain analysis upgrades — Aixtron is a far purer photonics CPO equipment play than the 2026-04-28 subsection captured. The ASML-for-InP framing understates the directness of exposure: Aixtron's revenue mix is moving toward 50%+ photonics within 12 months.
2. [[Sectors/Semiconductor Capital Equipment]] insight #6 (optical equipment enabler chain) underweights AIXA — currently captures FORM/AEHR/TSEM/GFS but stops at the test/foundry layer. MOCVD upstream is the equivalent leverage point and is missing.
3. AIXA is a candidate for a standalone thesis or addition to SEMICAP insight #6. VECO is not — the MOCVD exposure is too dilutive within VECO's mix.

## Summary

The user's question — "What proportion of Aixtron and Veeco's revenue is tied to MOCVD, and how big can it get if CPO takes off?" — has a striking answer asymmetry. Aixtron is essentially 100% MOCVD as a tool category (it sells nothing else), so the meaningful question is *application mix*. Optoelectronics (InP, GaAs laser MOCVD for AI datacom and CPO) was 12% of 2024 equipment revenue, jumped to 23% in 2025, and is on track for ~45-55% of 2026 equipment revenue based on Q1 2026 order intake of 65% optoelectronics. Aixtron's stated 2026 guidance of ~€560M (raised from €520M) reflects optoelectronics revenue more than doubling YoY (>€256M from ~€128M), with management explicitly characterizing the InP cycle as extending into 2027. By 2028 in the bull case, optoelectronics could reach 50-60% of total revenue.

Veeco's exposure is structurally smaller and more diluted. The Compound Semiconductor segment (which includes MOCVD plus power electronics, RF, and photonics applications) was $59.6M in 2025 (9% of total revenue) and is guided to ~$80M in 2026 (~12% of total). InP-specific MOCVD within that segment is roughly $20-30M annually. Veeco's main growth drivers are laser annealing (TSMC 2nm GAA, Samsung leading-edge logic) and ion beam deposition (advanced packaging) — both larger and faster-growing than MOCVD. The CPO take-off scenario adds maybe 3-6 percentage points to Veeco's revenue mix over 2025-2028, not a structural transformation.

The actionable conclusion: a long Lumentum position is implicitly long Aixtron in a way that the SEMICAP optical equipment enabler chain has not yet captured. Aixtron deserves a closer look — either as a standalone thesis or as an addition to SEMICAP insight #6. Veeco's MOCVD line is a net positive but does not move the needle on its overall thesis.

## Contradiction Check

**Counter 1: Power electronics could re-accelerate, diluting optoelectronics share at Aixtron.**
The SiC cycle bottomed in 2024-2025 on EV inventory correction. A SiC recovery in 2026-2027 (driven by new EV programs, BEV penetration in China, data center 48V power architectures) could push power electronics back to 50%+ of Aixtron mix, capping optoelectronics share at 30-40% even with absolute growth. Partially offset by Aixtron commentary that optoelectronics growth is overlapping with power electronics recovery, not substituting for it. The bull case for AIXA actually requires both segments growing simultaneously.

**Counter 2: Coherent's 6-inch InP success could disrupt MOCVD demand.**
If Coherent's 6-inch InP yields meaningfully exceed legacy 3-4 inch lines (already partially demonstrated), the industry transitions to higher-throughput single fabs rather than capacity duplication. Fewer MOCVD reactors per wafer of output. Conversely, the transition to 6-inch itself requires new MOCVD reactor purchases (the existing 3-4 inch installed base cannot be retrofitted), so the mid-term tailwind is unambiguous even if long-run unit volumes flatten. Aixtron's G10-AsP is explicitly positioned for the 6-inch transition.

**Counter 3: TFLN modulators and SiPh Mach-Zehnder could displace InP at 400G+.**
Thin-film lithium niobate modulators (Coherent's DFB-MZ, Marvell's TFLN) and silicon photonics Mach-Zehnder approaches threaten InP modulator dominance at 400G/3.2T. If these technologies win post-2028, MOCVD demand growth slows. However, even SiPh and TFLN modulators require external InP/GaAs CW laser sources, so the laser-source MOCVD demand persists. The threat is to EML modulators specifically, not to the broader photonics MOCVD TAM.

**Counter 4: Chinese InP fab buildout faces export controls.**
US/Dutch export controls on advanced semiconductor equipment have not yet explicitly targeted MOCVD, but the policy direction is restrictive. If Aixtron and Veeco are blocked from selling G10-AsP/Lumina+ tools to Chinese InP fabs (Yuanjie, Everbright), the Chinese contribution to MOCVD TAM ($40-80M cumulative) drops out. Aixtron is more exposed to this risk given European trade policy alignment and its larger China revenue base historically. Veeco has less Chinese MOCVD exposure.

**Counter 5: Aixtron's 18% EBIT margin in 2025 vs ~32% in 2024 signals operating deleverage risk.**
The optoelectronics ramp is happening into a cyclically depressed total revenue base. Aixtron historically operated at 25-35% EBIT margins; sub-20% margins represent real risk to consensus EPS even if revenue grows. Operating leverage works in reverse — fixed cost absorption deteriorated faster than mix improved. The 2026 guidance assumes margin recovery, but the path depends on power electronics not deteriorating further.

**Counter 6: The 65% Q1 2026 optoelectronics order intake is preliminary and not necessarily representative of full-year mix.**
Order intake skews quarter-to-quarter based on customer purchase timing. A single large LITE/COHR/Broadcom order could distort Q1 optics. The 23% full-year 2025 mix is the more reliable baseline. The full-year 2026 mix is more likely 35-50% than 65%, with 65% being a high-water mark for the order book skew.

## Open Questions / Follow-ups

1. **Direct customer disclosure**: Has Aixtron filed any 8-K-equivalent disclosure naming LITE, COHR, or Broadcom as G10-AsP customers? If yes, the implicit-long thesis becomes explicit. If not, the thesis depends on triangulating from the Nokia/SMART Photonics named customers plus the scale of LITE/COHR fab expansions.

2. **Aixtron historical operating leverage**: At what total revenue does Aixtron return to 30%+ EBIT margins? If €700M is the threshold, the 2028 bull case translates to material EPS upside. If €800M+ is needed, the operating leverage story is weaker.

3. **Veeco MOCVD market share within Compound Semi segment**: How much of Veeco's $60M Compound Semi is power electronics vs. RF vs. photonics? If photonics is <30% of segment, Veeco's CPO leverage is even more dilutive than the rough estimate above.

4. **Greensboro MOCVD vendor selection**: When LITE finalizes equipment selection for Greensboro, will Aixtron G10-AsP be the dominant tool, or will Veeco Lumina+ split the order? The split materially affects relative positioning.
