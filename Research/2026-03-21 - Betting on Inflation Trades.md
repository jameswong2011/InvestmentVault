# Betting on Inflation Trades

Assuming you want liquid cash securities rather than leverage or options, the cleanest menu is: inflation-linked bonds for a direct inflation view, floating-rate paper for a “rates stay higher” view, and commodity/energy funds for a commodity-shock view. Pricing matters: the U.S. 10-year breakeven inflation rate was 2.37% on 2026-03-19, and the Fed notes TIPS beat nominal Treasuries only if actual inflation ends up above inflation compensation, which also reflects risk and liquidity premiums. citeturn110674view0turn110674view1

- **Direct inflation-linked bonds.** U.S. Treasury Inflation-Protected Securities (TIPS) adjust principal and coupon payments with CPI, and Treasury sells them in 5-, 10-, and 30-year maturities. U.S.-listed ETF examples are **SCHP** (0.03% expense ratio, 6.6-year effective duration) and **STIP** (0.03% expense ratio, 2.41-year effective duration if you want less rate sensitivity). On the ASX, **UTIP** gives you AUD-hedged U.S. TIPS exposure (0.22% fee, 6.54-year modified duration), while **ILB** gives you Australian inflation-linked government bond exposure (0.18% annual management fee, 7.34-year effective duration). These are the cleanest retail instruments for a straight inflation view, but they are still rate-sensitive because duration is still meaningful. citeturn110674view1turn467870search1turn580304view1turn894408view2turn925699view2turn925699view3

- **Floating-rate notes and floating-rate bond ETFs.** U.S. Treasury floating-rate notes mature in 2 years and reset off the 13-week Treasury bill rate. A U.S.-listed ETF example is **USFR** (0.15% expense ratio, 0.02 effective duration). On the ASX, **QPON** says its income rises when interest rates rise; it charges 0.22%, has a 0.14-year modified duration, and its current all-in yield is 5.00%. This is a better trade when the real thesis is “inflation keeps central banks hawkish” rather than “CPI will surprise materially above breakevens.” citeturn834859view2turn894408view3turn797327view0

- **Commodity and energy funds.** For a commodity-led inflation spike, U.S. examples are **DBC** (broad commodity exposure, 0.89% expense ratio) and **XLE** (S&P 500 energy sector, 0.08% expense ratio). ASX examples are **BCOM** (broad basket across energy, grains, precious metals, industrial metals, softs, and livestock; 0.66%), **FUEL** (large global energy companies; 0.57%), and **OOO** if you specifically want crude oil. The important warning on **OOO** is that it tracks WTI crude **futures**, not spot oil, and the rolling of futures contracts can materially help or hurt returns. citeturn533426search6turn280299search0turn488878view0turn280299search1turn721383view6turn796307search2turn721383view5turn280299search2

- **Series I savings bonds.** If the goal is low-risk inflation protection for cash rather than a speculative macro trade, **I bonds** currently pay **4.03%** for bonds issued from Nov. 1, 2025 through Apr. 30, 2026, and the inflation component resets every 6 months. The limits matter: electronic purchases are capped at **$10,000 per SSN/EIN per calendar year**; you cannot cash out for **1 year**, and cashing out before **5 years** costs **3 months of interest**. citeturn834859view1turn834859view0turn636741search4

Bottom line: for a **clean inflation bet**, use **TIPS / UTIP / ILB**. For a **higher-for-longer rates** bet, use **Treasury FRNs / USFR / QPON**. For an **oil or raw-materials shock** bet, use **DBC / BCOM / XLE / FUEL / OOO**. The more advanced institutional expression is long TIPS and short nominal Treasuries, because ordinary Treasury notes pay a fixed rate of interest. citeturn110674view1turn834859view3

The key distinction is **realized inflation** versus **inflation already priced in**. On March 19, 2026, the market’s 5-year breakeven inflation rate was 2.61% and the 10-year breakeven was 2.37%. That means an inflation trade only really wins if realized inflation comes in above what the market had already discounted, or if the market revises expected inflation upward before you sell. The Fed also notes breakevens are not pure inflation expectations because they include inflation-risk and liquidity premiums. citeturn715946search1turn715946search2turn584932view11

For **U.S.-exchange-listed securities**, the closest thing to a direct realized-inflation payoff is a **TIPS ETF matched to your horizon**. Treasury says TIPS principal is adjusted with CPI-U, coupons are paid on the adjusted principal, and at maturity Treasury pays the original or adjusted principal, whichever is greater. Treasury’s regulations also note that the TIPS index ratio uses an approximate three-month lag, so inflation does not hit instantly. iShares’ term TIPS ETF lineup currently includes year-specific funds such as IBIC (2026), IBID (2027), IBIE (2028), IBIF (2029), and IBIG (2030), and iShares says these funds pay out their NAV in the maturity year and terminate with a final distribution. citeturn877980view0turn177815search1turn861372view0

The price sensitivity of a TIPS ETF before maturity is best thought of as: **short-run return ≈ CPI accrual + carry − duration × change in real yields**. That is why realized inflation helps, but rising real yields can still swamp it. Current effective durations are about **1.15 years for IBID**, **2.13 for LDRI**, **2.41 for STIP**, **6.45 for TIP**, and **6.6 for SCHP**. A rough rule is that a **1 percentage-point rise in real yields** means about **-1.2% for IBID**, **-2.1% for LDRI**, **-2.4% for STIP**, and about **-6.5% to -6.6% for TIP/SCHP**, before CPI indexation offsets part of the hit. So the cleaner your goal is “track realized inflation over the next 1–5 years,” the better **term TIPS ETFs** or **short-duration TIPS ETFs** fit; broad TIPS ETFs like TIP/SCHP are much more contaminated by real-rate risk. citeturn584932view0turn584932view1turn584932view2turn584932view5turn571626view0

A practical way to frame it is this: if you think inflation over the next two years will average materially above what the market is pricing, a **2027/2028 term TIPS ETF** or a **short-duration TIPS ETF** is a much more direct expression than TIP or SCHP. Broad TIPS ETFs can still lose money in an inflation scare if the market reprices real yields higher at the same time. citeturn715946search1turn584932view1turn584932view2turn584932view5turn861372view0

**FRN ETFs** such as **TFLO** and **USFR** are different. Treasury says floating-rate notes reset off the most recent 13-week Treasury bill; TFLO’s effective duration is **0.01** and USFR’s is **0.02**. So their **price** sensitivity to realized inflation is close to zero. They are really a bet that inflation keeps **short-term rates** elevated, which lifts future coupon income without much duration risk. They fit “higher for longer policy rates,” not “direct CPI upside.” citeturn822309view0turn584932view3turn584932view4

**Commodity ETFs** such as **PDBC** and **DBC** are not CPI-linked securities; they are commodity-futures strategies. Invesco describes PDBC as an actively managed ETF investing in commodity-linked futures and other derivatives across **energy, metals, and agriculture**, and describes DBC as a convenient way to invest in **commodity futures**. That means their sensitivity to realized inflation is **indirect**: they can respond very strongly if inflation is driven by oil, metals, or agricultural shocks, but much less if inflation comes from wages, rents, or services, and futures roll/collateral effects matter. They are better thought of as **commodity-shock hedges** than clean CPI hedges. citeturn345322search0turn345322search1turn345322search6

**XLE** is another step removed. State Street says it tracks the **Energy Select Sector Index** and targets companies in oil, gas, consumable fuels, and energy equipment/services. That can work well if the inflation outcome is oil-led, but it is still an **equity** trade on earnings, valuation, and broader market conditions, not a direct bet on CPI itself. citeturn927848view4

For a U.S.-exchange inflation trade, the clean ranking is: **most direct to realized inflation = term TIPS ETFs like IBID/IBIE/IBIF, then STIP/LDRI; more rate-sensitive but still inflation-linked = TIP/SCHP; mostly a short-rate bet = TFLO/USFR; mostly a commodity-shock bet = PDBC/DBC; oil-led inflation with equity beta = XLE.** The cleanest single-ticker expression is usually a **horizon-matched term TIPS ETF**, or **STIP/LDRI** if you want a rolling near-term inflation sleeve rather than a fixed maturity. citeturn861372view0turn584932view0turn571626view0turn584932view1turn584932view2turn584932view3turn584932view4
