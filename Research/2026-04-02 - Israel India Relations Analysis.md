**Direct answer**

The Raj-era Jewish and British merchant connection was a real but secondary influence. It helped create familiarity, goodwill, and a durable story of Jews living securely in India, but today’s India–Israel partnership is driven much more by post-1992 strategic convergence in defence, technology, agriculture, water, and trade. India recognized Israel in 1950, opened full diplomatic relations only in 1992, and in February 2026 the two sides elevated ties to a “Special Strategic Partnership for Peace, Innovation and Prosperity.” Official Indian briefs also note US$3.75 billion in bilateral merchandise trade in FY 2024–25, a 2025 defence cooperation MoU, over 42,000 Indian citizens in Israel, and over 100,000 Jews of Indian origin in Israel. **Why the Raj still matters**

Under the Raj, Jewish merchants mattered mainly through Baghdadi houses operating inside British imperial commerce. The Sassoons in Bombay and the Ezras in Calcutta used imperial trade circuits to build large fortunes; David Sassoon’s firm stretched to Calcutta, Shanghai, Canton, and Hong Kong, while the family helped build Bombay’s cotton industry, dock infrastructure, schools, hospitals, and charities. In Bombay, David Sassoon moved among Parsi magnates, Iranian merchants, and British officials, and one study describes the Ezras as embedded in elite Indian and Jewish cultures. So yes: Raj-era Jewish merchants did enter urban mercantile and civic elites. citeturn963059view1turn963059view0turn690870view1

That legacy still matters mostly as memory and symbolism. The 2017 India–Israel joint statement said Jewish communities had always had a home in India and explicitly thanked the Jewish community in India and Jews of Indian origin in Israel for bringing the two societies closer. Modi repeated the same bridge idea in his 2026 Knesset address, saying the Indian community in Israel and the Jewish diaspora in India had contributed significantly to both countries. So the Raj merchant past helped create a usable narrative of mutual comfort. The specifically British role, though, was mostly structural: empire supplied the ports, legal order, shipping, and markets in which these Jewish firms rose; it did not by itself create a pro-Israel Indian foreign policy. citeturn765794view0turn216446view2turn746798view2turn963059view0

But those Raj links did **not** themselves create state-level friendliness. Independent India initially approached Israel through anti-colonial and anti-partition lenses: India voted against the UN partition plan, recognized Israel only in 1950, and then kept “recognition without relations” until 1992. P. R. Kumaraswamy’s account ties that long delay to India’s discomfort with religion-based partition after 1947, its consistent support for Palestine, and the Pakistan/Arab-Muslim setting of Cold War diplomacy. Indian Jews were not major political drivers inside India either: although Zionist associations appeared in Bombay in 1920, one study says Indian Jews generally stayed out of Indian domestic and foreign-policy struggles. So if the question is whether Raj merchants *established* today’s friendship, the answer is: only indirectly, and not decisively. citeturn644958search2turn746798view0turn862682view0turn849372view1

**On intermixing and elite links**

There are really two different histories here. At the deep community level, yes: genetic studies show that both Bene Israel and Cochin Jews have substantial Jewish and Indian ancestry, which means real Jewish–Indian admixture occurred centuries ago in the formation of those communities. But that is an older community-formation story. It is not the same as saying that Raj-era Jewish merchant houses widely married into the later Indian business elite. citeturn534588view0turn534588view1

For the colonial merchant elites, the evidence points more to endogamy than fusion. The Baghdadi community is described as highly endogamous, with cousin marriage common; another study says Baghdadi Jews often sought European status and viewed association with the Bene Israel as too Indian. A recent study of Bene Israel in Aden likewise describes strong endogamy and marriage boundaries. Even elite marriage strategies tended to consolidate Jewish families with other Jewish families, as in the Ezra case. Taken together, the literature supports dense social and business collaboration with Indian elites, but not a large, traceable pattern of intermarriage that would directly connect Raj-era Jewish merchants to today’s Indian elite networks. citeturn349868search2turn690870view2turn349868search12turn902769search1

So to your last question: I do **not** see strong evidence for a durable, mixed Jewish–Indian merchant bloc linking directly to today’s Indian elites. Many Baghdadi Jews left India after 1947, and the strongest living human bridge is now the Indian-origin Jewish population in Israel, not old Bombay or Calcutta merchant lineages inside India. Official India–Israel documents treat that community as a bridge, but one policy study argues its role has been mostly social and economic rather than elite policy influence.
