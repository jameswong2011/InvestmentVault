## The core theses on X and what the accounts are arguing

The investment arguments circulating in this cluster of posts and the broader X photonics community during this exact window converge on several interlocking claims. First, **the copper-to-glass transition is mandatory, not optional**. At 800G speeds and beyond, copper wiring hits hard physical limits around heat, signal degradation, and power consumption — scaling to a million GPUs with pluggable transceivers would consume ~180 MW in optics alone, per NVIDIA's own math. Second, the optical supply chain is effectively **sold out through at least 2027**, creating pricing power and margin expansion for incumbents. Third, silicon photonics, CPO, and LPO (linear pluggable optics) are advancing on three simultaneous fronts, with only one core goal: eliminate energy consumption and latency to reshape pricing power in the 1.6T era. Fourth, unlike the 2000 fiber bubble, the 2026 infrastructure buildout is rigid demand forced by AI compute needs — not speculative overbuilding.

The five accounts cover the photonics investment landscape from different vantage points. **@damnang2**, a self-described semiconductor engineer in Silicon Valley with three of the five posts, brings technical insider perspective on silicon photonics process technology and CPO architecture. @MartinShkreli, with ~398K followers and a track record of contrarian tech calls, appears to be weighing in on the sector's investability after the NVIDIA catalyst. @superposition_V's late-February post predates the NVIDIA announcement and likely represents an early-mover thesis on CPO adoption timing.

## Conclusion: a tale of two timescales

Three insights emerge from the data that are underappreciated. First, the **"blast radius" asymmetry** is often overlooked: while CPO has 5× fewer failures, each failure affects 64+ ports versus one for pluggables, making the net operational impact highly dependent on cluster architecture and fault tolerance design. Second, **network flattening** enabled by CPO's high-radix switches may deliver more economic value (up to 7% cluster cost reduction) than the power savings alone (~3%), making topology optimization the hidden driver of CPO economics. Third, the **448G SerDes generation expected around 2027–2028** represents a hard inflection point where signal integrity challenges may make pluggables technically unviable at the required scale, transforming CPO from an optimization choice to an architectural necessity—regardless of its yield maturity at that point.

The quantitative picture reveals CPO as a technology with **proven reliability advantages** (5× better MTBF, zero link flaps in millions of test hours) and **transformative power efficiency** (3.5–5× improvement), constrained by **immature manufacturing yields** (60–75% versus >90% for CMOS) and an **upfront cost premium** that only reverses at hyperscale. The critical yield threshold of **>80–85%** remains 15–25 points away for most heterogeneous integration processes—roughly where CMOS was in the late 1990s, suggesting a multi-year learning curve remains.

## Company-by-company investment positioning and what the numbers show

**Applied Optoelectronics (AAOI)** is the consensus "dark horse" on X — the most discussed name among the @damnang2 posts. Full-year 2025 revenue hit **$455.7 million** (up 83% YoY), and management guided 2026 revenue to **exceed $1 billion** versus consensus of ~$834 million. The company's 800G ramp is expected to dominate revenue beginning Q2 2026, with production capacity targeting over 200,000 units per month by mid-2026. AAOI launched a **400mW narrow-linewidth pump laser** specifically designed for silicon photonics and CPO in December 2025, positioning it as a component supplier to CPO architectures as well as a transceiver vendor. The company is deeply bound to Microsoft (28.8% of 2025 revenue) through its cost-advantaged LPO solution, which occupies an excellent ecological niche in the mid-end market — sensitive to power consumption but not yet willing to pay for full CPO. The stock surged **22.4% on the NVIDIA announcement day despite having zero direct involvement in the deal**. Key risks include extreme customer concentration (Digicomm 53%, Microsoft 29%), heavy dilution from a $250 million ATM offering, and still-negative GAAP profitability.

**Coherent Corp (COHR)** occupies the role of vertically integrated industrial-scale producer. With Q2 FY2026 revenue of **$1.69 billion** (up 22% pro forma), a book-to-bill ratio exceeding **4x** in data centers, and bookings visibility extending into calendar 2028, Coherent is the volume leader with roughly 25% market share in optical transceivers. The company received **$2 billion in non-dilutive capital from NVIDIA** as part of a multibillion-dollar purchase commitment for advanced laser and optical networking products, specifically targeting silicon photonics for the Spectrum-X platform. Coherent's 6-inch indium phosphide wafer ramp — with wafer starts quadrupling quarter-over-quarter — gives it a manufacturing moat. The stock was added to the S&P 500 on March 6, 2026, and trades at ~35x forward earnings after a 93% YTD run. Analyst targets range from $168 to Barclays' **$350**, raised the day after the NVIDIA deal. Initial CPO revenue is expected late 2026. The X consensus views COHR as the "strategic streamliner" — a company that sold its aerospace, defense, and materials processing divisions to concentrate resources on capturing the fattest margin in AI data center photonics.

**Lumentum (LITE)** is positioned as the specialized performance leader. Q2 FY2026 revenue hit a record **$665.5 million** (up 65.5% YoY), with non-GAAP EPS of $1.67 beating consensus by $0.28. The company guided Q3 revenue to **$780M-$830M**, implying ~85% year-over-year growth. Lumentum is **currently the only supplier shipping 200G-per-lane EMLs at volume** — the critical component for 1.6T pluggable transceivers — and its CEO has called 2026 "a breakout year for laser chip sales." NVIDIA invested $2 billion via convertible preferred stock, creating a dilution overhang that distinguishes it from Coherent's cleaner non-dilutive deal. The company's OCS (optical circuit switch) backlog surged past **$400 million**. Rosenblatt's price target of **$900** is the highest on the Street. The X thesis on LITE centers on its leap from "component price differences" to "system premium" through vertical integration — it achieved this by acquiring Cloud Light for $750 million in 2023 and now sits in the "lead pack" of transceiver suppliers for the 1.6T transition.

**IQE plc (IQE)** sits at the base of the supply chain as the world's leading outsourced manufacturer of compound semiconductor epiwafers. The stock surged **88% in five trading days** in late February 2026 on takeover speculation, with a strategic review ongoing under the UK Takeover Code since September 2025 — Lazard is advising on potential offers for the company as a whole. IQE's FY2025 revenue was approximately **£97 million**, with photonics demand from AI/data center markets providing the brightest spot in an otherwise challenged business. The company launched a **6-inch InP foundry platform** to serve future silicon photonics demand and secured multiple Tier 1 design wins for laser and detector products enabling next-gen AI infrastructure. At a market cap of only ~£156-186 million (~$195-230 million), IQE represents a high-risk, high-reward play on the InP substrate bottleneck that constrains the entire photonics supply chain. The X discussion around IQE focuses on its potential as an acquisition target — its InP epiwafer capabilities become strategically critical as demand outstrips supply.

### The dollar math at hyperscale

SemiAnalysis provides the most granular independent cost analysis. For NVIDIA's Q3400/Q3450 class switch, **72 pluggable 1.6T transceivers cost approximately $72,000**, while CPO optical engines plus external laser sources have a raw cost of **$35,000–$40,000** but reach **$80,000–$90,000** at the buyer level after vendor gross margins of approximately 60%. At the networking level, CPO delivers **31% lower total networking cost** versus DSP transceivers by eliminating transceiver overhead and reducing SerDes complexity. At the total cluster level, however, server racks dominate spending, limiting CPO's impact to approximately **3% total cluster cost reduction**—or up to **7%** when CPO enables network flattening from three layers to two (which cuts transceiver costs by **86%** and total networking cost by **46%**).

## Conclusion: the investment logic has shifted, but valuation discipline still matters

The key risk that the X discussion underemphasizes is valuation. COHR at ~35x forward earnings, LITE after a 967% one-year run, and AAOI at 173% YTD gains all embed significant growth expectations. A deceleration in hyperscaler capex, a delay in 1.6T ramp timing, or faster-than-expected CPO cannibalization of pluggable transceivers (hurting AAOI's core business) could create sharp corrections in names trading at these multiples. The next major catalyst is **OFC 2026 (March 17-19)** and **NVIDIA GTC (March 16)**, where partnership confirmations and CPO product roadmaps will either reinforce or challenge the current pricing. For investors, the highest-conviction insight from this analysis is that **the laser and component layer — the "picks and shovels" of photonics — offers the most durable positioning** because these companies supply both pluggable and CPO architectures, insulating them from technology-path risk while capturing the full volume of the optical buildout.

The core takeaway for your analysis: COHR is the most "reasonably" valued of the group on forward estimates given its scale and visibility, but even at ~36x forward P/E it's pricing in heavy execution on InP ramp and 1.6T transition. LITE is richer on every metric but has the highest revenue growth trajectory. AAOI, AXTI, and IQE are all pre-profit and priced purely on the optionality of supply chain positioning — classic momentum/narrative names where traditional valuation frameworks struggle to provide guardrails. The three unprofitable names carry the most downside risk if the CPO timeline slips or hyperscaler capex decelerates.

Morningstar shows P/S (TTM) at 13.4x with no meaningful trailing P/E (the company is GAAP unprofitable). The forward P/E on Intellectia was listed at ~217x, though this seems to be using near-term estimates before the $1B+ revenue guide kicks in. TTM revenue is ~$456M with losses of -$156M, ROE of -40%, and ROIC of -8%. If management delivers on $1B+ CY2026 revenue guidance, forward P/S drops to ~7x with the EV potentially compressing further given positive operating income guidance (~$120M non-GAAP). But on current financials this is emphatically a "pay for growth" story — no trailing P/E or EV/EBIT is computable because EBIT is deeply negative. One analysis flagged forward P/E of ~48x, P/B above 4.9x, and P/S above 6.6x as reflecting growth expectations well ahead of current fundamentals.

On modulators, which are the most competitively differentiated component, there are two dominant architectures. Mach-Zehnder Modulators (MZM) split light into two arms, apply voltage to change refractive index, then recombine. They're broadband and thermally stable but large (~1-4mm). Micro-Ring Modulators (MRM) are tiny resonant structures (~5-10μm radius) that are extremely compact but narrowband and thermally sensitive, requiring active wavelength locking circuits. TSMC Director Shih-Fen Huang highlighted at SEMICON Taiwan that MZMs excel in high-speed, high-power scenarios while MRMs provide compact, high-density advantages, making them core components for efficient CPO transmission. TSMC's successful demonstration of MRMs on its 3nm process at ISSCC 2026 is the breakthrough making high-density CPO viable.

The third post (status/2023220154822447405) is "Lecture 3: The Devil is in the Details" — their technical deep dive into the specific components: waveguides, modulators (MRM vs MZM), photodetectors, couplers, and the critical external laser question. PhotonCap has explicitly stated that "Micro-Ring-Modulator-based CPO is the clear future," referencing the ISSCC 2026 papers. Key claims include that the critical bottleneck is not photonic circuit design but optical testing and characterization (dramatically slower than electrical testing), and that laser integration remains the single highest-risk element because co-packaging a laser with a hot ASIC creates thermal stress that rapidly degrades laser diodes. PhotonCap's broader framework emphasizes picks-and-shovels: TSEM for foundry, FORM for optical test/metrology, LITE for lasers, GLW for fiber, GFS for foundry, and POET for hybrid integration.

The photonics trade has entered a new phase. The structural thesis is sound — AI infrastructure scaling physically requires the copper-to-optical transition, and the companies controlling laser capacity, silicon photonics integration, and InP manufacturing sit at the chokepoint. **LITE and COHR are now strategic NVIDIA partners**, not just vendors, creating a visibility and pricing power dynamic that supports multi-year earnings growth. AAOI offers the highest-beta exposure with its guided revenue doubling and Microsoft/hyperscaler dependency. IQE is a speculative play on the substrate bottleneck and potential acquisition premium.

iST observes three recurring barriers that consistently slow mass-production readiness. Optical characterization requires full wavelength sweeps and repeated alignment for every die — unlike electrical probing, there is no instant plug-and-test. Conventional wafer acceptance test tools can indicate degradation but cannot localize the loss mechanism or quantify its spatial origin. These constraints make optical test dramatically slower, hindering design turnaround and delaying production ramp.
