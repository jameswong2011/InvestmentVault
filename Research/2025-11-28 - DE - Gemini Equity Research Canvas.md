# EQUITY RESEARCH: DEERE & COMPANY (NYSE: DE)

## Navigating the Cyclical Trough: The Convergence of Smart Industrial Strategy and Agricultural Headwinds

### Executive Summary

Deere & Company (NYSE: DE), the undisputed global hegemon in agricultural machinery and a formidable tier-one player in construction and forestry equipment, currently stands at a definitive strategic and cyclical juncture. Following the release of its Fourth Quarter and Full Year Fiscal 2025 earnings in late November 2025, the company faces a complex operating environment characterized by divergent signals: a beat on quarterly revenue expectations contrasted sharply by a deteriorating outlook for large agricultural equipment demand in Fiscal 2026. This report initiates coverage with a **NEUTRAL / HOLD** rating, advising institutional investors to await a clearer bottom in the large agriculture cycle—expected to materialize in mid-to-late 2026—before engaging in aggressive accumulation.

The core investment tension for Deere & Company lies between the immediate macroeconomic pressure—driven by normalizing commodity prices, elevated interest rates, and a projected decline in U.S. crop receipts—and the long-term valuation unlock promised by Deere’s "Smart Industrial" strategy. The latter aims to transition the company from a pure-play hardware manufacturer to a provider of integrated hardware-software solutions, targeting a recurring revenue model of 10% by 2030 through precision agriculture and autonomy. While the market has partially priced in this technological transformation, assigning Deere a premium valuation relative to traditional machinery peers, the gravity of the agricultural cycle cannot be ignored in the near term.

Q4 2025 revenue of $12.39 billion (+11% YoY) demonstrated resilience, particularly in the Construction & Forestry segment which acted as a critical ballast against agricultural softness. However, the guidance for Fiscal 2026 forecasts a net income range of $4.0–$4.75 billion, marking a significant retrenchment from the $5.027 billion achieved in FY2025 and the $7.1 billion peak of FY2024. This guidance implies a contraction in earnings power that will test the resilience of the stock's multiple. The divergence between "Net Farm Income," which remains optically high due to livestock strength and government payments, and "Crop Receipts," which are forecast to decline, creates a specific headwind for Deere's most profitable segment: large production agriculture.

Furthermore, the legal landscape presents an evolving risk factor. The ongoing "Right to Repair" litigation, spearheaded by the FTC and various state attorneys general, threatens the integrity of Deere’s high-margin aftermarket parts and services ecosystem. While the company is vigorously defending its walled garden, regulatory pressure to open diagnostic tools to independent repair shops could lead to long-term margin compression in the recurring revenue streams that are central to the bullish thesis.

Despite these headwinds, Deere’s balance sheet remains a fortress, and its capital allocation strategy continues to favor shareholder returns through consistent dividends and opportunistic share repurchases. The company’s technological moat—anchored by the John Deere Operations Center and a proprietary autonomy stack—remains unassailable by competitors in the immediate term. However, with the stock trading at approximately 25x earnings in a shrinking earnings environment, the risk-reward profile is currently balanced. We project that the "air pocket" in demand will persist through the first half of 2026, offering better entry points for the long-term investor.

## 1. Fundamental Analysis

### 1.1 Revenue Architecture and Growth Trends

Deere & Company’s financial performance for the fiscal year ending November 2025 reveals a company managing a disciplined contraction while attempting to maintain pricing power. The Q4 2025 results, released on November 26, 2025, presented a nuanced picture often described by analysts as a "mixed harvest." While top-line numbers showed surprising resilience, the composition of that revenue raises questions about sustainability in the coming fiscal year.

**Quarterly Performance vs. Expectations**
In the fourth quarter of 2025, worldwide net sales and revenues increased 11% year-over-year to $12.39 billion, significantly outpacing the analyst consensus which hovered between $9.82 billion and $9.99 billion.[1, 2, 3] This upside surprise, a beat of nearly $2.5 billion, was not driven by a broad-based recovery in organic demand, but rather by specific segmental strength and shipment timing. Notably, the Construction & Forestry division saw sales surge 27% due to higher shipment volumes, a direct beneficiary of infrastructure spending tailwinds.[2]

However, when viewed through the lens of the full fiscal year, the trajectory is undeniably negative. Full-year FY2025 net income settled at $5.027 billion ($18.50 per share), a sharp and material decline from the record $7.100 billion ($25.62 per share) achieved in FY2024.[4] This 29% decline in annual profitability underscores the severity of the post-pandemic normalization in the agricultural machinery market. The market's reaction—a pre-market decline of over 5% following the release—indicates that investors are looking past the quarterly revenue beat and focusing intently on the contracting earnings power.[2]

**Segmental Revenue Breakdown**
The disparity in performance across Deere’s three primary industrial segments highlights the divergent micro-economic forces at play:

1.  **Production & Precision Agriculture:** This segment, the crown jewel of Deere’s portfolio, reported Q4 net sales of $4.74 billion, a 10% increase year-over-year.[5] While optically positive, this growth was driven largely by price realization and shipment timing rather than robust end-user demand. The guidance for 2026 is sobering: management forecasts a 5-10% decline in net sales for this segment.[6] This projected decline reflects the fundamental reality that North American farmers are pulling back on capital expenditures for large equipment (combines, 4WD tractors) as crop receipts fall.
2.  **Small Ag & Turf:** Net sales for Q4 were up 7% at $2.45 billion.[2] However, this segment is facing intense inventory pressure. The sales growth here masks a significant deterioration in profitability, discussed in the margin section below. For 2026, Deere projects a rebound with sales up approximately 10% [6], largely dependent on a recovery in the consumer and small-contractor economy.
3.  **Construction & Forestry:** This segment was the outlier, with net sales jumping 27% to $3.38 billion.[2] The driver here is clear: the lagging effect of the U.S. Infrastructure Investment and Jobs Act is finally translating into iron on the ground. However, even this segment faces headwinds in 2026, with the outlook flattening to "flat to up 5%" [5], suggesting the explosive growth phase of the infrastructure cycle may be peaking.

### 1.2 Margin Analysis and Cost Structure

The most concerning aspect of the FY2025 report is the deterioration in profitability metrics despite revenue growth in the final quarter. This divergence signals a breakdown in operating leverage and rising structural costs that the company has been unable to fully offset through pricing actions.

**Operating Margin Compression**
Operating profit in the core **Production & Precision Agriculture** segment declined to $604 million in Q4 2025 from $657 million in the prior year, despite the 10% increase in top-line sales.[5] This implies a notable contraction in operating margins for the segment, falling to approximately 12.7% from 15.3% a year prior.[5] For a mature industrial company, margin contraction on rising sales is a red flag, indicating that the cost of goods sold (COGS) is rising faster than the company can raise prices.

**Key Cost Drivers**
The margin squeeze is not monolithic but rather the result of a confluence of specific factors:
*   **Production Costs:** Higher production costs negatively impacted operating profit by approximately $147 million in the ag segments.[7] This reflects sticky inflation in labor, energy, and raw materials that continues to plague the manufacturing sector.
*   **Tariffs and Trade Barriers:** Management explicitly highlighted "higher tariffs" as a specific headwind affecting the Construction & Forestry segment.[2, 5] As geopolitical tensions persist, particularly with China (a key supplier of components), tariff-related costs are becoming a structural drag rather than a transitory issue.
*   **Warranty and Incentive Costs:** The **Small Ag & Turf** segment provided the most dramatic negative surprise. Operating profit in this segment plummeted to just $25 million from $234 million in the prior year.[7] This near-total collapse in profitability was driven by $85 million in warranty costs and significant sales incentives required to clear inventory. This suggests potential quality control issues or, more likely, an aggressive effort to destock dealer channels at the expense of factory margins.

**Future Margin Outlook**
Looking ahead to Fiscal 2026, Deere forecasts Production & Precision Ag margins to compress further to a range of 11–13%, down from 15.4% in fiscal 2025.[6] This guidance is critical for the investment thesis. It suggests that Deere expects to lose pricing power in the coming year, forcing it to absorb more cost inflation or offer deeper discounts to move large equipment. The implied deleveraging is significant: a small drop in volume is translating into a large drop in profit, characteristic of high-fixed-cost manufacturing businesses entering a down cycle.

### 1.3 Free Cash Flow and Capital Allocation

Despite earnings volatility, Deere remains a robust cash generator, a characteristic that provides a floor to the stock price even during cyclical downturns. The company’s ability to convert net income into free cash flow (FCF) allows it to maintain shareholder returns without jeopardizing its R&D budget—a crucial factor for the "Smart Industrial" thesis.

**Free Cash Flow Dynamics**
Deere’s annual free cash flow for 2024 was $9.52 billion, a 4.94% increase from 2023.[8] This massive liquidity buffer was built during the peak of the cycle. However, as net income contracts in 2025 and 2026, FCF generation will naturally moderate. The volatility in FCF is often exacerbated during downturns by working capital builds; as sales slow, inventory tends to sit longer on balance sheets before being converted to cash. Deere has been proactively managing this, noting that North American earthmoving inventory is down 35% [9], a positive sign of working capital discipline.

**Dividend Sustainability**
Deere continues to prioritize returning capital to shareholders. The Board declared a quarterly dividend of $1.62 per share payable in November 2025.[10] With a payout ratio hovering around a moderate 32% [11], the dividend is exceedingly safe. Even if earnings were to dip to the bottom end of the 2026 guidance range ($4.0 billion), the implied payout ratio would remain healthy, likely under 45%. This makes Deere an attractive "dividend growth" hold for income-focused portfolios, even if capital appreciation is stalled.

**Share Repurchases**
Management’s strategy involves deploying excess cash to share repurchases once rating requirements and dividend targets are met.[12] However, the velocity of buybacks is likely to decelerate in 2026 compared to the aggressive programs of 2022–2024. With net income forecast to drop by roughly $1 billion year-over-year, the surplus cash available for buybacks will diminish. Investors should not expect share count reduction to be a primary driver of EPS growth in 2026.

### 1.4 Valuation Analysis: The "Tech Premium" Debate

Deere currently trades at a valuation premium relative to its heavy machinery peers, a discrepancy that reflects the market's partial acceptance of its technology narrative. However, this premium also creates valuation risk if the company is re-rated as a pure cyclical industrial.

**Analysis of the Premium**
Traditionally, heavy machinery stocks trade at 12x–15x P/E during mid-cycle conditions. Deere’s current valuation of ~25x [13, 14] is historically rich and sits significantly above its 5-year average of ~17.8x.[14] This expansion in the multiple is the "Tech Premium." Investors are paying up for the promise of recurring software revenue, which commands higher multiples (typically 25x-30x) than hardware revenue.

However, the risk of de-rating is acute. With 2026 earnings guided down to $4.0–$4.75 billion (implying EPS of roughly $14.50–$17.20), the forward P/E on 2026 numbers at current prices (~$471–$487) is effectively 28x-33x. This is a "growth stock" multiple on a shrinking earnings base. If the "tech transition" narrative falters, or if the ag downturn is deeper than expected, the stock could re-rate back to 15x. On $16.00 earnings, a 15x multiple implies a share price of $240, representing massive downside risk. The current price essentially prices in a "soft landing" and a rapid return to growth in 2027.

**Comparison vs. Caterpillar**
While CAT appears to have a higher TTM P/E in some datasets (28.6x), its forward outlook is buoyed by mining and energy exposure, which are in different cycles than agriculture. Deere’s EV/EBITDA of >16x is substantially higher than AGCO’s 9.8x [18], reflecting Deere’s dominant market share and superior tech stack. The gap between DE and AGCO is the "moat premium"—the market believes Deere’s walled garden is more defensible than AGCO’s open architecture.

### 1.5 Insider Ownership and Recent Trades

Insider activity provides a bearish to neutral signal, suggesting that those closest to the company's operations do not view the current stock price as a bargain.

**Net Selling Activity**
Over the last 12 months, there have been 32 insider sells against 25 open market buys, with a net activity of -41,450 shares.[20] While net selling is not uncommon due to stock-based compensation vesting, the lack of significant open-market buying during the recent stock dips is notable.

**Key Executive Transactions**
Specifically, Rajesh Kalathur, the President of JD Financial and CIO—a key architect of the "Smart Industrial" strategy—executed sales of roughly $12.3 million (24,580 shares) in the six months leading up to November 2025.[21] While executives often sell for diversification (10b5-1 plans), significant sales by the CIO, who has the best visibility into the fintech and software growth, could be interpreted as a signal that the valuation fully reflects the near-term success of those initiatives.

## 2. Sector & Macro View

### 2.1 The Agricultural Cycle: Entering the Trough

The macroeconomic environment for global agriculture is shifting from a period of historic windfalls, driven by pandemic disruptions and the Ukraine war, to a constrained margin environment for farmers. This cyclical shift is the primary headwind facing Deere & Company.

**USDA 2025 Outlook: The Devil in the Details**
The USDA’s forecast for 2025 Net Farm Income is $179.8 billion.[22, 23] On the surface, this looks bullish—it is a nominal increase of nearly 40% year-over-year. However, for Deere investors, the *composition* of this income is far less favorable.
*   **Livestock vs. Crops Divergence:** The aggregate income boost is driven almost entirely by stronger livestock markets (cattle, hogs, poultry) and a massive 356% surge in government payments (disaster aid, conservation programs).[22, 23] Livestock farmers purchase feed mixers and small tractors, but they do not buy the high-margin X9 Combines and 9RX Tractors that drive Deere's profitability.
*   **Crop Receipt Contraction:** Crucially, **crop cash receipts**—the primary driver for large equipment sales—are forecast to *decline* by $12.3 billion (-4.9%).[24, 25] Receipts for corn are expected to fall 3.7%, soybeans by 7.2%, and wheat by 9.8%.[22] This decline is due to global supply gluts and normalizing prices. When corn prices fall below the cost of production (or hover near breakeven), row-crop farmers aggressively cut CAPEX. This directly validates Deere’s guidance for a 5-10% decline in Production & Precision Ag sales in 2026.

**Farmer Balance Sheets and Sentiment**
While farm income is technically high, production expenses are forecast to rise 2.6% in 2025 [25], continuing a multi-year trend of input inflation (fertilizer, seed, labor). This squeezes operating margins. Although farmland values remain at record highs, providing ample collateral for loans, high interest rates (farm operating loans are often 8%+) discourage borrowing for new machinery. The "propensity to spend" on capital equipment diminishes significantly when the ROI on a new combine is eroded by financing costs and low grain prices.

### 2.2 Construction & Forestry: The Infrastructure Offset

While Agriculture struggles, the Construction & Forestry (C&F) segment acts as a stabilizing ballast for Deere, although it is not immune to its own cyclical challenges.

**Infrastructure Tailwinds**
The segment benefited from a 27% sales surge in Q4 2025 [2, 9], driven by the continued rollout of projects funded by the U.S. Infrastructure Investment and Jobs Act (IIJA). Road building, bridge repair, and non-residential construction projects require the exact earthmoving equipment Deere produces. This diversification helps smooth the volatility of the ag cycle.

**Inventory Discipline**
Deere has been notably disciplined in this segment. Management reported that North American earthmoving equipment inventory is down ~35% year-over-year.[9] This suggests that Deere has been "under-producing" relative to retail demand to clear channel inventory. This discipline positions the segment to match production with retail demand in 2026, avoiding the margin-crushing discounting seen in previous construction downturns.

**2026 Outlook**
Despite the strong 2025 finish, Deere projects C&F sales to be "flat to up 5%" in 2026.[5] This deceleration indicates that the initial pulse of infrastructure spending has been absorbed, and the market is normalizing. High interest rates are also weighing heavily on residential construction (housing starts), which dampens demand for smaller construction equipment (compact track loaders) used in homebuilding.

### 2.3 Competitive Positioning and Market Share

Deere remains the undisputed market leader but faces intensifying competition in specific verticals and regions.

**The "Big Three" Dynamic**
Globally, the agricultural machinery market is an oligopoly dominated by Deere, CNH Industrial (Case IH/New Holland), and AGCO (Fendt/Massey Ferguson), with Kubota leading the smaller tractor segment.[26] Deere holds the dominant market share in North American high-horsepower tractors and combines. Its competitive advantage is no longer just horsepower or dealer network density; it is the **tech stack**. The closed ecosystem of the John Deere Operations Center creates high switching costs—once a farmer’s data is in the Deere cloud, moving to a Case IH fleet becomes a data migration headache.

**The Mixed-Fleet Challenge**
Competitors are responding to Deere's walled garden by embracing "mixed fleet" interoperability. AGCO, for example, has launched the PTx brand (following a JV with Trimble) to offer precision ag solutions that work on *any* brand of tractor.[27] This "Android vs. iOS" dynamic is the key competitive battleground of the next decade. While Deere’s "iOS" (closed, seamless, premium) approach currently yields higher margins, AGCO’s "Android" (open, flexible) approach is gaining traction with farmers who run mixed fleets.

**The China Threat**
In the Chinese market, domestic players like YTO Group, Zoomlion, and Weichai Lovol are gaining ground.[28] Supported by government initiatives to modernize Chinese agriculture and reduce reliance on foreign tech, these companies are improving their quality and offering products at significantly lower price points. While they do not yet pose a threat to Deere in North America, they are fierce competitors in developing markets (Southeast Asia, Africa) where Deere seeks future growth.

## 3. Thesis Validation

### 3.1 The Bull Case: The "Smart Industrial" Transformation

The primary argument for owning Deere is not the hardware cycle, but the software evolution. The bull case rests on the belief that Deere can decouple its earnings from the volatility of corn prices.

1.  **Recurring Revenue Unlock:** Deere has a stated ambition to grow recurring revenue to 10% by 2030.[29] This shifts the business model from volatile equipment sales to stable, high-margin software subscriptions (SaaS). The "Solutions as a Service" model allows farmers to pay for autonomy on a per-acre basis rather than upfront. If Deere achieves this, it fundamentally changes the valuation paradigm, justifying a higher multiple.
2.  **Autonomy Leadership:** Deere is the only major OEM with a commercially available, fully autonomous tractor (8R) and tillage solution, with expansion into autonomous spraying (5ML Orchard) and construction (dump trucks) announced for 2025/2026.[30, 31] This technological lead is estimated to be 3–5 years ahead of competitors. In a world of chronic labor shortages, autonomy is not a luxury; it is a necessity for large-scale farming.
3.  **Installed Base Monetization:** With the launch of "Next Generation Perception System" retrofit kits at CES 2025 [32], Deere can monetize its vast installed base of older machines. This reduces reliance on new unit sales and allows Deere to capture value from the secondary market, something it has historically failed to do.

### 3.2 The Bear Case: Cyclical Gravity and Legal Risks

Counter-arguments suggest the stock is priced for perfection in an imperfect environment, and structural risks loom large.

1.  **Cyclical "Air Pocket":** The decline in crop receipts is a fundamental demand destroyer. With 2026 net income guided to ~$4.4 billion (midpoint), the stock is trading at historically high multiples for a down-cycle earnings base. If corn prices remain suppressed below break-even production costs ($4.50-$4.80/bushel), equipment demand could contract longer than the anticipated one-year trough. The market is betting on a "V-shaped" recovery; history suggests "U-shaped" is more likely.
2.  **Valuation Compression:** If the market decides Deere is still primarily a cyclical machinery company rather than a tech company, the multiple could compress from 25x back to the historical 15x. This would result in a massive drawdown in share price, irrespective of operational performance.
3.  **Pricing Power Fatigue:** The margin contraction in Q4 2025 and the guidance for 2026 suggest Deere has hit a ceiling on price increases. After years of aggressive price hikes (often exceeding inflation), farmers are pushing back. Without pricing levers, revenue growth must come from volume, which is falling.

### 3.3 The Black Swan: "Right to Repair" Litigation

Investors must not overlook the *Deere & Co. Repair Services Antitrust Litigation*. This is a structural risk to the company's most profitable revenue stream.

**The Conflict**
Deere has historically restricted access to the software tools required to diagnose and repair its modern equipment, forcing farmers to use authorized dealers. This ensures a steady stream of high-margin service revenue for its dealer network. However, the FTC and farmer advocacy groups argue this is an illegal monopoly on repair services.[33]

**Legal Status and Implications**
In 2025, a federal judge granted Deere access to competitor data (AGCO, CNH) to build its defense, but the litigation is proceeding and the FTC's pressure remains intense.[34] If Deere loses or is forced to settle, it may have to open its diagnostic ecosystem to independent repair shops. This would commoditize repair services, driving down prices and eroding the high margins that Parts & Services contribute (typically 15-20% of revenue but a much higher share of profit). This would be a permanent impairment to the company's earnings power.

### 3.4 Final Verdict: Neutral / Hold

**Justification:** The long-term strategic pivot to autonomy is undeniable and creates a massive addressable market. However, the next 12–18 months present a "valuation valley" where earnings will decline while the stock price remains elevated. The disconnect between a deepening ag recession (lower crop prices) and the stock’s premium multiple offers an unattractive risk-reward profile for new capital in the immediate term. The "Smart Industrial" thesis is valid, but the price you pay for it matters. At 25x peak (and falling) earnings, the price is too high. We prefer to wait for the 2026 guidance de-risking to fully play out.

## 4. Catalyst Watch

Investors should monitor the following events to gauge the timing of the cycle turn and the success of the tech uptake:

### 4.1 Short-Term Catalysts (0–6 Months)
*   **CES 2026 (January 2026):** Key keynote expected regarding the future of construction technology and AI integration.[35] Look for announcements on "retrofit" capabilities for older models. The market will be looking for concrete data on adoption rates of the autonomous solutions announced in 2025.
*   **Commodity Classic (February 2026):** The premier farmer-led trade show. Deere typically launches major model year updates here.[36] Analyst channel checks on farmer sentiment and order book strength at this event will be critical. If farmers at the show are pessimistic about 2026 planting intentions, the stock will react negatively.
*   **Q1 2026 Earnings (February 2026):** The first check-in on the 2026 guidance. Any reduction in the $4.0–$4.75B net income range would likely trigger a sell-off. Conversely, if Deere maintains guidance despite weak crop prices, it would demonstrate the resilience of the Construction segment.

### 4.2 Long-Term Catalysts (6–18 Months)
*   **Brazil/South America Harvests:** Monitoring production levels in Brazil is crucial. If South American production falters due to weather (La Niña/El Niño), global grain prices could rebound, stimulating U.S. farmer CAPEX. A supply shock is the fastest way to end the ag downturn.
*   **Infrastructure Bill Peak Spending:** 2026 represents a peak funding year for many infrastructure projects. If the rollout of funds accelerates, the Construction & Forestry segment could surprise to the upside, offsetting Ag weakness.
*   **Autonomy Take Rate Data:** Deere needs to start disclosing specific "take rates" for its autonomous solutions. Proof that farmers are renewing subscriptions for See & Spray or Autonomy would be a major validator of the recurring revenue thesis.

## 5. Investment Summary

### Core Investment Thesis
1.  **Innovation Leader:** Unrivaled leadership in autonomous farming and precision technology (See & Spray, autonomous tillage) secures long-term pricing power and competitive differentiation.
2.  **Cyclical Headwinds:** Fiscal 2026 will be a challenging "transition year" with large ag sales declining 5-10% and net income dropping ~15-20% due to lower crop receipts and high interest rates.
3.  **Margin Pressure:** Near-term margins are compressing due to production cost inflation and negative operating leverage, though floor pricing remains disciplined.
4.  **Structural Offset:** Construction & Forestry acts as a partial hedge against the Ag downturn, supported by multi-year government infrastructure spending which has not yet peaked.
5.  **Valuation Premium:** The stock currently prices in a successful tech transition (P/E ~25x). Any execution stumble or deeper ag recession poses valuation risk.

### Final Recommendation
**Recommendation:** **HOLD** (Neutral)

**Confidence Level:** **High**. The data regarding the agricultural cycle downturn is robust (USDA forecasts, commodity prices), and the valuation premium is mathematically verifiable. The divergence creates a clear "wait and see" setup.

**Expected Timeframe:** **6–12 Months**. We expect the stock to trade sideways or drift lower during the first half of 2026 as the reality of lower earnings sets in. A more constructive stance may be warranted in late 2026 as the market begins to discount a 2027 recovery.

**Target Price Context:** Current levels (~$470-$480) fairly value the company assuming a "soft landing" in Ag. Buy zones emerge closer to $400–$420 (approx. 20x mid-cycle earnings); Sell zones exist above $500 (approaching 30x trough earnings).
