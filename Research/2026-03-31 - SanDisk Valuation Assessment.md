# NAND flash faces a multi-year structural shortage

**The NAND market is entering a supply deficit unlike any in its history.** Demand is growing at **20–22% annually** while supply can only expand at **15–17%**, creating a widening 3–7 percentage point structural gap that all major manufacturers, analysts, and industry forecasters agree will persist through at least 2027. This matters for Sandisk (SNDK) investors because the thesis of flat wafer supply plus density-only bit growth is largely correct — and the investment implications are profound. The two largest NAND producers, Samsung and SK Hynix (collectively ~50% of market revenue), are actively *shrinking* their NAND wafer output to redirect capacity toward far more profitable HBM and DRAM, while AI datacenter buildouts are driving enterprise SSD demand to surpass smartphones as the single largest NAND end market for the first time in 2026. Gartner projects **45% NAND revenue growth to $93.8 billion** in 2026, with Wolfe Research forecasting 95% year-over-year price increases. Every major supplier confirms that 2026 production is entirely sold out, with customers already negotiating 2027 allocations.

# Sandisk: a NAND pure-play riding the AI supercycle

**Sandisk (SNDK) is the single best-positioned pure-play NAND flash investment in the current AI-driven memory supercycle.** Since its spin-off from Western Digital on February 21, 2025, the stock has surged from ~$28 to ~$609, driven by an extraordinary earnings inflection: non-GAAP gross margins expanded from 22.7% to 51.1% in three quarters, with guidance for **65–67%** in the April 2026 quarter. The company trades at ~6.7x FY2027 consensus EPS of $90.46 — cheap for a business guiding quarterly revenue of $4.4–4.8B with $12–14 in EPS. But the investment thesis rests on three fundamental questions: whether HBF (High Bandwidth Flash) can create a new multibillion-dollar product category, whether standalone NAND can structurally outperform integrated memory manufacturers, and whether the NAND supercycle has legs beyond 2026. This report addresses all three.

## Conclusion: what this means for Sandisk as a pure-play NAND investment

The key risk is not demand destruction from compression or efficiency — that risk is negligible. The primary risk is **cycle timing**: if YMTC's Phase III ramps faster than expected, if Micron's Fab 10B accelerates, or if AI capex decelerates, the market could rebalance by late 2027 or 2028. Yole Group has flagged 2027 as a potential inflection. But the weight of evidence — $660–690 billion in 2026 hyperscaler capex guidance, enterprise SSDs becoming the largest NAND segment, all 2026 and early 2027 capacity sold out, NAND wafer prices up 246% YoY — overwhelmingly supports a **multi-year structural upcycle** rather than a typical 12–18 month cyclical upturn. Gartner's projection of **$93.8 billion in 2026 NAND revenue** (45% growth) and operating margins reaching **30–40%** (levels not seen since the 2017 supercycle) underscore the earnings potential for a pure-play NAND producer at this point in the cycle.

## The supply-demand balance points to a multi-year structural upcycle

**The current cycle is fundamentally different** for three reasons. First, there is a **zero-sum capacity allocation** between HBM/DRAM and NAND — manufacturers face a structural economic incentive to *not* expand NAND. Second, AI demand is not a one-time stimulus but a **secular growth driver** with multi-year visibility on hundreds of billions in committed capex. Third, the industry's post-2022 losses created deep **institutional reluctance** to build greenfield NAND capacity with 7–10 year payback periods. As IDC noted, this is "not just a cyclical shortage but a potentially permanent, strategic reallocation of the world's silicon wafer capacity."

The consensus timeline: **tight market through all of 2026 and most of 2027**, with potential easing beginning in H2 2027 as Kioxia K2 reaches full ramp, YMTC Phase III starts production, and industry layer counts cross 300+. Meaningful relief from new greenfield capacity (Micron Fab 10B) arrives no earlier than 2028. The bull case, articulated by Phison's CEO, envisions shortages lasting **"up to a decade"** as AI demand growth outpaces even expanded capacity. The bear case, flagged by Yole Group, warns that 2027 could mark the beginning of a down cycle if the PC renewal cycle ends and YMTC ramps aggressively — though this view is increasingly contrarian given the scale of AI buildout.

Bringing supply-demand into balance would require new greenfield fabs, which take **18–24 months from construction start to volume production** and cost **$10–24 billion each**. The only greenfield NAND fab currently under construction among the top three producers is Micron's Singapore Fab 10B, which won't produce wafers until **H2 2028** at earliest. YMTC's Phase III could add meaningful capacity by 2027, but U.S. Entity List restrictions limit its technology roadmap, roughly half its output may go to DRAM, and its production primarily serves the domestic Chinese market.

The pricing data already reflects this structural shift. NAND contract prices rose **55–60% quarter-over-quarter** in Q1 2026 (revised up from an initial TrendForce forecast of 33–38%). NAND wafer pricing is up **246% year-over-year**. A 30 TB enterprise TLC SSD surged from $3,062 to $10,950 — a **258% increase in nine months**. Samsung raised select memory chip prices by up to 60% since September 2025 and is contemplating an additional 20–30% for 2026 contracts. QLC NAND is backordered by **two years** for some products.

## Valuation is reasonable at current levels but demands conviction on cycle duration

For historical context, pre-acquisition SanDisk (before the 2016 WD deal) traded at **1.5–3.0x EV/Revenue** and **5–12x EV/EBITDA** with 42–43% gross margins. Current forward multiples on FY2027 estimates (~3.9x EV/Revenue, ~5.5–7x EV/EBITDA) fall within this historical range, but at dramatically higher absolute margin levels (65%+ gross margin guidance). The stock's **1,200%+ appreciation** since listing is extreme, yet the earnings growth has broadly kept pace.

At ~$609, the market prices the base case at roughly 6.7x forward earnings. The consensus price target is **$770** (range $600–$1,000), with 13 Buy ratings and zero Sells. BofA's bull-case target of $900 requires sustained pricing strength and enterprise SSD market share gains. A bear case of $400–500 would require NAND pricing deterioration of 10–20%, possibly catalyzed by Google's March 2026 TurboQuant compression algorithm announcement (which caused the recent ~20% pullback from all-time highs).

## BiCS FLASH competes on density per layer, not layer count

In enterprise SSDs, Sandisk's market share is a weakness. It holds just **4.1%** of enterprise SSD revenue versus Samsung's 35% and Solidigm's 30.2%. The DC SN861 (PCIe Gen5, 1 DWPD, up to 7.68 TB) is competitive but not class-leading — Micron's 9550 MAX dominates benchmarks with the lowest latency and highest throughput. However, Sandisk is aggressively ramping enterprise qualifications: two hyperscaler qualifications are active as of Q1 FY2026, with three more and a top storage OEM planned for calendar 2026. The "Stargate" BiCS8 QLC product is approaching hyperscaler qualification.

Sandisk's NAND technology position requires nuance. On the headline metric of layer count, Sandisk trails: its shipping BiCS8 generation has **218 layers** versus SK Hynix's 321 layers, Samsung's 286 layers, and Micron's 276 layers. But raw layer count is a misleading benchmark. Sandisk CTO Alper Ilkbahar has articulated a four-vector scaling philosophy — lateral, logical, vertical, and architectural — and the CBA architecture enables Sandisk to achieve **15–20% higher gigabyte density** than competing ~230-layer products with fewer layers.

## Contract structure is evolving but still heavily spot-exposed

However, as of Q2 FY2026, the **vast majority of revenue remains on short-term contract or market pricing**. This is both a risk and an opportunity: it means Sandisk is capturing the full benefit of surging NAND prices (contract prices rose 33–38% QoQ in Q1 2026), but it also means the company is fully exposed to a pricing reversal. The industry-wide shift toward non-cancellable, non-returnable (NCNR) agreements and post-settlement pricing mechanisms — where final payment adjusts to prevailing market prices — is still in its early innings.

The supply-demand imbalance gives Sandisk extraordinary near-term leverage. Management stated demand "continues to exceed supply" and that they were "unable to fulfill demand for our customers" in Q2 FY2026. Customers willing to commit to longer-term agreements receive **priority allocation**; those preferring quarterly negotiations face limited availability. This dynamic favors a gradual shift toward LTAs — but the transition will take years, and cycle risk remains the dominant factor.

## The Kioxia JV gives Sandisk integrated-like economics without integrated capital burden

Sandisk's manufacturing model is its most underappreciated structural advantage. Through Flash Ventures — a joint venture where Sandisk holds **49.9%** and Kioxia holds 50.1% — the company accesses wafers from eight flash manufacturing facilities in Japan (six in Yokkaichi, two in Kitakami) at **cost-plus pricing**. This gives Sandisk wafer economics functionally equivalent to fully integrated manufacturers without bearing the full capital burden of fab ownership.
