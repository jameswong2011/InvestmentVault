# Cross-Procedure Capability in Surgical Robotics

## Original Query
> To what extent can Intuitive Surgical and its competitors in the US surgical robotics market use their machines to do cross procedure surgeries.

---

## Conclusion

The US surgical robotics competitive landscape in 2026 is defined by a paradox: the market is simultaneously more crowded and more concentrated than ever. At least **eight soft-tissue robotic systems** now hold some form of US FDA clearance (da Vinci, Hugo, Versius, Senhance, MIRA, Maestro, Anovo, and Dexter), yet ISRG performs **over 95% of US robotic soft-tissue procedures**. The reason is procedural breadth compounding. ISRG's six-specialty, 85-instrument, 11,000-system platform creates a self-reinforcing cycle where more procedures generate more clinical evidence, which trains more surgeons, which drives more system purchases, which funds more instrument development and indication expansion. Hugo and Ottava represent the most credible long-term threats, but both entered the US with single-indication clearances against a platform that added cardiac surgery to its fifth-generation system in the same month Ottava submitted its first FDA application. The structural under-penetration of robotic surgery—only **20–45% across major specialties**—suggests the market may accommodate multiple winners. But the winners will likely be those that grow the pie rather than take ISRG's slice, competing for the roughly **1.5 million annual US soft-tissue procedures** not yet performed robotically rather than displacing an incumbent whose switching costs are measured not just in dollars but in decades of surgical muscle memory.

# Intuitive Surgical's procedural fortress and the race to challenge it

**Intuitive Surgical dominates US surgical robotics not through a single advantage but through compounding, self-reinforcing moats: FDA clearances across six-plus surgical specialties, an ecosystem of ~85 proprietary instruments, 90,000 trained surgeons, and a 25-year predicate chain that competitors must replicate specialty by specialty.** The first credible US challengers—Medtronic's Hugo (cleared December 2025 for urology only) and J&J's Ottava (De Novo submitted January 2026 for general surgery)—entered the market a full quarter-century behind, each starting with a single indication against ISRG's multi-specialty platform performing **3.15 million da Vinci procedures in 2025** across $10 billion in revenue. The competitive question is not whether rivals can build capable robots—they can—but whether they can close a decade-long procedural breadth gap before ISRG's installed base of **11,106 da Vinci systems** and its razor/razorblade economics make displacement practically impossible.

## Why architectural choices determine procedural destiny

However, fundamental architectural boundaries do exist. **Multi-port systems** (da Vinci Xi/5, Hugo, Versius, Ottava) excel at laparoscopic-type procedures across the abdomen, pelvis, and thorax but cannot access natural luminal pathways. **Single-port systems** (da Vinci SP) address narrow anatomical spaces—the oropharynx, rectum, and deep pelvis—through a single cannula but trade reach for access geometry. **Flexible catheter platforms** (Ion, Monarch, Galaxy) navigate bronchial trees and ureters but cannot manipulate tissue the way rigid robotic arms can. ISRG is the only company operating across all three architectural modalities—multi-port (da Vinci 5/Xi/X), single-port (SP), and flexible endoluminal (Ion)—giving it procedural reach from the bronchial tree to the beating heart.

Each new procedural indication requires a **separate FDA clearance**, even on already-cleared hardware. Da Vinci SP's expansion history illustrates the pace: from urology-only at initial clearance (2018) to six specialty areas over seven years, each requiring clinical evidence and regulatory submission. Competitors face this same sequential process. The **510(k) pathway** (used by 90% of cleared surgical robots) requires demonstration of substantial equivalence to a predicate device, with typical review timelines of 3–12 months per indication. The **De Novo pathway** used by novel systems (Hugo, Ottava, Versius) takes 12–24+ months and costs roughly **$162,000 in FDA user fees** versus $24,000 for 510(k). Once a De Novo device is classified, it creates a new predicate that competitors can reference—but the initial entrant bears all the time and cost burden.

## The orthopedic parallel and the emerging challengers

**Stryker Mako** dominates orthopedic surgical robotics with **3,000+ installed systems** and **1.5 million cumulative procedures**, but it operates in an entirely separate competitive arena from soft-tissue robots. Mako's six FDA-cleared indications—total knee, partial knee, total hip, hip revision (March 2025), spine (2024), and shoulder (November 2024)—all involve image-guided bone preparation using a single haptic robotic arm that constrains the surgeon's movements. This is architecturally incompatible with soft-tissue telemanipulation: Mako has no surgeon console, no multi-arm system, no articulating instruments, and no 3D visualization system. CEO Kevin Lobo has expressed interest in soft-tissue robotics but has made no acquisition or development announcement. Stryker's August 2025 clearance of **Mako RPS**, a handheld robotic power tool for total knee arthroplasty, signals a strategy of extending robotic assistance into ambulatory surgical centers rather than crossing into soft tissue.

**Noah Medical's Galaxy** (cleared March 2023) competes with Ion in robotic bronchoscopy using electromagnetic navigation and integrated digital tomosynthesis. Its MATCH 2 study reported **96.7% diagnostic yield**, and the system features a disposable bronchoscope eliminating cross-contamination risk. However, an **FDA warning letter in April 2025** citing manufacturing deficiencies creates regulatory overhang. **Asensus Surgical** (Senhance system, cleared for general surgery, gynecology, and urology) was **acquired by Karl Storz in August 2024** for $0.35 per share; Karl Storz is developing the next-generation LUNA system. **Virtual Incision's MIRA**, the world's first miniaturized surgical robot (2 pounds, De Novo cleared February 2024 for colectomy), represents a novel approach—deploying through a single umbilical incision to make 90% of US ORs "robot-ready within minutes." **Vicarious Surgical**, developing a single-port robot with VR visualization, is in severe financial distress with only **$9.8 million in cash** and no FDA clearance. **Titan Medical** is effectively defunct, having ceased Enos development in 2023 and completed a reverse merger with Conavi Medical.

Among smaller entrants, **CMR Surgical's Versius Plus** received 510(k) clearance in December 2025 for cholecystectomy, with US commercialization launching in 2026. Internationally, Versius has completed over **45,000 procedures** across gynecology, colorectal, general surgery, thoracic, and urology—making it arguably the second-most-used soft-tissue robotic platform globally. Its compact, modular arms require no dedicated OR and can move between rooms in minutes. CMR raised **$200 million in April 2025** specifically for US market entry but faces the same indication-by-indication expansion challenge as Hugo.

## da Vinci's six-specialty, 85-instrument empire

The instrument ecosystem is the structural backbone of this procedural breadth. ISRG offers approximately **70 multiport instruments** compatible across da Vinci 5, Xi, and X systems, plus **14 SP-specific instruments**, plus Ion catheter and biopsy tools—totaling roughly **85+ distinct instrument types**. These include EndoWrist graspers, needle drivers, monopolar and bipolar cautery, SureForm staplers (30/45/60mm), vessel sealers, Harmonic shears, and da Vinci 5-exclusive Force Feedback instruments that measure push/pull forces at the instrument tip. Each instrument has a programmed use limit, enforcing replacement purchases that generated **$6.02 billion in instruments and accessories revenue in 2025**—60% of total revenue.

The da Vinci platform is FDA-cleared across **six major surgical categories**: urologic, gynecologic, general laparoscopic, thoracoscopic, cardiac (added January 2026 for da Vinci 5), and transoral (head and neck). Within these categories, surgeons perform dozens of specific procedure types—radical prostatectomy, hysterectomy, colectomy, lobectomy, mitral valve repair, and transoral tumor resection, among many others. Intuitive does not publish a single count of discrete FDA-cleared procedures, but the breadth is unmatched: the da Vinci Xi alone carries clearances for adult and pediatric use across all six specialty categories.

## The compounding moat that competitors must unravel

- **Regulatory breadth**: Clearances across six-plus specialties built over 25 years, with each competitor starting from a single indication and facing 5–10 years of sequential expansion to approach parity
- **Instrument lock-in**: ~85 proprietary instruments generating $6 billion annually in recurring revenue, with limited-use enforcement and no third-party alternatives (though Deutsche Bank projects remanufactured instruments could cut US instrument sales by **10–15% by 2028**)
- **Surgeon training network**: 90,000 trained surgeons, residency-level integration so deep that hospitals reportedly decline to hire urologists without da Vinci experience, and a critical-mass effect where surgeons completing 13 cases in 90 days show **>90% likelihood of continued use**
- **Installed base economics**: 11,106 da Vinci systems representing $1–2 million each in sunk capital, plus service contracts of $100,000–$225,000 annually, creating switching costs that are simultaneously financial, operational, and clinical

Competitors are pursuing differentiated entry strategies rather than head-on replication. Medtronic leverages **portfolio bundling and modular economics**—sharing arms across ORs and offering flexible purchasing models suited to mid-sized hospitals priced out of da Vinci. J&J bets on **architectural differentiation**—table-integrated arms and Ethicon instrument familiarity. CMR targets **portability and cost**—no dedicated OR required. Moon Surgical offers **augmentation rather than replacement**, enhancing existing laparoscopic workflows with AI-powered robotic assistance at lower cost. Each strategy implicitly concedes that matching da Vinci's procedural breadth is a multi-year, possibly unachievable goal and instead targets specific market segments where the incumbent's advantages are thinnest.

## Medtronic Hugo and J&J Ottava arrive with narrow beachheads

Hugo's competitive strategy centers on **modularity, cost flexibility, and portfolio bundling**. Arms can be shared across operating rooms. The open surgeon console offers situational awareness advantages. Medtronic can bundle Hugo with its broader surgical product portfolio—including the industry-first robotic LigaSure vessel sealer (CE marked July 2025) and the Touch Surgery digital ecosystem for training and tele-proctoring. However, analysts remain skeptical about near-term US traction: ISRG already commands roughly **83% penetration of US urologic robotic procedures**, and Hugo enters with a single cleared indication against da Vinci's six specialty categories.

**Medtronic Hugo RAS** became the first large medtech company to receive US soft-tissue surgical robot clearance since Intuitive, earning FDA authorization in **December 2025 for urologic procedures only**—prostatectomy, nephrectomy, and cystectomy. The first US commercial case, a robotic prostatectomy, was performed at Cleveland Clinic on February 17, 2026. Hugo's modular design uses **four independently mobile arm carts** (versus da Vinci's boom-mounted architecture), enabling flexible positioning across care settings and potentially multi-quadrant access. Medtronic is pursuing three US indications in parallel: urology (cleared), hernia repair (IDE trial completed with 193 procedures, data under FDA review), and gynecology (IDE trial initiated October 2025). Internationally, Hugo holds CE Mark clearances for urology, gynecology, and general surgery across **35+ countries** and has been used in tens of thousands of procedures since 2021.

**Johnson & Johnson's Ottava** submitted its De Novo FDA application in **January 2026** for general surgery in the upper abdomen—gastric bypass, gastric sleeve, small bowel resection, and hiatal hernia repair. First clinical cases (Roux-en-Y gastric bypass) were completed at Memorial Hermann in early 2025. A second IDE study for inguinal hernia was approved in late 2025. Ottava's most distinctive architectural feature is **table-integrated robotic arms** that deploy when needed and stow beneath a standard surgical table, with "Twin Motion" enabling synchronized patient-robot repositioning during surgery without re-docking. The system exclusively uses Ethicon instruments, leveraging J&J's decades of surgical instrumentation expertise. Analyst estimates for FDA approval range from late 2026 (Leerink Partners) to 2028 or beyond (J.P. Morgan), reflecting uncertainty in the De Novo review timeline. Ottava is not authorized for sale anywhere in the world.
