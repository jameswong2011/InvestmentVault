# Nvidia CES 2026 Presentation: Major Announcements

## New Hardware and Chipset Announcements
 *Jensen Huang showcases Nvidia’s new Vera Rubin platform at CES 2026, revealing next-gen AI chips.* **Nvidia unveiled its “Vera Rubin” architecture – a full platform of six new chips that together form a powerful AI supercomputer.** Named after astronomer Vera Rubin, this platform succeeds the Blackwell generation and includes the **Nvidia Vera CPU, Rubin GPU, NVLink 6 switch, ConnectX-9 SuperNIC, BlueField-4 DPU, and Spectrum-6 Ethernet**. Through extreme hardware/software co-design, Vera Rubin can train massive *mixture-of-experts* AI models as fast as Blackwell but far more efficiently. In fact, Nvidia claims **up to 10× lower inference cost per token and 4× fewer GPUs needed to train large models** compared to the previous-gen platform. Production is starting ahead of schedule, and industry support is strong – cloud providers and AI labs (AWS, Microsoft, CoreWeave, Meta, OpenAI, and others) plan to adopt Rubin, with leaders like Sam Altman and Mark Zuckerberg praising its potential.

**Nvidia also highlighted new GeForce RTX 50-series graphics hardware for consumers.** While no brand-new GPU SKUs were launched on-stage, partners **showed off new RTX 50 Series cards and laptops** built on the 3rd-gen Blackwell GPU architecture. The flagship **GeForce RTX 5090** and **5080** cards were noted to hit shelves by late January 2026, delivering roughly **2× the performance of their 40-series predecessors** thanks to Blackwell’s advances and DLSS 4 technology. New ultra-thin **RTX 50-Series laptops** were also showcased, featuring **Max-Q innovations that boost battery life by up to 40%** versus last-gen models. These Blackwell-based notebooks deliver **double the gaming performance of prior generation laptops** while staying as slim as 15 mm, enabled by improved power gating and frequency scaling techniques. Together, these hardware announcements underscore Nvidia’s push for faster, more efficient GPUs in both data centers and personal devices.

## AI and Machine Learning Technologies
**Artificial intelligence took center stage in Nvidia’s CES presentation, with new models and tools introduced for developers and creators.** The company rolled out the **Nvidia Nemotron 3** family of open-source AI models, which are ideal for fine-tuning *agentic AI* workflows on local GPUs. These models (available on GitHub) allow developers to customize AI agents without needing cloud-scale resources, reflecting Nvidia’s emphasis on *AI everywhere*. In addition, Nvidia announced an **Inference Context Memory** system using BlueField-4 DPUs to accelerate reasoning in AI agents, and an expanded **AI Enterprise software stack** in collaboration with Red Hat, optimized for the new Rubin platform. This end-to-end stack – from chips to software – is aimed at making AI development and deployment more efficient for enterprises.

Content creators also saw new AI-powered capabilities. **Nvidia demonstrated upgrades for AI-driven content creation**, such as **LTX-2 for 4K AI video generation and enhancements to the NVIDIA Broadcast app**. These tools leverage RTX GPU Tensor Cores to drastically speed up tasks like video editing, live noise removal, and virtual background effects. For example, the latest update enables creators to generate high-resolution video with AI in a fraction of the time previously required. Overall, Nvidia’s announcements underscored how its AI R&D – from foundational models to creative tools – is accelerating machine learning across industries.

## Automotive Technologies and Partnerships
**Self-driving car technology was a major focus of Nvidia’s CES 2026 showcase, as the company positioned itself as a leader in AI-powered automotive solutions.** Nvidia announced that its open autonomous vehicle stack, including the new **Nvidia Alpamayo** AI model portfolio, will enable **Level 4 self-driving capabilities** in production vehicles. Alpamayo is a suite of *reasoning-based* AI models, simulation blueprints, and datasets for autonomous driving. “Not only does it take sensor input and activate the steering, brakes, and acceleration, it also **reasons about what action it is about to take**,” CEO Jensen Huang explained. This represents a shift to *AI “thinking” cars*, as Alpamayo’s vision-language-action networks can make driving decisions and explain their logic for safety. To support this, Nvidia introduced **NVIDIA Halos**, a safety and cybersecurity framework that validates AI driving decisions and runs a parallel fail-safe driving system – ensuring that if the AI’s plan seems unsafe, a backup traditional ADAS logic takes over.

Leading automakers are embracing Nvidia’s automotive platform. Notably, **Mercedes-Benz announced its upcoming 2026 CLA coupe will be the first production car to ship with Nvidia’s entire AV stack, including the Alpamayo AI co-pilot**. This car will initially offer hands-off “Level 2++” driver assistance on most roads (with eyes-on monitoring), and it’s **planned to upgrade via software to full Level 4 autonomy over time**. Nvidia also expanded its **DRIVE Hyperion** reference platform – a modular Level-4-ready suite of in-vehicle computers, sensors, and software for autonomous driving. At CES, the company announced an expanded ecosystem of tier-1 suppliers, sensor makers, and integrators (including Aeva, Bosch, Magna, Sony and more) **developing hardware compatible with DRIVE Hyperion for robotaxis and autonomous trucks**. This ensures automakers can source *ready-made Hyperion-compliant cameras, LiDARs, radar and central computers* to accelerate their self-driving car programs. *“Everything that moves will eventually become autonomous, and DRIVE Hyperion is the backbone that makes that transition possible,”* said Ali Kani, Nvidia’s VP of automotive. 

To further catalyze autonomous driving R&D, **Nvidia open-sourced key parts of its self-driving software**. The **Alpamayo 1** driving model (a 10-billion-parameter vision-LM for “world modeling”) is now available to the research community on Hugging Face. Nvidia also released **AlpaSim**, a fully open-source simulation engine for testing AVs in virtual scenarios, and **open driving datasets** covering 1,700+ hours of diverse real-world driving to help train and evaluate these AI drivers. Together, these moves – open models, an expanding partner network, and a production car launch – show Nvidia’s aggressive push into the autonomous vehicle arena, directly taking on incumbents like Tesla’s Full Self-Driving. As one headline put it, *“Watch out, Tesla – Nvidia is coming for your lunch.”*

## Robotics and Embedded Systems
 *Nvidia’s partners unveiled next-generation robots powered by Nvidia AI – from industrial humanoids to surgical robots (images courtesy of Caterpillar, LEM Surgical, AGIBOT, and Franka Emika).* **Robotics received a huge boost from Nvidia at CES 2026, as the company announced a full-stack “physical AI” ecosystem for general-purpose robots.** Nvidia’s vision is to enable “**generalist**” robots that can learn many tasks, not just single-purpose machines. To that end, Jensen Huang declared *“the ChatGPT moment for robotics is here”*, as Nvidia introduced open AI *foundation models* that allow robots to **see, reason, and act with human-like understanding of the real world**. 

Central to this is a set of new **open-source AI models for robots**, all available via Hugging Face. These include **NVIDIA Cosmos 2.5** (Transfer and Predict) – world models for generating synthetic training data and evaluating robot policies in simulation. There’s also **Cosmos Reason 2**, a *reasoning* vision-language model that lets robots interpret their environment and plan actions. Building on that, Nvidia unveiled **Isaac GR00T (N1.6)** – a next-gen Vision-Language-Action model purpose-built for humanoid robots. GR00T uses Cosmos Reason as its “brain” to enable whole-body control: humanoids can coordinate arms, legs, and vision to manipulate objects and navigate simultaneously. Early adopters like Franka Robotics, NEURA Robotics, and Humanoid are already using GR00T models to train new robot behaviors.

To validate these AI brains, **Nvidia launched Isaac Lab-Arena**, an open-source simulation framework on GitHub for safe, scalable robot testing. Lab-Arena provides realistic environments, standardized tasks, and benchmarks (e.g. RoboCasa, RoboTwin) so researchers can rigorously evaluate robot skills in VR before real-world deployment. Nvidia also introduced **OSMO**, an open-edge orchestration tool that links data generation, training, and deployment across cloud and robot hardware – essentially a “command center” for robotics workflows. All these pieces – open models, sim tools, workflows – aim to reduce the cost and complexity of building intelligent machines.

On the hardware side, **Nvidia revealed the Jetson T4000**, a new edge AI module powered by the Blackwell GPU architecture. Part of the Jetson “Thor” family, the T4000 delivers **4× greater energy efficiency** and about **1,200 FP4 teraOPS of AI compute** in a tiny form factor. It comes with 64 GB of memory and operates in a 40–70 W power envelope – a cost-effective upgrade path for autonomous machines in the field. Priced at ~$1,999 (in 1k-unit volumes), Jetson T4000 brings supercomputer-class AI to robots at the edge. At CES, several partners – **Boston Dynamics, Caterpillar, LG, Franka Emika, and others – unveiled new robots built on Nvidia’s stack**, from agile mobile manipulators to heavy-duty industrial robots. This includes humanoid robots now integrating the high-end **Jetson AGX Thor** compute, giving them the brainpower for real-time vision and motion planning.

Nvidia is also fostering a community approach. It **deepened its partnership with Hugging Face** to connect Nvidia’s 2 million+ developers with Hugging Face’s 13 million AI researchers. Notably, Hugging Face’s open-source **“LeRobot”** platform – and its *Reachy 2* humanoid robot – now support direct deployment on Nvidia Jetson hardware. This allows robot makers to experiment with Nvidia’s AI models using affordable robots, without being locked into proprietary systems. The strategy is clear: Nvidia wants to become *the default platform for robotics*, akin to how Android became ubiquitous for smartphones. Early signs show momentum, with robotics now the fastest-growing category on Hugging Face and many companies (from **Boston Dynamics to Caterpillar**) already relying on Nvidia’s tech for their robots. By providing everything from chips to pretrained brains, Nvidia is positioning itself as the one-stop hub for the coming wave of intelligent robots.

## Gaming Innovations and Graphics Technologies
Gaming enthusiasts were not left out – **Nvidia announced a slew of updates to its GeForce gaming ecosystem** that promise better performance, visuals, and experiences for gamers and creators alike. **Foremost was the debut of NVIDIA DLSS 4.5**, the latest version of Nvidia’s AI-powered Deep Learning Super Sampling. DLSS 4.5 introduces Nvidia’s **2nd-generation Super Resolution transformer model**, which further improves image quality and reduces artifacts compared to DLSS 4. It also adds a new **6X Dynamic Multi Frame Generation** capability for RTX 50-series GPUs, which can generate up to five intermediate frames per rendered frame, enabling smooth gameplay at 4K and 240+ FPS with ray tracing. *Over 400 games and applications* already support DLSS 4/4.5 as of CES, making it Nvidia’s fastest-adopted gaming tech to date. All GeForce RTX GPU owners can upgrade to DLSS 4.5 Super Resolution right now via the latest GeForce Game Ready Driver and NVIDIA app update.

Nvidia also spotlighted upcoming **RTX enhancements in big-name games**. Several anticipated titles – including *007: First Light, Phantom Blade Zero, PRAGMATA,* and *Resident Evil: Requiem* – will launch with support for **full ray tracing and DLSS 4** features. In fact, **Capcom’s PRAGMATA and the new Resident Evil will feature path-traced graphics with DLSS**, pushing visual fidelity to new heights. Additionally, **NVIDIA ACE (Avatar Cloud Engine) is making its way into games** to enable smarter AI characters and assistants. Nvidia showed a tech demo in *Total War: PHARAOH* where an AI advisor (powered by ACE’s generative models) helps players navigate complex strategy mechanics. And in the popular *PUBG: Battlegrounds*, an upcoming “Ally” update will introduce an AI squadmate that learns and improves over time using Nvidia ACE’s long-term memory and dialogue capabilities. These demos underscore how **Nvidia’s AI tech can enhance gameplay** not just via graphics, but through more intelligent game worlds.

On the display front, **Nvidia launched its next generation of G-SYNC monitors, dubbed “G-SYNC Pulsar.”** This new class of esports-grade monitors uses innovative **variable backlight strobing** to achieve an unprecedented **1,000 Hz of effective motion clarity**, eliminating virtually all blur and tearing. The first G-SYNC Pulsar models (27-inch, 1440p 360Hz IPS panels from Acer, AOC, ASUS, and MSI) hit the market on Jan. 7, starting around $599. They also feature **Nvidia’s new Ambient Adaptive technology**, with built-in light sensors that automatically adjust screen brightness and color temperature based on your room lighting. *In bright daylight, the monitor boosts brightness and shifts to cooler tones; at night it lowers brightness and warms the colors to reduce eye strain,* Nvidia says. This adaption ensures optimal visuals and comfort across all environments.

Other gaming announcements included **NVIDIA RTX Remix “Runtime”** – a tool to help modders inject modern lighting effects into classic games. An upcoming update will let the community use RTX Remix to add *dynamic lighting, physics-based materials, and AI enhancements* into older game engines, breathing new life into mods. Nvidia also released a **GeForce NOW expansion**, bringing its cloud gaming service to more platforms. **Native GeForce NOW apps for Linux PCs and Amazon Fire TV** were announced, enabling high-performance cloud gaming without a console or GPU. The Linux beta will start with Ubuntu 24.04, and the Fire TV app will support 4K streaming on devices like the Fire TV Stick 4K Max. Additionally, GeForce NOW is adding support for flight simulator peripherals (HOTAS joysticks and yokes) – just in time for *Microsoft Flight Simulator 2024* – meaning cloud gamers can plug in their Thrustmaster or Logitech flight controls and have them work seamlessly over the cloud. Finally, Nvidia’s software updates for gamers and streamers are rolling out: **a new Game Ready Driver** with day-one optimizations and **an NVIDIA Broadcast app update** that adds features like eye-contact correction and VLOG filters, leveraging AI to improve live streams. From faster GPUs and smarter AI allies to ultra-fast displays and cloud gaming, Nvidia’s CES gaming news aimed to supercharge both the performance and the experience of gaming.

## Enterprise and Data Center Developments
**For enterprise and data-center customers, Nvidia’s CES announcements signaled a leap forward in AI infrastructure and partnerships.** The highlight was the **NVIDIA Vera Rubin platform** – an end-to-end AI supercomputing solution launching now for hyperscalers and research labs. By combining six specialized chips (CPU, GPU, DPU, NIC, switch, etc.), Rubin systems can be scaled to train and deploy the largest AI models at lower cost. For instance, Microsoft revealed that its next-gen *“Fairwater”* AI super-factories will use Nvidia **Vera Rubin NVL72** rack-scale systems, ultimately scaling to “hundreds of thousands” of Vera Rubin superchips across their data centers. Cloud provider CoreWeave is another early adopter, planning to offer Rubin-based instances via its Mission Control service. To support broad rollout, **Nvidia is collaborating with Red Hat** to deliver a fully optimized software stack (RHEL, OpenShift, and AI frameworks) for Rubin deployments. This ensures enterprise customers can seamlessly integrate Rubin hardware with existing IT and cloud environments.

The **performance claims for Rubin are substantial**. Thanks to innovations like 6th-gen NVLink interconnects, a new Transformer Engine, and support for confidential computing, a Rubin-based cluster can **run advanced AI workloads far more efficiently**. Nvidia states that **inference on huge language models costs up to 10× less per token on Rubin vs. Blackwell**, and that Rubin can train massive *mixture-of-experts* models using only a quarter of the GPUs previously needed. This is a crucial advantage as models scale to trillions of parameters. Real-world validation came from Nvidia’s partners: **OpenAI’s CEO Sam Altman** emphasized that *“Intelligence scales with compute… The NVIDIA Rubin platform helps us keep scaling so advanced intelligence benefits everyone.”* Likewise, Meta’s CEO Mark Zuckerberg noted Rubin’s step-change in efficiency will be key to deploying powerful AI models to billions of users. Such endorsements, along with support from Anthropic, Google, Oracle, Dell, HPE and more, highlight **Nvidia’s central role in the next generation of AI infrastructure**.

Beyond Rubin, Nvidia touched on other enterprise updates. They announced that **BlueField-4 DPUs** (data processing units) are now sampling to customers, bringing secure acceleration for cloud workloads. In one example, Nvidia’s BlueField tech is being integrated into a **Cybersecurity “validated design”** for AI factories, offloading encryption and AI inference to secure sensitive data in real time. Nvidia also mentioned **NVIDIA IGX and MGX** servers making progress in industrial edge deployments, and showcased partners using **NVIDIA Omniverse** for digital twins in manufacturing (underscoring the breadth of enterprise use cases). Overall, the message was clear: from cloud data centers to the network edge, **Nvidia is providing the hardware and software to power AI-driven business** – and doing so with an expanding ecosystem of partners.

Underpinning these announcements is a clear message: **Nvidia sees AI as the defining technology of our time, and it is positioning itself as an indispensable leader and partner in that future.** Whether it’s through silicon (GPUs, DPUs), software (CUDA, AI frameworks), or whole platforms (robotics, vehicles, cloud), Nvidia is aligning with key players across the tech ecosystem.
