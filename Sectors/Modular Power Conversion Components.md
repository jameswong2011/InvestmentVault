## Key industry questions

- Is the **architectural fork at ~1,500–2,000 A** (lateral multiphase buck → vertical Power-on-Package) a 5–7 year IP-gated transition that re-cuts the share map back to specialist module vendors (Vicor, Flex, eventual MPS clean-sheet), or does GaN/SiC + IVR (integrated voltage regulator) silicon innovation flatten the gap inside 18–24 months and let lateral incumbents (MPWR, TXN, Renesas, ADI) hold their H100-era sockets through Rubin Ultra?
- Does **40× current multiplication** at the final converter (Vicor 2nd-gen VPD spec disclosed Q1 2026) become the de facto industry requirement for AI accelerators above 2,000 A — and if so, how durable is the multi-year gap between Vicor (40×) and IVR-based competitors (~2×) given that closing it requires both new switching topology *and* new packaging?
- Will **co-packaged optics (CPO)** at NVIDIA Quantum-X (early 2026) and Spectrum-X Photonics (2H 2026) rewrite the H100-era power-vendor pecking order for switching silicon — micro-ring resonators shifting 80 pm/°C make a lateral converter's adjacent-chiplet thermal disturbance a wavelength-stability problem, not just an efficiency problem — pulling power-module placement under the package bottom regardless of cost economics?
- Does the **800V→6V distribution architecture** circulating in some industry research (single-step conversion from rack-level high voltage directly to processor-near low voltage) supplant Vicor's preferred 800V→48V→0.7V chain — or does the I²R penalty at the final segment (6V vs 48V baseline = 64× wiring loss) force the industry back to 48V on-board even at 800V upstream?
- Are **ITC Limited Exclusion Orders** (Vicor's first LEO Oct 2024; second ITC final determination expected 2027; Patrizio's "no two without three" hint) a one-time enforcement window or a structural rents engine — and does the Federal Circuit appeal currently in play (timing TBD) narrow the LEO scope or hold it intact for the FY27–FY28 royalty acceleration?
- Does the broader **PMIC market** (Texas Instruments ~12.5% global revenue; Monolithic Power Systems, Analog Devices, Renesas, Infineon, ON Semi, ST, NXP) consolidate or fragment as the AI-density product line bifurcates — top-of-stack chip-level high-density (Vicor, Flex, post-clean-sheet MPS) vs. mid-stack PMIC (commodity buck + LDO) where IDM scale and automotive cross-subsidy still dominate?
- What is the durable **IDM advantage** vs **module specialist** advantage in this category over 2027–2030 — does TXN's $32B revenue + 300mm fab base let it walk down the density curve faster than Vicor can scale its Andover ChiP fab, or does the ChiP / 3Di / FPA stack remain a separate category that IDMs can't replicate without committing to a non-silicon manufacturing line?

## Industry history

The category sits at the intersection of three legacy industries — **discrete power semiconductors** (1950s–present), **switched-mode power supplies** (1970s–present), and **integrated PMICs** (2000s–present) — and was reshaped by AI-accelerator current density crossing thresholds that none of the three predecessor architectures could handle without re-decomposition.

**Discrete power era (1950s–1990s).** Bipolar power transistors (RCA, Motorola, GE, Toshiba) dominated 1950s–1970s; Texas Instruments and Motorola built their analog franchises on this base. The 1970s introduction of vertical-MOSFET switching transistors (International Rectifier, founded 1947 El Segundo CA) compressed switching loss enough to make switched-mode power supplies (SMPS) commercially viable, displacing linear regulators in computing and telecom. By the 1990s, the architecture was settled: a discrete MOSFET + discrete inductor + discrete capacitor + discrete controller IC, sitting on a PCB as the "buck regulator" delivering processor core voltage.

**Brick-form module era (1981–2010s).** Vicor (Patrizio Vinciarelli, 1981 Andover MA) commercialized the first standardized "brick" DC-DC converters for industrial / aerospace / defense — taking a high-voltage input (48V or 380V) and outputting regulated low voltage in a fixed-form-factor package with magnetics + switches + control inside one molded module. The brick form factor became the industry standard for industrial power: Vicor V·I Brick / Maxi / Mini / Micro families through the 1990s; competitors emerged (Lambda Electronics, Lineage Power, Bel Fuse). The 1990s shift to multi-phase buck for processor power kept module vendors at the *industrial* layer; the *computing* layer was won by discrete-MOSFET multiphase architectures from International Rectifier (acquired by Infineon 2014, $3B), Linear Technology (acquired by Analog Devices 2017, $14.8B), and Maxim Integrated (acquired by Analog Devices 2021, $21B).

**Integrated PMIC consolidation (2000s–2020s).** Monolithic Power Systems (Michael Hsing, 1997 San Jose) pioneered single-chip integration of MOSFET + control + protection on a CMOS process — the first competitive challenge to discrete-component multiphase buck. Texas Instruments built the broadest analog/PMIC catalog through organic R&D + the 2011 National Semiconductor acquisition ($6.5B). Analog Devices consolidated specialty analog through Linear Technology (2017) + Maxim (2021). Renesas (formed 2003 from Hitachi/Mitsubishi/NEC merger, IPO 2010) absorbed Intersil (2017, $3.2B) for analog/PMIC depth. Infineon (1999 Siemens spin-out) acquired International Rectifier (2014) and Cypress Semiconductor (2020, $9B) for power-discrete + microcontroller franchises. By 2022, the global PMIC market was a five-IDM oligopoly (TI, ADI, Infineon, Renesas, ON Semi) plus MPS at the high-performance computing edge — total ~$50B annual revenue, 8–12% growth, automotive + industrial + consumer base.

**Vicor's H100 displacement (2022–2024).** Through V100 (2017) and A100 (2020), NVIDIA's reference designs used Vicor 48V→core-voltage VPD modules — the founder's "Factorized Power Architecture" published 2014 was the architectural template. The H100 (2022 production) shifted to a lateral multiphase architecture from MPS at ~700A core current; the public framing was cost + lateral integration ease. Vicor's product revenue stagnated at ~$330M FY2022, the stock collapsed from $145 (Dec 2021) to $35 (Oct 2023), and securities-fraud class actions filed (DiCello Levitt, Kessler Topaz, Rosen, 2024) cemented the "Vicor lost the AI cycle" narrative. The competing private framing (founder discipline, walking from un-economic margins) was ignored. The Compal Rubin NVL8 GTC demonstration (March 2026), Vicor's $300M backlog at +70% YoY (Q1 2026), and the management disclosure that 2nd-gen VPD specs (3 A/mm² / 40× / 1.5 mm) are now in production with a lead customer reframed the period in retrospect as Vinciarelli refusing the H100 to preserve margin while accumulating IP for the current-density regime where lateral could not follow.

**ITC Limited Exclusion Order (Oct 2024 → present).** Vicor's first ITC complaint produced a Limited Exclusion Order in October 2024, allowing US Customs to seize imports of products infringing the VPD patent portfolio. Delta / Cyntec / Foxconn settled rather than appealed ($45M booked 2025); $57M of organic licensing revenue followed (+23% YoY). A second ITC complaint is in process, with final determination expected 2027 — Vicor's FY2026 $570M revenue guide assumes *zero* new licensing agreements ahead of that determination, signaling deliberate delay to maximize exclusion-order leverage at settlement. Patrizio's Q1 2026 call commentary ("In Italy we say there is no two without three") flagged a third ITC case in queue. The LEO converted the H100 socket loss from a pure share-loss outcome into a partial-rents outcome: any infringing module crossing US Customs becomes Vicor royalty revenue at "nearly 100%" gross margin — a structural feature absent from discrete + integrated PMIC competitors who lack the underlying module-architecture patents.

**AI-density inflection (2024–present).** NVIDIA roadmap density compressed from ~12-month cadence (Hopper 2022 → Blackwell 2024 → Blackwell Ultra 2025 → Vera Rubin H2 2026 → Rubin Ultra H2 2027), with per-processor current scaling from H100's ~700 A through GB200's ~1,200 A, GB300's ~1,500 A, Rubin's ~2,000 A, and Rubin Ultra's projected 2,500–3,000 A peak. P = I²R loss in the "last inch" of PCB copper at 70 µΩ trace resistance scales quadratically: 800 A dissipates 45 W as heat *before* current reaches the die; 1,500 A dissipates 158 W. Multiphase buck's response — adding phases — consumes the very PCB area driving the loss problem. Above ~1,500–2,000 A, the architecture self-limits, forcing a return to vertical placement under the processor (Vicor's home turf since the V100 era). Co-packaged optics (NVIDIA Quantum-X early 2026, Spectrum-X Photonics 2H 2026) compounds the architectural pressure: optical I/O claims the package topside, pushing power conversion to the bottom regardless of cost. The category is now in mid-transition between the lateral PMIC era (won by MPS / TI / ADI on cost + integration) and the vertical module era (Vicor's IP-gated stronghold), with the share trajectory in 2027–2028 dependent on whether IDM competitors can reverse-engineer the Vicor packaging/topology stack inside the multi-year reverse-engineering clock.

## Competitive dynamics

The sector splits cleanly into **module specialists** (Vicor, Flex, and a long tail of brick-form vendors) competing on density and IP, and **integrated PMIC IDMs** (TI, ADI, MPS, Renesas, Infineon, ON Semi, ST, NXP) competing on cost, breadth, and scale. The cross-franchise competition is asymmetric: IDMs have $20–40B revenue scale and 300mm CMOS fabs but lack the proprietary packaging required for >1,000 A modules; module specialists have the packaging IP but lack the silicon scale that lets IDMs amortize R&D across automotive + industrial + consumer.

**The structural observation: the share map by current regime is more useful than the share map by vendor.** Above ~1,500 A, the lateral architecture self-limits regardless of which silicon vendor chooses it, so the procurement question shifts from "MPS vs Vicor on price" to "lateral vs vertical on whether the design closes thermals at all." MPS holds the H100-era socket structurally — Vicor cannot displace a working lateral incumbent at 700–1,500 A on cost — but cannot follow the architecture above 2,000 A without a clean-sheet vertical product that doesn't exist in production today.

**Customer channel concentration.** No major vendor in the category discloses customer mix by name. Triangulation from earnings commentary + ODM disclosures + NVIDIA reference design language suggests Vicor is ~20–40% NVIDIA-direct + indirect through Compal/Foxconn/Quanta/Wiwynn AI accelerator card OEMs; MPS is ~30–40% NVIDIA-related at present given H100/H200/GB200/GB300 multiphase content; TXN / ADI / Renesas / Infineon are <10% AI-density-specific each, with majority revenue in automotive + industrial + consumer. **New-entrant threat mapping.** The credible threats are not in scale silicon (where IDM capex + IP depth are prohibitive) but at the architectural-fork edges: (i) **MPS clean-sheet vertical** — most-watched vector; if MPS commits R&D to a vertical product, Vicor's IP buys 5–7 years but not infinity; (ii) **Flex VPD product line** (announced 2025) — only credible second-source vertical vendor today; manufacturing-led, will likely win Rubin dual-source slots regardless of Vicor's primary win; (iii) **TI 800VDC reference architecture** — TI is positioning as the silicon Tier-1 for the rack-down 800V chain, not the chip-level module — adjacent rather than direct, but creates wholesale-channel competition for Vicor's 800V→48V bus conversion IP; (iv) **Hyperscaler in-source** — Meta authored OCP power-shelf guidelines, Google internal designs cover spec but not manufacturing; the structural bound is reverse-engineering ChiP/3Di without buying the fab.

## Product level analysis

The category decomposes into seven product families covering the power chain from rack-level high voltage through processor core voltage. Each family has distinct competitive structure.

| Product family | Function | Voltage / current | Leader(s) | Moat source |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Bus converters (800V→48V)** | Rack-to-board high-ratio step-down | 800V → 48V at 5–10 kW | Vicor BCM, Delta, Flex | Patent IP at 800V high-ratio fixed-K; Vicor proprietary topology |
| **Pre-regulators (PRM)** | Stable factorized bus | 48V regulated → 48V factorized | Vicor (proprietary FPA), MPS (proprietary), TI / ADI (multi-phase) | Control loop accuracy + thermal; not architecturally unique |
| **Multiphase buck (lateral)** | 48V/12V→0.7V at <1,500 A | 48V or 12V → 0.7V at 50–100 A/phase | MPS (H100/H200/GB200 socket), TXN, Renesas, Infineon | Silicon integration density + cost |
| **Trans-inductor voltage regulator (TLVR)** | Magnetic-coupled multiphase refinement | 48V/12V → 0.7V at <1,500 A | MPS (extension product), TI (catalog) | Magnetic design + control |
| **Lateral Power Delivery (LPD) module** | Module-form lateral converter, side-of-package | 48V → 0.7V at 200–800 A | Vicor (Gen 4 LPD), Flex, others | Module packaging; ~800 A ceiling structural |
| **Vertical Power Delivery (VPD)** | Module-form vertical converter, under-package | 48V → 0.7V at 500–1,500 A continuous, 2,000 A peak | **Vicor (Gen 5 / 2nd-gen 2026)**, Flex (catch-up); MPS + TI watching | Vicor 5–7yr IP moat — FPA + SAC + ChiP + 3Di stack |
| **Integrated Voltage Regulator (IVR)** | On-die voltage regulation | Direct → 0.7V at ~2× multiplication | Intel research, Apple (M-series internal), Renesas/MPS catalog | On-die capacitor/inductor IP; IDM-only |

**Vicor 2nd-generation VPD (disclosed Q1 2026)** — Power-on-Package module placed directly under the processor on the opposite side of the substrate. First public disclosure of three integrated specs:

- **Current density: 3 A/mm²** — Gen 4 was 2 A/mm²; Vinciarelli described competitors at this layer as "inadequate" without naming. The improvement comes from ChiP packaging + double-sided cooling + 3Di stacked-magnetic layers.
- **Current multiplication: 40×** — at K=1/40, delivering 2,000 A to the processor requires only a 50 A bus feed. IVR competitors achieve ~2×, which would require a 1,000 A bus feed for the same load (impractical thermals + thick wiring).
- **Package thickness: 1.5 mm** — buyers (NVIDIA, Google per APEC analyst commentary) reportedly asking suppliers for <3 mm; 1.5 mm is half that ceiling. Vinciarelli on the Q1 2026 call: "We're not stopping at 1.5 millimeter, we're going thinner."
- **Output impedance: 0.8 mΩ at the VTM stage** — order of magnitude below conventional multiphase; flat to ~1 MHz. Combined with SAC's <1 µs load step response, makes 2,000 A/µs transients tractable without massive decoupling capacitance.

**Vicor Factorized Power Architecture (FPA)** — physical separation of regulation (PRM) from transformation (VTM/GCM). Regulation needs feedback-loop stability (controller benefits from being away from processor switching noise); transformation handles large current and heat (benefits from being directly under the processor to minimize PDN distance). Monolithic regulators cannot optimize for both. FPA system efficiency 90–95% combined PRM+VTM, vs ~85–88% for high-end multiphase buck at equivalent load.

**Vicor Sine Amplitude Converter (SAC) topology** — fixed-ratio resonant converter switched at zero current and zero voltage crossings (ZCS+ZVS). Eliminates switching loss; produces sinusoidal current waveform with low harmonic content. Operates at fixed ratio (no variable feedback loop), so regulation must be upstream at PRM. Result: 97% efficiency single-stage, ~3.5 MHz effective switching frequency, output Z flat to ~1 MHz, sub-microsecond load step response. The trade-off — fixed ratio means SAC alone cannot produce variable output voltage — is solved by the FPA decomposition where PRM handles variable regulation upstream of the fixed-ratio SAC transformation.

**Vicor ChiP (Converter housed in Package) packaging** — proprietary panel-level manufacturing process (wafer-fab-style yield management on substrate panels, not silicon wafers), double-sided cooling (halves thermal impedance vs single-sided), grounded metal shielding for EMI containment, and stacked-ChiP topology for the multilayer integration that GCM requires. The fab process is the moat: stacked ChiP is what makes a single module the size of a small chip deliver 1,000 A. Andover Fab 1 nameplate revised from $1B to $1.5B in Q1 2026 via cycle-time reduction at bottleneck steps + adjacent-building offload; second 3Di line installation Q3/Q4 2026.

**Monolithic Power Systems (MPWR) multiphase + integrated buck** — incumbent in the lateral architecture serving H100 / H200 / GB200 / GB300. Won the H100 socket on cost + lateral integration ease; product GM ~58–60%; AI accelerator power share estimated 15–20% by FinancialContent. Strategic question for 2027–2028: whether MPS commits R&D to a clean-sheet vertical product, given Jensen Huang's own April 2026 framing that ASIC margin (Broadcom XPUs) is ~65% vs Nvidia GPU ~70% — a structurally similar Vicor 50–55% product GM threshold suggests vertical entry would be margin-accretive vs lateral commoditization. The IP barrier (FPA + SAC + ChiP + 3Di stack) buys Vicor 5–7 years; MPS has the engineering depth to attempt the vertical attack but has not committed publicly.

**Texas Instruments (TXN) 800VDC reference architecture** — unveiled March 2026 alongside NVIDIA reference platforms. Positions TI as the silicon Tier-1 for the rack-down 800V chain (silicon switches, GaN controllers, power modules) rather than the chip-level module. Strategic adjacency rather than direct competition: if 800V→48V wins as upstream architecture, TI captures bus-conversion silicon TAM; Vicor still owns the 48V→0.7V final-segment module. The 800V→6V camp (single-step from 800V to processor-near low voltage, with Vicor as architect-rebutter rather than implementer) is the alternate path that could commoditize the 48V intermediate stage if it materializes.

**Analog Devices (ADI) μModule** — Linear-Technology-legacy module-format silicon; closest IDM analog to Vicor's brick form. Power range 5–60 A typical; integrated inductor + control + switches in a 9–15 mm SMT package. Differs from Vicor VPD in topology (multiphase buck inside the module, not factorized) and current ceiling (10× lower at peak). ADI's strength is automotive + industrial breadth; HPC density entry would require new fab + topology rather than scale-up of μModule.

**Flex VPD product line (announced 2025)** — only credible second-source vertical vendor in production today. Manufacturing-led (Flex's contract-manufacturing scale + supplier network) rather than IP-led; lacks Vicor's SAC + 3Di stack. Likely Rubin dual-source candidate — NVIDIA typically dual-sources critical components, and Flex is the obvious second source given its existing ODM relationships with Compal / Foxconn / Quanta / Wiwynn that already buy Vicor for lead positions. The strategic implication: Flex captures partial socket content even where Vicor wins primary, compressing Vicor's per-system content from 100% to 50–60%.

**Brick-form legacy products (Vicor V·I Brick, Maxi/Mini/Micro; Bel Fuse legacy; Lambda Electronics)** — industrial / aerospace / defense module families with 30+ year heritage. Lower power density (10–500 W typical), higher reliability ratings (10⁹+ MTBF hours), longer product lifecycles (10–20 years per design). Vicor "Brick" segment: $48M Q1 2026 (~43% revenue mix), +7.7% QoQ; serves factory automation, EV powertrains, aerospace/defense (Lockheed, Boeing, BAE 30+yr installed base). Mid-single-digit growth, lower-margin (~30–35% GM), counter-cyclical to AI capex — provides revenue floor in a 2027–2028 AI capex slowdown.

**Strategic pattern:** the IDM consolidation wave (2014–2021) bought scale + breadth at the IC layer (TI / ADI / Infineon / Renesas), but no IDM bought the *module* layer where Vicor competes. The Linear Technology + Maxim acquisitions are the closest — ADI's μModule franchise is module-format silicon — but architecturally distinct from Vicor's vertical-current-multiplier topology. The IDM consolidation logic ($14B–$21B per deal, 6–8x revenue) reflects scale economics on shared manufacturing + global sales channels; the Vicor IP moat is structurally separate because the module specialist can't be acquired in (without buying the fab + the founder + the IP all at once) and the IDM module products can't replicate Vicor's specs without committing to a non-silicon manufacturing line.

**The "no Vicor acquisition" question.** At $13B market cap (April 2026) with founder Vinciarelli holding ~80% voting power via dual-class structure (10 votes per Class B share, 11.7M Class B shares, 47% economic + 80% voting), Vicor is not a typical acquisition target — the 80% voting block makes hostile bids structurally impossible, and a friendly acquirer would need Vinciarelli's specific consent. The most plausible acquirer set (TI, ADI, MPS, Infineon, Renesas) all have automotive cross-subsidy that Vicor doesn't share, making integration synergies hard to underwrite at the $20–30B premium that would clear the founder. The category therefore stays bifurcated for as long as Vinciarelli holds the founder block — which constrains industry consolidation to the IDM side.

Status (April 2026): no public commitment; lateral product line continues serving H100/H200/GB200/GB300. 2. **Flex VPD product line** (manufacturing-led, near-term threat): announced 2025; first products in customer evaluation. Strategic role: dual-source for NVIDIA Rubin / Rubin Ultra against Vicor primary; not architecturally differentiated. Compresses Vicor's per-system content from 100% to 50–60% but does not threaten the architectural moat.

3. **Hyperscaler in-source** (slow-bleed, structural): Meta authored OCP DC Power Shelf v3 + OAI System Liquid Cooling Guidelines; Google internal power-architecture team grew through 2025; Microsoft Maia integration hints at module-level customization. None has shipped a fully self-designed VPD module at production scale. The structural bound is buying or building a ChiP-equivalent fab, which neither has committed to.

4. **TI 800VDC reference platform** (adjacent expansion): TI's NVIDIA partnership at the rack-down 800V silicon layer creates wholesale-channel competition for Vicor's 800V→48V bus-conversion module IP, but at the chip-level module layer Vicor remains differentiated. Net: TI's expansion into 800VDC is share-additive at the bus-conversion stage and share-neutral at the final-converter stage.

5. **Chinese power-module specialists** (Global South vector): unnamed in NVIDIA partner ecosystem, but Huawei's Digital Power business (per [[Sectors/Data Center Power & Cooling]] §Competitive dynamics) and Chinese contract manufacturers (Delta, Cyntec, Foxconn — though now LEO licensees) have low-cost vertical product lines targeting Chinese hyperscale + Saudi/UAE/Asia-Pacific. Caps Vicor's Global South revenue growth to ~70% of otherwise-available TAM, but US/EU markets remain protected by ITC enforcement.

6. **Astera Labs / CXL / connectivity adjacencies** — not direct competitors at the power layer, but the OCP-spec disaggregation push Astera leverages (smart-fabric switches, retimers, AECs) creates similar pressure to disaggregate single-vendor integrated power+module bundles. Watch OCP 2027–2028 power-shelf RFP language for "integrated bundle" vs "best-of-breed disaggregated" hyperscaler procurement language shift.

**Pricing-power impact of the IDM M&A wave:** the IDM consolidation (TI+National Semi 2011, ADI+Linear 2017, ADI+Maxim 2021, Infineon+IR 2014 + Cypress 2020, Renesas+Intersil 2017) created enough scale economics that PMIC IDMs hold 55–65% gross margins despite commodity pricing pressure on individual SKUs. The structural read: IDMs cross-subsidize AI-density product margins with automotive + industrial volume. Vicor lacks this cross-subsidy and depends entirely on AI / HPC density premium — which makes the FY2027–2028 product GM trajectory more sensitive to socket-content outcomes than IDM peers.

## Macro shifts

**1. NVIDIA roadmap density compression (structural, bullish for vertical).** Generational cadence compressed from ~2 years to ~12–15 months. Each generation steps current density 30–150%: H100 ~700A → GB200 ~1,200A → GB300 ~1,500A → Vera Rubin NVL144 ~2,000A → Rubin Ultra NVL576 ~2,500–3,000A peak per processor. Below ~1,500A, multiphase buck (MPS-style lateral) is dominant on cost; above ~2,000A, vertical (Vicor-style) is required by physics — P = I²R loss in the "last inch" of PCB copper at 70 µΩ trace resistance scales quadratically (45W at 800A → 158W at 1,500A → 280W at 2,000A). The architectural fork at ~1,500–2,000A maps directly to the Rubin generation transition (H2 2026) and Rubin Ultra (H2 2027). **Investment implication:** Vicor / Flex / eventual-MPS-vertical capture incremental share at every generation that crosses the 2,000A threshold; MPS lateral holds the H100/H200/GB200/GB300 socket through end-of-life but not future generations.

**2. Co-packaged optics (CPO) thermal-coupling forcing function (structural, bullish for VPD specifically).** NVIDIA Quantum-X 28.8 Tbps switch (early 2026) and Spectrum-X Photonics (2H 2026) put 18 optical engines + 1,000+ fibers on the package topside — leaving no room for lateral multiphase converters. More subtly, silicon micro-ring resonators shift 80 pm/°C in resonant wavelength (per Padmaraju et al. citation in [[Research/2026-04-28 - VICR - Vertical Power Delivery Technical Architecture and Q1 2026 Earnings - deep-dive]]); a 100 GHz WDM channel near 1550nm is only 800 pm wide, so a 5–10°C thermal disturbance from a lateral converter dumping heat into adjacent photonic chiplets requires active heater compensation that costs additional power. VPD on the package bottom routes converter heat through a separate cooling path, decoupling the thermal domains. **Investment implication:** the H100-era cost-and-manufacturing-ease criteria that favored MPS flip to "space and thermal" criteria in the CPO era — same NVIDIA ecosystem, opposite winner. The thermal argument is independent of the current-density argument, doubling the structural force on VPD adoption.

**3. 800VDC distribution architecture transition (2H 2026 inflection).** NVIDIA March 2026 published full 800VDC reference architecture with TI, Vertiv, Schneider, Eaton, Delta, Flex, GE Vernova, Mitsubishi, Siemens, and 22 other ecosystem partners. Rationale: 800VDC cuts distribution losses 30–40% vs 480VAC at 1 MW rack scale. The architectural debate that matters for module specialists: 800V→48V→0.7V (Vicor's preferred path; 48V intermediate stage on-board) vs 800V→6V (single-step; some industry research camps). The I²R penalty quantification:

| Distribution voltage | Current ratio vs 48V baseline | I²R loss ratio |
|---|---|---|
| 48V (baseline) | 1× | 1× |
| 6V | 8× | **64×** |
| 2V (IVR-style) | 24× | **526×** (per Phil Davies cited assumptions) |

Vinciarelli on Q1 2026 call: "frankly ill-conceived" referring to 800V→6V; "Vicor has proprietary technology at 800 volts. We did a lot of pioneering developments with respect to bus conversion from 800 volts." **Investment implication:** even in the bear case where 800V→6V wins, Vicor's IP exposure at the 800V upstream bus-conversion stage opens a royalty path — the architectural debate doesn't have a path that fully excludes Vicor.

**4. ITC LEO enforcement industry-wide (2024–2027).** Vicor's 1st LEO (Oct 2024) is the precedent; 2nd ITC final determination expected 2027; 3rd case hinted at by Patrizio. Other US-domiciled IP holders are likely to follow (the ITC enforcement playbook is now public + repeatable). **Investment implication:** "license rather than infringe" calculus becomes the default for Asian ODMs across the power-module category; pricing power and royalty rents extend 5–7 years before patents expire. Federal Circuit appeal scope is the binding question — narrows scope = compresses pipeline; holds scope = extends through patent term.

**5. Onshoring + tariff regime (bullish for US-based module specialists).** Section 301 tariffs on Chinese power components + ITC Limited Exclusion Orders bias ODM bills-of-material toward US-manufactured modules. Vicor's Andover Fab 1 footprint is procurement-positive in this regime. TI / ADI / MPS have global manufacturing footprints (less U.S.-concentrated). **Investment implication:** procurement preference shift toward US-domiciled supply for AI-critical components compresses the cost gap that historically favored Asian-manufactured PMIC silicon, narrowing one of MPS's structural advantages over Vicor.

**6. GaN/SiC adoption at the IC layer (bearish for discrete-MOSFET incumbents, neutral for module specialists).** GaN and SiC switching transistors increase density at the IC layer (where MPS, TXN, ADI, Infineon live) but do NOT obviate the module-level architecture choice (where Vicor lives). Higher switching frequency + lower switching loss compresses inductor + capacitor size, which is positive for IC integration economics but does not change P = I²R in the last inch of copper. **Investment implication:** GaN/SiC narrative that "power-density wins go to silicon innovators" is correct on the IC layer (where MPS lives) and wrong on the module layer (where Vicor lives). Sell-side conflation of the two layers under-weights Vicor's architectural moat.

**7. AI capex supercycle ($600–690B 2026; ~75% AI-directed).** Hyperscaler capex flows directly to power + cooling at ~8–12% of total AI infra capex (per [[Sectors/Data Center Power & Cooling]] §Macro shifts #5). Of that, the chip-level power-module layer captures ~5–10% — implying ~$3–6B annual TAM in 2026. Vicor's $570M FY26 revenue guide is ~10–18% of that TAM; MPS's AI accelerator power revenue is similar order of magnitude. **Investment implication:** capacity-constrained module specialists (Vicor backlog $301M at 2.7× quarterly revenue, "sold out for foreseeable future" per Vinciarelli) are demand-bottlenecked; IDM peers have spare capacity but lack the high-density product. The category is structurally undersupplied at the high-density end through 2027–2028.

**8. Chinese bifurcation (TAM cap at Global South).** Huawei Digital Power + Chinese contract manufacturers (Delta, Cyntec, Foxconn — now Vicor licensees) capture Chinese hyperscale + Saudi/UAE/Asia-Pacific AI DC awards. Vicor's Global South exposure is bounded by US/EU-aligned customer mix; international revenue cap ~70% of otherwise-available TAM. **Investment implication:** consolidated international growth rate slows from 15–20% to 8–12% if Global South caps bind in 2027–2028; partially offset by ITC LEO enforcement protecting US/EU markets.

## Investor heuristics

**What's priced in (consensus view):**

- AI capex tailwind continues at elevated levels through 2026–2027 with peak concern at H2 2027. Vicor 23x FY26E EPS, MPS ~30x, TI ~22x, ADI ~25x.
- "Vicor lost the AI cycle at H100" is the consensus framing for Vicor; "MPS won AI accelerator power" is the consensus framing for MPS — both anchored on 2022–2024 socket history.
- 800VDC + Rubin generation step-up are tailwinds for the broader category; specific vendor-by-vendor share allocation is unmodeled.
- Federal Circuit LEO scope is a binary risk that buy-side and sell-side flag as unresolved; consensus does not weight it explicitly in DCF.
- IDM consolidation is complete; no more $10B+ deals expected.

**What's mispriced (non-consensus angles):**

1. **The architectural fork at ~1,500–2,000A is the critical data point, not the H100 socket history.** Sell-side coverage hardened around 2022–2024 share losses; the structural reframe is that lateral multiphase self-limits at this current threshold regardless of which silicon vendor builds it. MPS's H100 win is durable at 700–1,500A through end-of-life; cannot follow above 2,000A without a clean-sheet vertical. Vicor's V100/A100 wins at 500–800A were the foundation for Rubin VPD because the architectural geometry is the same. **Mispricing:** consensus extrapolating "Vicor lost H100 = Vicor lost AI" misses that the architecture is current-regime-dependent, and Rubin / Rubin Ultra cross the threshold where Vicor's home turf returns.

2. **ITC LEO is a margin-accretive structural rents engine, not a one-time settlement.** Delta/Cyntec/Foxconn settled rather than appealed (signaling patents well-grounded); $45M settlement + $57M organic licensing 2025; FY2026 guide assumes zero new licensing pending 2027 ITC final determination + 3rd-case hint. Royalty margin is "nearly 100%" per management. The 50%-of-product-revenue upper-bound framing (Phil Davies, multiple appearances) implies $255M+ on a $510M product base — a step-function in FY27–FY28 GM if 2nd ITC settles favorably. **Mispricing:** sell-side DCFs model licensing as flat residual rather than as structural feature of the competitive geometry; the option-value asymmetry ("win socket OR lose to infringer with royalty") is missing.

3. **Power-module IP is not commodity — replication clock is 5–7 years for unfunded entrants, 3–5 years for well-funded.** External procurement specs (input/output voltage, power rating, package dimensions) are standardized by IEC + OCP; internal design (FPA topology, SAC ZCS+ZVS timing, ChiP packaging stack, 3Di interconnect, 40× current multiplication, thermal-mechanical co-design under 50,000-hr accelerated test history) is highly differentiated and accumulates as IP through field-failure history that new entrants do not have. The Vicor moat is closer to medical-device or aerospace componentry than industrial HVAC. **Mispricing:** consensus models extrapolating commodity electrical industrial pricing dynamics under-credit Vicor's pricing power and gross margin durability by 200–300 bp at terminal margin.

4. **Founder-controlled dual-class is structurally protective in this category, priced as a discount.** Vinciarelli holds 47% economic + 80% voting (10 votes per Class B). The 44-year tenure preserved the Andover footprint through the 2005–2020 offshore-cost cycles, refused the H100 socket on margin terms, and built the IP portfolio patiently. The 80% voting structure makes hostile acquisition impossible — which protects independence but also means Vicor cannot be acquired into a strategic buyer at premium. **Mispricing:** sell-side typical founder-CEO discount applied to Vicor (succession risk at 78yo) ignores that the dual-class structure also delivered the disciplined capital allocation that made the Rubin opportunity possible. The structural patience premium is undervalued; the succession risk is over-emphasized.

5. **Capacity is now the enabler, not the constraint — but consensus is anchored on the prior bottleneck.** Pre-2025, Andover Fab 1 was binding at ~$1B run-rate. Q1 2026 disclosure: Fab 1 nameplate now $1.5B/yr (50% above plan), second 3Di line Q3/Q4 2026, Fab 2 strategy switched from greenfield to existing-building retrofit. FY2026 $570M revenue guide vs $1.5B nameplate = ~38% utilization. Vinciarelli: "Sold out for the foreseeable future." **Mispricing:** sell-side models extrapolating historical 4–6% revenue growth into 2027–2028 miss that the limiter has shifted from supply to licensing-deal closure timing; structural growth path is gated by demand (Rubin shipments + licensing settlements) rather than capacity for the first time in company history.

6. **CPO thermal-coupling is a second, independent reason for VPD adoption — not a duplicate of the current-density argument.** Even setting aside P = I²R, silicon micro-ring resonators shifting 80 pm/°C make adjacent-chiplet thermal disturbance from a lateral converter a wavelength-stability problem. A 5–10°C disturbance forces 400–800 pm tuning correction; 100 GHz WDM channel width is only 800 pm. VPD on the package bottom decouples thermal domains. **Mispricing:** sell-side AI-power coverage focuses on current-density narrative; the thermo-optic argument is independent and additive, materially under-weighted in vendor selection forecasts for Quantum-X / Spectrum-X Photonics deployments.

7. **MPS clean-sheet vertical attack is the binding 2027–2028 risk — not "AI capex peak."** Bear-case framing focuses on hyperscaler capex cut in 2027. Closer 2027–2028 risk: MPS commits R&D to a clean-sheet vertical product (margin math is favorable: Vicor 50–55% product GM > MPS lateral commoditization trajectory). Vicor's IP buys 5–7 years but not infinity; reverse-engineering by a $30B+ silicon vendor is a 3–5 year track. The architectural moat is buyable, not built — Vicor's lead collapses if MPS or a well-funded entrant ships matching specs. **Mispricing:** consensus does not flag MPS clean-sheet vertical as a discrete catalyst risk; it should be the #1 watched signal for 2027–2028.

8. **The category bifurcates into "AI accelerator high-density" (~$3–6B TAM, Vicor + Flex + future-MPS-vertical) and "general PMIC" (~$50B TAM, TI + ADI + MPS + Infineon + Renesas) over 2026–2030 — but consensus models them as one market.** Pricing power, growth rate, and competitive structure are different in each segment. AI-density is structurally premium-priced (50–55%+ GM, 20–30%+ revenue growth, 2–4 vendor competition); general PMIC is automotive-cross-subsidized (55–65% GM, 8–12% revenue growth, 8+ vendor competition). **Mispricing:** Vicor multiple should be re-anchored to AI-density-segment peers (Vertiv 35–40x EPS, ASML, Synopsys) rather than general-PMIC peers (TI 20–22x, MPS 28–32x); the 23x FY26E multiple under-credits the AI-density purity.

9. **Customer concentration risk is bounded but unquantifiable — and this is itself a mispricing.** Vicor does not break out customer concentration in 10-K filings; sell-side estimates run 20–40% NVIDIA-direct exposure. If NVIDIA is >25% of revenue, asymmetry is real and concerning. If NVIDIA is <15% and demand flows through ODMs (Compal, Foxconn, Quanta, Wiwynn) buying Vicor on bill-of-materials specifications, the concentration framing is overstated. **Mispricing:** consensus discounts Vicor for unbounded NVIDIA concentration; actual concentration likely lower than perceived because ODM-mediated channel structure obscures direct exposure. 10-K Item 1 disclosure (annual) is the catalyst.

**Non-consensus takeaways:**

- **The architecture-by-current-regime framing is the critical analytical move.** Above ~2,000A, vertical wins; below ~1,500A, lateral wins. Vendor selection is current-regime-driven, not cost-driven, in the high-density tail.
- **Vicor + ITC enforcement is the cleanest play on AI-density module rents.** Pure-play exposure, IP-protected pricing, founder-controlled discipline. Bounded by founder concentration risk (78yo, no public succession plan) and Federal Circuit appeal scope.
- **MPS is the cleanest play on lateral PMIC commoditization** — high-quality H100-era socket holder with structural margin compression risk as commodity pricing accelerates and clean-sheet vertical entry remains uncommitted.
- **TI / ADI / Infineon / Renesas are diversified industrials with AI-density tail exposure** — automotive cross-subsidy makes them less levered to AI capex peak risk but also caps upside on AI-density premium.
- **Watch for 2027 LEO Federal Circuit decision + MPS R&D disclosures + Rubin Ultra reference design partner** as the three critical 2026–2028 tells.
