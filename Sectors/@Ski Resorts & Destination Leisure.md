# Ski Resorts & Destination Leisure

## Active Theses
- [[Theses/MTN - Vail Resorts]] — Vail Resorts (governance-constrained public operator vs Alterra private capex gap / impossible trinity: dividend + capex + affordability / conviction low/avoid)

## Key industry questions
- Can the Epic-Ikon duopoly defend pricing power as climate convexity compounds, demographic narrowing accelerates, and private capital (Alterra) outpaces public capital (Vail) ~4:1 on per-resort capex — and is "ski resort operator" the right unit of analysis vs the broader destination-leisure real estate these players increasingly monetize through base-village development, heli-ski, and fractional ownership?

## Industry history

**1946-1970 — Birth (10th Mountain Division + Austrian imports):** Aspen Skiing Company incorporated 1946 by Friedl Pfeifer (Austrian 10th Mountain Division instructor), Walter Paepcke (Chicago industrialist), and three partners; Ajax Mountain opens 1946-47 with a single chairlift. Boyne Resorts founded 1947 by Everett Kircher (Studebaker dealer), $1 land lease at Boyne Mountain, Michigan. Vail Associates incorporates 1957 — Pete Seibert (10th Mountain vet) and Earl Eaton build a Swiss-style alpine village that opens December 1962. Killington opens 1958 under Preston Leete Smith. US ski operations peak at ~700 by late 1970s.

**1980-2000 — First consolidation wave:** 50%+ of US ski areas close between 1984-2000 as insurance, snowmaking capex, and real estate economics squeeze small operators. Les Otten's American Skiing Company (ASC) assembles Killington, Sunday River, Sugarloaf, Heavenly, Steamboat via debt-funded rollup; IPOs 1997 at $18 and begins 15-year collapse. Intrawest (Joe Houssian, Vancouver) IPOs 1997 with a real-estate-first model — Whistler Blackcomb, Tremblant, Stratton, Snowshoe. Crown family purchases 50% of Aspen in 1985, full ownership 1993 under Lester Crown. Powdr Corp founded 1994 by John Cumming with Park City Mountain Resort.

**2000-2015 — Financialization & the Epic Pass revolution:** Apollo takes Intrawest private 2006; ASC collapse complete by 2007 (Sunday River and Sugarloaf sold to Boyne; Killington to Powdr; Heavenly to Vail). Vail Resorts (NYSE: MTN) IPOs 1997; acquires Keystone, Breckenridge, Heavenly, Northstar, Kirkwood through 2010. **Epic Pass launches for the 2008-09 season at $579 for unlimited access to 5 resorts** — decoupling revenue from snowfall and locking customer cash in spring for winter operations. Industry financialization arrives; MTN trades at 35-40x P/E. Mountain Collective pass launches 2012 as premium resort coalition (Aspen, Jackson Hole, Alta, Snowbird, Squaw) — two days per resort, positioned against Epic.

**2016-2020 — Duopoly formation:** Vail acquires Whistler Blackcomb 2016 ($1.1B), Stowe 2017, Peak Resorts 2019 ($264M, 17 small urban-feeder resorts). **KSL Capital Partners + Henry Crown acquire Intrawest $1.5B in April 2017; two days later Mammoth Resorts (Mammoth, Big Bear, June, Snow Summit); August 2017 Deer Valley.** Alterra Mountain Company formally named January 2018 (portmanteau of "altitude" + "terra"). Ikon Pass launches 2018-19 season with 12 original resorts, grows to 76 (19 owned + 57 partners) by 2025-26. Indy Pass launches 2019 by Doug Fish: 100+ independent resorts, ~$329, anti-corporate positioning.

**2021-2024 — #Epicalypse & the experience fracture:** Vail cuts Epic Pass price 20% for 2021-22; pandemic stimulus creates demand surge; overcrowding becomes existential brand crisis (45-minute lift lines at Vail, Stevens Pass; closed terrain from staffing shortages). Stevens Pass consumer-protection lawsuit filed 2022. A-Basin defects from Epic 2019 citing overcrowding, joins Ikon Base 2020. **Park City ski patrol strike December 2024-January 2025 during peak holidays forces Vail to institute $20/hour minimum wage across all North American resorts.** Vail launches Resource Efficiency Transformation late 2024 targeting $100M savings via 14% corporate headcount cuts. **Alterra acquires A-Basin fully November 2024** — a strategic coup locking the Colorado Front Range enthusiast. Powdr announces partial sell-off 2024-25 (Killington and Pico to locals; Mount Bachelor, Eldora, Silver Star listed). Boyne and Powdr form 19-resort "strategic brand partnership" 2024 (distribution cooperation, no ownership change). KSL closes $3B+ Alterra continuation vehicle February 2024.

**2025-2026 — Structural inflection:** Alterra commits $400M+ capex for 2025-26 season; Deer Valley "Expanded Excellence" — 3,700 acres new terrain via Mayflower/Extell partnership (tripling Deer Valley to 5,726 skiable acres), 7 new lifts for 2025-26, 10-seat East Village Express gondola, $600M+ East Village real estate with 1,000+ residences and 250,000 sq ft of retail. Aspen reorganizes under "Aspen One" umbrella (2023); ~$80M Snowmass capex for summer 2025. **Vail FY2026 Rockies snowfall 40% below FY2012's previous 30-year low; Q2 FY2026 skier visits -13% YoY; EBITDA guidance slashed from $842-898M to $745-775M.** Rob Katz returns as Vail CEO May 2025; introduces 20% Epic Pass discount for ages 13-30 in March 2026. Alterra CEO transition March 2026. Entabeni Systems (acquired Indy Pass 2023) moves HQ to Black Mountain, NH March 2026. NSAA reports 61.5M US skier visits for 2024-25 — second-best on record, +1.7% YoY; participation 3.1% of Americans (~10.7M people).

## Competitive dynamics

North American duopoly with private-capital asymmetry and a fragmenting long tail:

| Operator | Ownership | Resorts | 2026-27 Pass | N.A. Share | Capex |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Vail (Epic)** | Public (NYSE: MTN) | 42 (37 N.A., 3 AUS, 2 EU) | $1,089 full | ~30-34% | Defensive: $215-220M / ~$5.2M per resort; $100M efficiency program |
| **Alterra (Ikon)** | Private (KSL + Henry Crown) | 19 owned + 57 partners (76 total) | ~$1,349 full / $924 base | ~15% | Offensive: $400M+ / ~$21M per owned resort; Deer Valley 3,700-acre expansion |
| **Aspen One** | Private (Crown family since 1985/1993) | 4 (Aspen Mtn, Snowmass, Buttermilk, Highlands) | Ikon anchor partner | ~6% | Targeted luxury (~$80M summer 2025); anti-rollup posture |
| **Boyne Resorts** | Private (Kircher family since 1947) | 10 (Big Sky, Sunday River, Sugarloaf, Loon, Brighton, Cypress, Summit-at-Snoqualmie, +3) | Mostly Ikon partner | ~8% | Regional anchor; Big Sky heavy investment; 2024 Powdr partnership |
| **Powdr** | Private (Cumming family since 1994) | Shrinking — Killington/Pico sold 2024; Bachelor/Eldora/Silver Star on the block | Mixed Ikon/Mountain Collective | ~3-5%, declining | Strategic retreat to Woodward/adventure segments |
| **Compagnie des Alpes** | Public (Euronext Paris) | 10 French ski domains + 14 leisure parks | Mostly non-mega-pass | N/A (Europe lead) | $1.54B TTM revenue; French municipal concession model |
| **SkiStar AB** | Public (Nasdaq Stockholm) | 7 (Sweden, Norway, Austria) | Own pass | N/A (Scandinavia lead) | $510M TTM revenue; integrated lodging + real estate |
| **Indy Pass (Entabeni)** | Private (2023 acquisition) | 300+ partner guarantee 2026-27 | $399 Indy+ | Coalition (no ownership) | Zero capex; distribution-only anti-duopoly play |

**Pricing power is bifurcating.** Alterra raised Ikon ~5% for 2026-27 on top of 9% for 2025-26. Vail raised Epic only ~3.6% and cut 20% for ages 13-30 — the clearest revealed-preference signal that Alterra attacks yield while Vail defends units. Ikon Base ($924) undercuts Epic full ($1,089) by $165 while delivering objectively superior Utah access (Deer Valley, Alta, Snowbird, Solitude, Brighton, Snowbasin) and Colorado early/late season (A-Basin October-July). Epic must now compete on price AND experience simultaneously.

**Regional contestation:**
- **Colorado:** Vail I-70 "Big 4" (Vail, Beaver Creek, Breck, Keystone) + Crested Butte vs. Alterra encirclement (Winter Park train-accessible, Steamboat destination families, A-Basin Oct-July expert terrain, Copper partner, Eldora Boulder local). A-Basin's 2019 defection + 2024 Alterra acquisition locked the Front Range enthusiast.
- **Utah:** Park City Mountain (Vail, largest US resort by skiable terrain; bruised by 2024-25 patrol strike) vs. Deer Valley East Village (Alterra, most ambitious N.A. expansion in 40 years) + Alta, Snowbird, Solitude, Brighton, Snowbasin. Market-share erosion likely most acute here.
- **California/Tahoe:** Heavenly, Northstar, Kirkwood (Vail, lower-elevation rain-snow risk) vs. Palisades Tahoe base-to-base gondola + Mammoth (Alterra, 11,000 ft, season extends to July).
- **Montana:** Big Sky (Boyne, #2 by US acreage) unaligned with either mega-pass at core — Ikon partner — the wild-card asset both operators would likely want.
- **New England/Mid-Atlantic:** Vail Stowe, Okemo, Hunter + Peak Resorts portfolio vs. Alterra Sugarbush, Stratton + Boyne Sunday River, Sugarloaf, Loon. Climate ground zero.
- **Europe:** Compagnie des Alpes (French Alps municipal concessions) + SkiStar (Scandinavia). Mega-passes culturally uncommon. Vail Crans-Montana (2022) tests whether Epic translates; Alterra has no European footprint.
- **Japan:** Niseko (Hong Kong/Malaysian/Singaporean capital building "Asian Whistler"; Kutchan commercial land +30% YoY 2023) vs. Hakuba (family-run, resistance to outside investment).
- **China:** Genting Secret Garden and Thaiwoo (Chongli district post-Beijing 2022 Olympics) — 9.43M visitors in early 2023-24 season, +316% YoY. Seven of world's top 10 indoor ski resorts now in China.

**Threat of new entrants:** low in core N.A. outdoor (capex barriers, land scarcity, permitting) but material at the distribution layer (Indy Pass coalition, Mountain Collective) and in Asian indoor/outdoor infrastructure (state-backed Chinese operators, Niseko foreign capital).

## Product level analysis

**Mega-pass economics — the four-tier market (2026-27):**

| Pass | Price | Access | Days per Resort | Holders | Economic Logic |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Epic (full)** | $1,089 (+3% taxes = ~$1,120) | 42 Vail-owned globally | Unlimited | ~2.3M | Volume maximization; breadth-over-depth; ancillary capture |
| **Epic Local** | ~$719 | ~20 resorts, holiday blackouts | Unlimited | Subset of 2.3M | Price-sensitive commuter skier |
| **Epic Day Pass** | Custom (1-7 day bundles) | Select | Fixed | Growing | Casual skier on-ramp funnel |
| **Ikon (full)** | ~$1,349 | 76 resorts (19 owned + 57 partners) | Unlimited owned, 5-7 partners | Undisclosed | Premium pricing; curated network; partner brand equity |
| **Ikon Base** | ~$924 | Same, more blackouts + 5-day partner cap | Unlimited owned, 5 partners | Undisclosed | Competitively priced vs Epic full |
| **Mountain Collective** | ~$629 | 25 premium resorts (Aspen, Jackson Hole, Alta, Snowbird, Sun Valley, etc.) | 2 days each | Smaller | Trophy-hunter bundle; survives Ikon-adjacent premium tier |
| **Indy+** | $399 | 300+ resorts (2026 guarantee) | 2 days each, zero blackouts | Growing | Anti-duopoly coalition; no corporate integration |

**Lift ticket economics (2025-26):** Walk-up $200-299 at premier destinations on holidays — designed to funnel casual visitors into pass purchases rather than to monetize window volume. Window ticket sales down ~17% YoY in FY2024 as the strategy succeeds, shrinking the pass-conversion funnel.

**Ancillary revenue stack per skier visit (Vail benchmark):**
- Ski school: $150-1,500/day (premium private lessons grew 6% FY2024 despite -8% visit volume)
- Dining: $28 on-mountain burger; near-monopoly pricing at summit lodges
- Retail/rental: $75-150/day rental; high operating leverage to visit volume (fell 12% FY2024)
- Lodging: $500-2,000/night at base; ski-in/ski-out premium
- Combined ski school + dining ≈ 20-25% of Vail's Mountain segment revenue

**Real estate integration — the hidden product line.** Modern ski-resort economics are 30-50% real-estate-funded:
- **Deer Valley East Village** (Alterra/Extell): 3,700 acres ski + 1,000+ residences + 250,000 sq ft retail + 68,000 sq ft recreation — $600M+ project
- **Snowmass Base Village** ($600M, Aspen Skiing Co. + East West Partners + KSL Capital)
- **Vail West Lionshead** 2024 redevelopment (4th base village, East West Partners + Town of Vail + Vail Resorts)
- **East West Partners** (founded 1986 at Beaver Creek; KSL-affiliated) — master developer across ~$2B+ of resort real estate
- **Replay Destinations** (Vancouver, $1.5B portfolio) — master developer at Canyons, operations across US/Hawaii/Caribbean
- **Extell** (NYC developer, Mayflower Resort landowner) — now Alterra's Deer Valley East Village partner

**Heli-ski / cat-ski / private luxury segment** — margin-structurally superior to mass-market resort:
- **CMH Heli-Skiing** (Alterra-owned via Intrawest acquisition): 11 BC lodges, 3M skiable acres (~23x all N.A. resort acreage combined), private-group pricing $25K-75K/week
- Smaller competitors (Points North, Ski Gulmarg, Eleven Experience, Last Frontier, Mike Wiegele Helicopter Skiing): $15K-50K/week
- Yellowstone Club (Montana, private), Club Ski at Deer Valley (fractional memberships $100K+) — luxury private-club segment captures top ~5% of industry yield

**The LTV flywheel:** Pass buyer → captive on-mountain spend (~$500-1,500/visit incremental) → lodging → real estate purchase. The real-estate-enabled resort (Deer Valley, Whistler, Snowmass, Vail Village) generates IRR that pure skiing cannot support. This explains Alterra's compounding capex advantage: each guest-facing upgrade drives real estate pricing at adjacent developments, which cross-subsidizes further terrain/lift investment. Vail, constrained to operational capex, cannot tap the same flywheel.

## Acquisitions and new entrants

**Four consolidation waves:**

| Era | Defining Transactions | Strategic Rationale | Winner | Loser |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Wave 1 (1985-2000)** | Crown buys Aspen (1985/1993); Intrawest IPO (1997); Les Otten assembles ASC | Mom-and-pop → institutional capital; real-estate-first model | Crown (patient); Intrawest | ASC (debt trap); independents |
| **Wave 2 (2006-2019)** | Apollo takes Intrawest private (2006); ASC dismantled (2006-08) with parts sold to Boyne, Powdr, Vail; Vail assembles Keystone/Breck/Heavenly/Northstar/Whistler ($1.1B)/Stowe/Peak Resorts ($264M); KSL+Crown form Alterra (2017-18) | Mega-pass model demands network scale | Vail (Epic scale); Alterra (patient rollup) | ASC remnants; small regional operators |
| **Wave 3 (2019-2024)** | A-Basin defects from Epic (2019) citing overcrowding; Vail Crans-Montana (2022); Alterra A-Basin acquisition (Nov 2024); KSL $3B+ continuation vehicle (Feb 2024) | Quality > quantity; private-capital patience horizon | Alterra (A-Basin coup); KSL (capital permanence) | Vail (Colorado flank exposed) |
| **Wave 4 (2024-2026)** | Powdr partial sell-off (Killington, Pico, Bachelor, Eldora, Silver Star); Boyne+Powdr distribution partnership; Aspen One reorganization (2023); Entabeni acquires Indy Pass (2023); Alterra Deer Valley East Village | Duopoly saturation; experience-quality bifurcation; fragmented long tail re-forms | Alterra (investment offensive); Indy (distribution alternative) | Vail (public-market constraint); Powdr (strategic retreat) |

**New entrants and disruption vectors:**

1. **Indy Pass / Entabeni (distribution disruptor):** 300+ resort coalition; zero operator capex; $399 Indy+; Erik Mogensen (Black Mountain NH owner) moved HQ to NH March 2026. Functions as a distribution protest vote — gives aging/independent resorts scale marketing without corporate absorption. Revenue-shared with resorts rather than centralized.

2. **Chinese indoor + outdoor (scale disruptor):** 7 of 10 largest indoor ski resorts globally now in China; outdoor Chongli district visitors +316% YoY early 2023-24. State-backed, Olympic-legacy infrastructure. BONSKI Zhengding is the largest indoor resort in northern China. Genting Hong Kong owns Secret Garden (2022 Olympic freestyle/snowboard venue). Different unit economics — subsidized land and infrastructure.

3. **Niseko / Japanese luxury (geographic redirection):** Hong Kong, Malaysian, Singaporean developers rolling up Niseko into "Asian Whistler" — Kutchan commercial land +30% YoY 2023, highest land-price growth in all of Japan. Indirect competitive threat to N.A. destination segment by redirecting Asian skier demand over 10-year horizon.

4. **Luxury private members clubs (yield compression):** Yellowstone Club (Montana, private ski), Iron Mountain Club, Club Ski at Deer Valley (fractional memberships $100K+). Captures the top ~5% of industry yield by removing access constraints that plague mass-market operators.

5. **Experience-differentiation independents:** Telluride (Chuck Horning), Jackson Hole Mountain Resort (Kemmerer family since 1992), Alta (member-owned), Grand Targhee — maintain Ikon partner or Mountain Collective status but resist full acquisition. Aspen-Snowmass effectively in this camp despite being an Ikon anchor.

**Pricing-power implications:** Consolidation increased duopoly power 2008-2020; pricing power has eroded 2021-2026 as the experiential product broke. Indy Pass, Mountain Collective, and A-Basin-style defections signal that operator brand equity is more fragile than it appeared during the 2008-2019 land-grab decade. **Private-capital operators (Alterra, Aspen, Boyne, Powdr, Yellowstone Club) structurally outperform public operators on guest experience because they face no dividend/buyback/quarterly EBITDA constraint.**

## Macro shifts

**Seven forces driving 10-year-plus change:**

1. **Climate (convex, step-function, not linear).** US ski seasons shortened 5.5-7.1 days (2000-19 vs 1960-79). Projections: 14-33 days shorter by 2050s (low-emission); 27-62 days (high-emission). FY2026 Rockies snow 40% below FY2012 (prior 30-year low); every Western US river basin at 15-65% of average snowpack April 2026. Lower-elevation resorts face stranded-asset risk within a decade; high-elevation portfolios (A-Basin 13,000 ft, Mammoth 11,000 ft, Winter Park 12,000 ft) are structurally climate-hedged. Snowmaking is energy-intensive and subject to ~28°F threshold — not a solution for warmer seasons.

2. **Demographics (narrowing participation base).** NSAA 2024-25: 86% white, 62% male, 74% household income >$100K. Under-25 share slid to 33% vs NSAA Growth Committee 40% target. Skiing is the second-most-expensive youth sport ($2,249/child/year). Aging skier base + stagnant beginner participation = structural TAM compression absent aggressive price/access concessions. Vail's 13-30 Epic Pass discount (March 2026) is the first explicit public acknowledgment of this cliff.

3. **Public-vs-private capital.** Vail ~$320M annual dividend + $200M buybacks + quarterly EBITDA discipline vs Alterra patient-capital horizon. 4x per-resort capex gap compounds annually. KSL $3B+ continuation vehicle (Feb 2024) extends Alterra's horizon another 7-10 years. If Alterra IPOs, this structural advantage evaporates — but there is no public indication of IPO intent.

4. **Real estate integration.** Modern ski development economics are 30-50% real-estate-funded (Deer Valley East Village, Snowmass Base Village, Vail West Lionshead). Pure-operator models (pre-2000 ASC) no longer work. KSL + East West Partners affiliation gives Alterra structurally permanent advantage in this dimension. Vail operates real estate opportunistically rather than as a core product.

5. **Global tourism redistribution.** Chinese skiers grew ~10M (2014) → 20M+ (2020) with Olympic-era 300M-participation policy target. Chinese indoor ski market growing fastest globally (7 of world's 10 largest). Niseko + Hakuba + Hokkaido absorbing Asian skier dollars that would previously have gone to Whistler/Park City. Not yet material to Vail/Alterra revenue but re-routes aspirational demand over 10-year horizon.

6. **Labor & housing structural inflation.** $20/hour minimum floor post-Park City strike (2025); mountain-town housing crises (Aspen, Park City, Vail, Whistler, Mammoth) make seasonal labor unreliable. Alterra's $400M 2025-26 program includes dedicated affordable-housing allocation for HR — Vail has no comparable program. Wage inflation structurally exceeds pricing power within Epic's mass-market segment.

7. **Technology (dynamic pricing, reservations, AI scheduling).** Vail's Resource Efficiency ($100M) is largely workforce-management automation — just-in-time staffing that increases brittleness to weather shocks (a storm hits, resort is understaffed). Reservation systems (Epic pilots at Stevens Pass, Park City) cap daily attendance — an acknowledgment that volume-strategy is exhausted. Alterra Deer Valley maintains "skiers only" + daily ticket caps — intentional scarcity commands premium pricing.

## Investor heuristics

**Consensus perception:**
- Duopoly is stable (Vail + Alterra = 50% N.A. share)
- Climate risk is a slow-moving tail event
- Vail's 6.9% dividend yield signals distress but payout is defensible via debt capacity
- Ski industry is mature, cyclical, weather-dependent; fair multiple ~8-10x EBITDA
- Alterra is the better-run private competitor, but not directly investable
- Real estate exposure is incidental to operator thesis

**What is priced in (as of April 2026):**
- FY2026 weather disaster (EBITDA $745-775M; MTN at ~$130)
- Epic Pass unit saturation (-2-3% two consecutive years)
- $20/hour labor cost permanence
- Alterra competitive pressure
- Elevated financial leverage (3.0x net debt/EBITDA)

**Non-consensus insights (what is not priced in):**

1. **Climate is convex, not linear.** Consecutive 30-year-low Rockies seasons (2024-25 and 2025-26) suggest the right tail is being reached, not approached. Insurance signals support this — mountain property insurance costs rising 30-50% annually in fire-prone zones. If "bad years" become baseline, Vail's ~$850M normalized EBITDA assumption decays by ~$80-100M/year, re-rating the stock mechanically.

2. **Real estate is the hidden balance sheet.** Vail's 42-resort land bank (Vail Village, Park City base, Whistler Blackcomb, Stowe, Beaver Creek) is undisclosed in appraisal terms. Comparable recreational real estate transactions suggest $2-10M/acre in premier locations. A conservative 10,000-acre commercially developable estimate implies $20-100B in latent land value — vs. $7.2B enterprise value. Liquidation/highest-and-best-use optionality is underweighted because it conflicts with the operating-business narrative management promotes.

3. **Public-market disadvantage is durable, not transitional.** Market typically assumes Vail can "catch up" via cost cuts or dividend reduction. The math does not work: closing the $200M annual capex gap with Alterra requires fully eliminating the $320M dividend AND the buyback AND absorbing labor inflation — simultaneous moves that destroy the shareholder base. Structural re-rating downward is probable absent an Alterra IPO.

4. **Ikon's partner-coalition model has hidden fragility.** 57 of 76 Ikon resorts are partners, not owned. Jackson Hole, Alta, Aspen could renegotiate or exit. Alterra CEO transition (March 2026) may test these relationships. In the short-medium term, however, Ikon's model converts partner brand equity into Alterra ARPU without capex — a capital-light flywheel the market does not fully credit on the upside either.

5. **Asian markets are sector-changing, not peripheral.** Chinese outdoor skiers grew from 10M (2014) to 20M+ (2020) with Olympic 300M target. Niseko commercial land +30% YoY 2023. Indoor ski infrastructure (7 of world's 10 largest in China) reshapes year-round destination economics. Not yet material to Vail/Alterra revenue — but 10-year TAM shift is substantial and currently ignored.

6. **Private-market valuation implies public mispricing.** KSL's $3B+ continuation vehicle (Feb 2024) valued Alterra implicitly at $8-12B+ for 19 owned resorts — ~$420-630M per resort. Vail trades at $7.2B EV for 42 resorts = ~$170M per resort. Even adjusting for Vail's Peak Resorts small-resort portfolio, the per-resort valuation gap is 2-4x. Either private capital is overpaying or public markets are under-pricing — the more likely explanation given patient-capital real-estate optionality is that public Ski operators carry a permanent governance discount.

7. **"Ski industry" is the wrong frame; destination-leisure real estate is the right one.** Modern ski-resort economics are dominated by real estate cross-subsidization (East West Partners, Replay, Extell-Mayflower). The correct comparison set is Trophy Properties + Club Med + Six Senses + Yellowstone Club — not ASC legacy operators. This re-framing elevates Alterra, Aspen, and Boyne into a higher-multiple peer set and clarifies why pure-operator public vehicles (MTN) cannot capture the terminal value in the model.
