# Product Tankers & Maritime Shipping

## Key industry questions
- Is the Hormuz ton-mile premium a 2-4 month spike or a regime change? Answer is set by insurance underwriters, not by navies — war-risk premia moved from 0.125% to 5%+ of hull value and Lloyd's JWC re-designated the entire Gulf as a conflict zone on March 3.
- Does the 19% orderbook-to-fleet ratio (146 MR + 88 LR2 scheduled for 2026, historic peak ~20M DWT) overwhelm demand from rerouting, or is scrapping of the 1,397 vessels now >20 years old (17% of the fleet, rising to ~30% by 2028) the offsetting release valve?
- Is the shadow/compliant bifurcation (~10-15% of product tanker fleet shadow, ~85-90% compliant) a permanent structural change that widens with each successive geopolitical shock?
- Does Hafnia's 13.97% stake in TORM (Dec 22, 2025, $311M from Oaktree) trigger a full merger that creates a 200+ vessel / ~$6B NAV consolidator, and does that re-rate the entire sector on pricing discipline?
- Can product tankers capture the crack-spread economy (diesel cracks $44-50/bbl, NY Harbor distillate $1.42/gal — highest since 2022) better than crude tankers capture crude price, i.e. is the thesis the "tighter barrel" rather than the barrel itself?
- What is the TCE underperformance paradox between Scorpio and TORM/Hafnia (3-10% persistent gap) — structural moat for the leaders or fixable gap at Scorpio worth $5-10/share in re-rating?
- Do micronuclear (AMPERA), methanol dual-fuel (Hafnia/Socatra at GSI), LNG dual-fuel (Hafnia Vista Lillesand), and ammonia-ready propulsion redraw the fleet replacement curve — or are current asset values ($168M VLCC resales vs $128.5M newbuilds) the terminal late-cycle signal?

## Industry history

**Pre-1960 integrated oil-major model → independent owner-operator transition.** Oil chartered-in tonnage share rose from 39% (1938) to 58% (1960), 73% (1990), and today ExxonMobil charters 99% of requirements with only five owned/leased tankers. Norwegian owners (Bergesen) and Greek families (Onassis, Niarchos) built speculative fleets against spot markets the majors no longer wanted to finance.

**1967 Suez closure → VLCC birth.** Six-Day War shut the canal for 8 years. Europe-bound Middle East oil rerouted around the Cape, adding 8,000-10,000 km per voyage; tanker rates doubled by end-July 1967. Shipyards responded with larger hulls — the 209,413 DWT Idemitsu Maru (Japan, 1966) was the first VLCC; ULCCs >320,000 DWT emerged by the mid-1970s. By the 1975 canal reopening most oil tankers were too large to use it.

**1973 oil shock → first shipping depression.** OPEC embargo drove prices +300% ($3 → $12/bbl). Tanker tonnage on order tripled 1970-1974; spot rates then collapsed >75% between October-December 1973. Tanker fleet peaked 1977 at 69% above 1970 capacity. Two-decade depression established the first major oil-major divestment wave and the dominance of independent spot-market owner-operators.

**OPA 1990 → permanent structural retreat of majors.** Exxon Valdez (March 1989) spilled 11M gallons in Prince William Sound. Oil Pollution Act 1990 imposed unlimited liability and double-hull construction for all US-trading tonnage. Combined with 1992 MARPOL Annex I (in force 1993), double-hull phase-in accelerated single-hull retirement through 2010-2015. Scrapping surge shifted shipbuilding decisively to Korean and Chinese yards able to produce high-volume double-hull construction.

**2010 shale boom → US Gulf Coast as global product hub.** US crude production rose from 5.5 mbpd (2010) to 9.6 mbpd (2015). US became net refined-product exporter in 2010 and world's #1 by 2014. PADD 3 petroleum exports hit 9.43 mbpd in 2024 (crude 3.96 / products 5.46). USGC→Europe and USGC→Latin America became foundational MR and LR1 trades — a structural re-routing of Atlantic basin flows.

**2017-2021 product-tanker consolidation wave.** Scorpio-Navig8 merger Sept 2017 (added ~27 LR1/LR2). BW Tankers + Hafnia Tankers merger Jan 2019 (102 vessels). Diamond S + Capital Product Partners $1.65B 2019. International Seaways + Diamond S merger July 2021 (100+ vessels). Hafnia + Chemical Tankers Inc. + 12 LR1s from Scorpio 2022 (233 product/chemical tankers, world's largest combined).

**IMO 2020 sulfur cap (Jan 1, 2020).** Cut fuel sulfur cap from 3.5% to 0.50% m/m; 77% SOx reduction, ~8.5Mt/year. Scrubber-installed tanker share reached ~5% in products (vs 29% crude) due to voyage patterns. Boosted MGO and VLSFO demand — both clean-tanker cargoes — and structurally raised product tanker ton-mile demand.

**Russia sanctions 2022 → shadow fleet birth.** G7+EU+Australia $60/bbl cap December 2022; products caps added February 2023. Russian shadow fleet grew from <100 (Feb 2022) to 600+ by end-2022. Ukrainian catalog lists 1,337 ships (Feb 2026); US-plus-allied sanctions list nears 600 EU + 500 UK + 216 OFAC. Shadow fleet moves 65% of Russian seaborne oil (~3.7 mbpd). Tonne-mile inflation: Russian barrels now travel to India/China, not Rotterdam, tightening effective supply for mainstream operators.

**2023 EEXI/CII/EU ETS triple regulatory layer.** EEXI (design) and CII (annual rating) mandatory from Jan 1, 2023; ~33% of fleet rated D/E at current operational behavior. EU ETS Maritime phases in 40% (2024), 70% (2025), 100% (2026). FuelEU Maritime (Jan 2025): -2%/-6%/-14.5% well-to-wake GHG-intensity cuts by 2025/2030/2035 vs 2020; non-compliance penalty €2,400/tonne VLSFO-equivalent.

**Nov 2023-Oct 2025 Red Sea / Bab el-Mandeb.** Houthi attacks forced Cape of Good Hope rerouting; Suez container transits -90% Dec 2023 to March 2024. LR2 Middle East→Europe premium peaked at $900K-$1M per voyage Jan 2024. Trump-brokered Houthi ceasefire May 2025; Maersk resumed Suez transits Dec 2025 — rerouting largely unwound by 2026 but risk premia structurally higher.

**Dec 22, 2025 Hafnia-TORM consolidation.** Hafnia acquired 13.97% of TORM (14.1M A shares, $22/share = $311M) from Oaktree, clearing a decade-old PE overhang. Combined entity would be 200+ vessels / ~$6B NAV and surpass Scorpio as #1 product tanker owner. BW Group (Hafnia's controlling shareholder) positioning for full merger; UK 30% mandatory-offer threshold is the watched trigger.

**Feb 28, 2026 Hormuz closure.** Iran's IRGC blocked commercial passage following US-Israeli strikes; 140 ships/day pre-closure collapsed to single digits. April 12, 2026 dual-blockade regime: US naval counter-blockade of Iranian ports + IRGC closure. Ceasefire expired April 21 without deal; ~600-700 vessels stranded (incl. ~200 compliant non-sanctioned tankers); ~20,000 seafarers trapped. LR2 rates from $36,767/day (Feb 20) to $120,000/day (Mar 6); VLCC benchmark $423,736/day all-time high.

**TCE underperformance paradox (Scorpio 3-10% gap vs TORM/Hafnia):** four drivers proposed in sell-side research. (1) TORM's "One TORM" fully integrated commercial-technical-chartering platform captures pool-fee leakage that Scorpio Commercial Pools shares with third-party participants. (2) TORM's older 11.3y fleet has dirty/clean flexibility and gray-edge-cargo optionality that Scorpio's young eco-fleet — vetted strictly by oil majors — cannot match. (3) Reporting windows: Scorpio's Q4-only LR2 $46K vs TORM's full-year $35,850 distorts apparent gap; multi-year normalized 3-5%. (4) Commercial pool economics: Scorpio pool fees ~3-5% of TCE distributed to ~20 third-party owners create real dilution at the publicly-reported STNG operating entity.

**Shadow / compliant bifurcation.** Total shadow fleet estimates: 978 tankers >27k DWT / 127M DWT combined (S&P Global); 636 sanctioned by Western governments; 600 EU designations (41 added Dec 2025); 500 UK; 216 OFAC; 367 IMO false-flagged (Apr 2026). OFAC designations cut vessel productivity -70%; EU/UK-only -30%. ~46% of Russian shadow fleet capacity operates unproductively. Product tanker shadow share: 10-15%. Only 29.4% of Russian crude tankers and 56.2% of Russian product movements carry International Group P&I coverage — the other 50-70% rely on Ingosstrakh, AlfaStrakhovanie, Maritime Mutual, or outright fake certificates. Each sanction tightening compresses shadow supply and widens the compliant-tonnage scarcity premium for STNG/INSW/Hafnia/TORM/Ardmore.

**Consolidation barriers.** Family ownership (Scorpio's Lolli-Ghetti/Lauro, d'Amico, Tsakos), Greek shipping diaspora (~1,000+ small owners = 27% of global tanker orderbook), cycle-timing (shipping M&A clusters at troughs when owners capitulate; current peak rewards cash-flow holders), and tax/domicile complexity (Bermuda, Isle of Man, Marshall Islands) all retard deal flow. Hafnia-TORM is the template-setting move — a successful full merger would likely trigger activist pressure on STNG and copycat attempts at INSW/Ardmore.

**Market share dynamics.** Gaining: Hafnia (explicit consolidator), ADNOC L&S/Navig8 (sovereign-backed), Scorpio in LR2 post-fleet renewal. Losing relative share: Greek family owners (Tsakos, Pyxis, Performance, d'Amico) as CII/ETS compliance and major-oil vetting requirements favor institutionally-owned young fleets. Greek owners still hold 27% orderbook share and 113 of 435 tankers ordered globally in 2024 (~26%) — the wild-card arbitrage between countercyclical patience and newbuild pricing.

**LR2s dominate long-haul clean product trades.** Modern 115,000 DWT LR2 carries ~125,000 m³ in 10-12 segregated coated tanks; fuel consumption 37-42 t/day at 14 knots laden (ECO); 28-32 t/day on Tier III dual-fuel. ~50% of global LR2 ton-mile demand originates west of Hormuz — the backbone of the current thesis. Principal builders: Hyundai Samho (Ulsan), Samsung HI (Geoje), Hanwha Ocean (Okpo), New Times, Dalian. STNG's 2019-built scrubber-fitted LR2s sold Q4-25 at $61.2M each.

**MR2s are the workhorse class.** ~23% of all tanker S&P transactions in 2025. 50,000 DWT benchmark (e.g., Stena Impression): 183m LOA, 32m beam, 11m draft, 12 tanks at 4,500 m³. Fuel ~17.5 t/day ballast / 19.5 laden at 12.5-13 knots — 10-15% better than 2015-2017 deliveries. Principal builder: Hyundai Mipo Dockyard (dominant). 2025 TCE: Ardmore MR Eco $23,441/day Q2; Atlantic basin peaked $53,372/day May 2024.

**LR1 and MR1 in secular decline.** Displaced by MR2 below and LR2 above. LR1 orderbook momentum recent but limited — Hafnia ordered four LNG dual-fuel LR1s via Vista/CSSC JV. Niche persistence on Europe-West Africa gasoline and Middle East naphtha (LR1/LR2 split 22%/78%).

**Specialised equipment.** All product tankers carry internally coated cargo tanks — epoxy phenolic (common, broad cargo mix, retains some) or zinc silicate (superior solvent/chemical resistance, rough surface). IMO Type 2/3 certification governs chemical compliance; most MRs and LR2s are IMO II/III dual-certified. Stainless steel tanks (Stolt-Nielsen, Odfjell) command ~40% newbuild premium.

**Scrubbers.** Product tankers have the lowest scrubber installation rate of any major segment (~5% vs 29% crude, 17% containers) — reflects short voyages, more port time. Retrofit $2-6.5M; payback driven by HSFO-VLSFO spread (2024-25 $73-161/mt, avg Q4-24 $87/mt). Modern STNG LR2s and MRs scrubber-fitted and eco-designed — the differentiator that supported $61.2M/vessel pricing on recent S&P trades.

**ECO design.** Post-2014 builds with optimized hulls, MAN B&W G-series engines, variable-pitch propellers. Savings: 6-7 t/day on MR2s = ~$1,800-2,100/day lower fuel cost at $300/mt VLSFO. Cumulative savings over 25-year life >$15M/vessel at steady bunker prices.

**STNG fleet case study (90 vessels, 10.1y avg):** 34 LR2 (Hyundai Samho dominant; some Sungdong/Daehan), 42 MR (Hyundai Mipo 2013-2020), 14 Handymax (oldest). Newbuild program: 4 MR at Jingjiang Nanyang ($45M each, Q2-26 through mid-2027), 4 LR2 at DSIC/Hanwha Ocean ($70.8M, 2027-29), 2 VLCC at Hanwha Ocean ($128M each, 2H28) — first crude tanker exposure since 2014 exit. Aggregate capex ~$720M.

**Orderbook composition (2026).** 20M DWT product tanker deliveries — historic peak: 146 MR + 88 LR2. Builder concentration: China 70%, Korea 20%, Japan 1% (lowest since 1996). Hyundai Mipo dominates MR; Hyundai Samho/Samsung/Hanwha/New Times/Dalian dominate LR2. Orderbook-to-fleet: 19% products, 22% crude, 37% containers. 2027: 63 Afra/LR2, 18 LR1, 93 MR.

**Fleet renewal dynamics.** Economic life 20-25 years. Special survey + drydock every 5 years at $3-5M/MR, $6-8M/LR2 incl. BWTS. Scrap steel April 2026: $275-285/LDT (MR gross value ~$3M at 11,000 LDT — ~one drydock cost). Hong Kong Convention on safe ship recycling in force end-June 2025 — tightening yard certification in Bangladesh/India/Pakistan. 2025 ship recycling +65% YoY (7.9M gt) as older tonnage flowed to scrap; 2026 expected acceleration as sanctions bite deeper into shadow fleet.

**Alternative propulsion and future fleet.** LNG dual-fuel: Hafnia Vista/CSSC 4 LR1s delivered (last: Hafnia Lillesand); +12-18% newbuild premium. Methanol dual-fuel: Hafnia/Socatra 4x 49,800 DWT MRs at GSI (Ecomar series); Proman Stena Bulk 6x at GSI (first Stena Pro Patria); +10-15% premium. Ammonia: MISC/AET contracted DSIC for world's first two ammonia dual-fuel Aframax tankers for Petronas PETCO; commercial ops 2029-30. Nuclear: STNG invested $10M in AMPERA for TRISO thorium-fueled 15-30 MWe subcritical solid-state reactors — never-refueled design over multi-decade lifecycle; near-term application is floating nuclear barges, long-term nuclear-powered commercial vessels. Wind-assist: 75% of WAPS installations are retrofits; rotor sails 54% share; fuel savings 4-15%; Anemoi building 200 rotor sails/year Jingjiang facility by 2027.

**Late-cycle signal: S&P > newbuild.** April 2026 VLCC resales $168M vs newbuild price $128.5M = $39.5M inversion. 15-year-old VLCCs +29% YTD to $80M; 10-year-old at $110M (+22% YTD). Yard capacity booked out to 2028-2030; prompt tonnage commands any premium. Historically this signal precedes 6-12 months of strong rates followed by steep mean reversion.
