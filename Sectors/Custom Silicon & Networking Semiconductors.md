## Key industry questions

- Will custom XPUs (Google Ironwood, Meta MTIA, AWS Trainium, OpenAI XPU, Anthropic rack-scale TPU) cannibalize Nvidia GPU share above or below the ~30% ceiling implied by current hyperscaler capex mix, and how much of the resulting ASIC revenue accrues to silicon design partners (Broadcom/Marvell/Alchip) vs is internalized by the hyperscalers themselves?
- Does Ethernet displace InfiniBand in back-end AI training by 2027 (Dell'Oro base case), and if so, does Broadcom's Tomahawk/Jericho franchise capture the architectural rent or does Nvidia's Spectrum-X + NVLink Fusion recapture it via semi-custom bundling?
- Will NVLink Fusion (Nvidia + Marvell + MediaTek + Alchip + Astera Labs, March 2026 $2B Nvidia–Marvell deal) or UALink 1.0 (AMD + Intel + Google + Meta + Microsoft + HPE) win the 2027–2028 hyperscaler scale-up rack standard — and does Broadcom's Tomahawk Ultra / SUE third-path benefit either way as the only merchant-silicon scale-up substrate already shipping in volume in 2026?
- Does Linear Pluggable Optics (LPO) compress merchant optical DSP gross margin from ~65% to ~55% by FY28 (Marvell shipping the industry-first 1.6T LPO chipset is a deliberate margin-destructive hedge against its own DSP monopoly), and is the resulting blended optical economics positive or negative for the silicon design partners as 1.6T port-count growth offsets the ASP loss?
- Is the second-source structural slot a permanent procurement feature (every hyperscaler RFP is bid by Broadcom + Marvell to avoid single-vendor lock-in) or a transitional state that collapses by 2028 as one design partner achieves an unbridgeable IP / packaging / SerDes lead — and what does "second-seat ceiling" pricing mean for Marvell's ability to ever close the AVGO ~65% to MRVL ~60% gross-margin gap?

## Industry history

The industry is the product of three overlapping consolidation waves — merchant switching silicon (1995–2015), semiconductor connectivity roll-up under Avago/Broadcom (2013–2019), and AI-era custom ASIC emergence (2013–present) — converging on a single buyer set (six US hyperscalers + ByteDance) and a single foundry (TSMC).

**Merchant switching silicon (mid-1990s → 2015)**. Broadcom Corporation (founded 1991, Henry Nicholas / Henry Samueli) pioneered merchant 1GbE/10GbE switching silicon, displacing proprietary switch ASICs from Cisco, Juniper, and Nortel. Fulcrum Microsystems (2003 clockless crossbar) and Fujitsu emerged as the principal merchant peers. The 2010–2012 inflection was architectural: cloud operators (Amazon, Facebook, Google) concluded that vertical differentiation in networking systems did not justify in-house silicon, so they standardized on merchant switches (Arista built its business on this thesis). Cisco's response was to enter merchant silicon with Silicon One in 2019 — roughly a decade after losing the architectural argument. Intel acquired Fulcrum in 2011 and later Barefoot Networks (Tofino) in 2019, then effectively exited merchant switching silicon by 2022–2023. By 2015, Linley Group measured Broadcom's merchant switching share at 94.5%; the concentration has held at 80–90% since despite repeated Marvell (Teralynx) and Nvidia (Spectrum-X) challenges.

**Avago/Broadcom roll-up (2013 → 2019)**. Avago — spun out of HP/Agilent in 1999, taken private by KKR/Silver Lake in 2005, re-IPO'd 2009 — operated with private-equity capital discipline under Hock Tan. Avago acquired LSI (2014, $6.6B; storage controllers + the engineering team that included Charlie Kawwas), the original Broadcom Corporation (2016, $37B; switching + Wi-Fi/BT + SerDes IP; Avago kept the Broadcom brand but the AVGO ticker), and Brocade (2017, $5.9B; Fibre Channel SAN). The consolidation created the IP depth — SerDes, packaging, memory controllers, networking stack — that became the foundation of the custom-ASIC and AI-networking franchises. 2018 onward, Tan pivoted to infrastructure software (CA Technologies 2018, $18.9B; Symantec Enterprise 2019; VMware 2023, $69B), converting diversified connectivity cash flow into subscription software cash flow and decoupling AVGO from the semiconductor cycle.

**Marvell roll-up (2018 → 2026)**. Marvell — founded 1995 by Sehat Sutardja and Weili Dai, IPO 2000 — operated as a storage and Ethernet IP company through the 2010s before repositioning as an AI-infrastructure silicon platform under Matt Murphy (CEO 2016+). Five acquisitions assembled the current franchise: Cavium (2018, $6B; ARM server CPUs + ThunderX networking + LiquidIO DPUs), Aquantia (2019, $452M; multi-gig Ethernet PHY), Inphi (2021, $10B; coherent optical DSP + electro-optics — the foundation of the 80%+ 800G DSP share franchise), Innovium (2021, $1.1B; Teralynx switching silicon — the merchant challenger to Broadcom Tomahawk), and Celestial AI (Feb 2026, $3.25B; Photonic Fabric IP for memory-pool disaggregation across the rack). The strategic logic mirrors the Avago/Broadcom playbook at smaller scale: roll up adjacent IP (SerDes + DSP + packaging + photonics), build the design-services platform, then position as Broadcom's structural alternate for hyperscaler RFPs. The execution gap — Trainium 2 RDL interposer defects forfeiting the Trainium 3 primary socket to Alchip in 2025, Teralynx trailing Tomahawk by ~2 generations (12.8 Tbps vs 102.4 Tbps), GAAP gross margin ~45% vs Broadcom ~65% — defines Marvell's positional ceiling: second-source procurement slot rather than category leadership.

**Custom ASIC emergence (2013 → present)**. Google's first TPU (TPU v1, 2013–2015, inference-only) was co-designed with Broadcom; the partnership has spanned all seven TPU generations through Ironwood (v7, 2026) and is contractually extended through 2031 under the April 2026 Anthropic-Google-Broadcom deal. TPU v8 ("Sunfish," TSMC N2, late 2027) is already in co-design. The pattern — hyperscaler owns architecture and software stack, Broadcom owns silicon implementation, SerDes, memory controllers, packaging, and TSMC orchestration — became the template for Meta MTIA (2020+; four generations co-developed), Amazon Trainium (primarily Alchip/Marvell), OpenAI XPU (10GW Broadcom deal, 2026), and Anthropic rack-scale TPU (Broadcom, 2027+ delivery). Custom XPU merchant revenue scaled from effectively zero in 2020 to Broadcom's $8.4B Q1 FY2026 AI semiconductor segment (+106% YoY).

**Networking architectural transition (2023 → 2027)**. The AI-training workload exposed Ethernet's lossy, single-path limitations against Nvidia InfiniBand (RDMA-native, deterministic). The counter-response — the Ultra Ethernet Consortium, founded 2023 by AMD, Arista, Broadcom, Cisco, Meta, Microsoft, HPE, Intel, Oracle — published UEC Specification 1.0 in June 2025 (1.0.1 in September 2025), reconstructing the Ethernet stack around multipath packet spraying, link-layer retry, credit-based flow control, and RoCE-native AI semantics. Dell'Oro forecasts Ethernet surpasses InfiniBand in back-end AI networks by 2027. Scale-up (intra-rack, GPU-to-GPU) remains the contested layer in 2026: Nvidia's NVLink Fusion (opened May 2025, partners include Marvell, MediaTek, Alchip, Astera Labs, Synopsys, Cadence), Broadcom's SUE (Scale-Up Ethernet, submitted to OCP), UALink (dedicated switch silicon from Upscale AI slipped to late 2026), and the OCP ESUN workstream (October 2025, signed by every major hyperscaler + every major silicon vendor including Nvidia) all compete for the same 150–400ns-latency, lossless-fabric socket that NVLink currently owns.

## Competitive dynamics

The sector has an unusually asymmetric structure: one incumbent (Broadcom) holds simultaneous dominance in two adjacent franchises (merchant switching silicon + custom ASIC design services), surrounded by specialists attacking each franchise separately. The cross-franchise leverage — shared SerDes IP, shared packaging, shared TSMC allocation, shared hyperscaler relationships — is the durable edge that single-franchise challengers cannot replicate, and it is strengthening rather than weakening as workloads scale.

**Marvell's structural position is the procurement complement to Broadcom, not a technical peer.** Hyperscalers do not accept single-vendor lock-in on multi-billion-dollar custom-silicon programs, so every RFP that goes to Broadcom is also shopped to a credible alternate. Alchip is Taiwan-only ownership (geopolitical concentration), MediaTek is mobile-first with limited HPC SerDes IP, GUC is sub-scale — by elimination, Marvell occupies the second-source structural slot in 2026. Two consequences flow from this: (i) the floor revenue is durable even if Marvell never closes the Broadcom technical gap because the procurement slot exists regardless of product gap; (ii) the same procurement logic that hands Marvell sockets caps its pricing power — every "second seat" is structurally lower-margin than the incumbent's, which is the empirical reading of MRVL ~60% non-GAAP gross margin vs AVGO ~65% (vs Alchip ~30% as the pure-implementation floor). The Trainium 3 design loss to Alchip (Trainium 2 RDL interposer defects forfeiting the primary socket; Marvell potentially regaining ~500K of 2.5M T3 units on a chiplet path, unconfirmed) is the first material counter-evidence that Marvell can defend even the second seat against geopolitically concentrated implementation specialists. The 2027–2028 test cases are: (a) AWS Trainium 4 socket assignment, (b) Google formalising the April 2026 MPU + inference TPU talks into a signed contract — currently third-seat behind Broadcom's through-2031 lock and MediaTek's fast-follower role.

**Optical DSP duopoly and the LPO self-cannibalisation paradox**. Marvell holds 80%+ share at 800G optical DSP (Inphi-legacy franchise), with Broadcom in secondary share — a duopoly at the PHY layer that has been more durable than the merchant-switching franchise because optical DSP design is bottlenecked by analog-IP teams that no other vendor has scaled. Marvell shipped the industry's first 1.6 Tbps Linear Pluggable Optics (LPO) chipset (200G/lane TIA + laser driver) in 2026 — the LPO architecture removes DSP content from short-reach optical modules to save power, compressing ASP and gross margin per port by an estimated 30–40% at equivalent port speed. This is margin-destructive hedging by Marvell against its own DSP monopoly: shipping LPO first secures share in the post-DSP architecture but compresses blended optical gross margin from ~65% (pure DSP) to ~55% (blended) over a 24-month window. Sell-side models that sum DSP growth + LPO growth without re-marking gross-margin assumptions are double-counting; the appropriate framing is that Marvell trades 10pp of structural margin to retain 80%+ unit share through the architectural transition.

**Pricing-power trajectory**. Broadcom's pricing power strengthens across both franchises over 2024–2027. In custom ASIC, switching costs rise with each generation because (i) leading-edge SerDes (224G PAM4 today, 448G entering development) requires shrinking analog-design teams at <=N2 nodes; (ii) HBM4 integration and CoWoS-L packaging create foundry-level orchestration dependencies only Broadcom has at scale; (iii) each TPU/MTIA generation embeds N years of architectural knowledge that a switch to Marvell or Alchip would waste. Jensen Huang's own rebuttal in the April 2026 Dwarkesh interview — "ASIC margin is ~65%, Nvidia is ~70%, what are you really saving?" — is an unintended tell: hyperscalers switching from Nvidia GPUs to custom ASICs still pay 65% margins to Broadcom, so Broadcom captures most of the architectural rent that used to accrue to Nvidia. In merchant switching silicon, the Tomahawk 6 / Tomahawk 6-Davisson (CPO) + Jericho 4 (scale-across) + Tomahawk Ultra (scale-up) triple-play provides SKU coverage across every cluster topology; competitors (Marvell, Nvidia, Cisco Silicon One) compete in one dimension at a time. Nvidia's Spectrum-X1600 slipping to H2 2026 — six months behind Tomahawk 6 — is the single most quantifiable evidence of the widening gap.

**New-entrant threat mapping**. The credible threats are not in scale silicon (where capex + IP depth are prohibitive) but at the edges: (i) connectivity specialists (Astera Labs, Credo) attacking the retimer/AEC/fabric-switch adjacencies with hyperscaler-customer-direct sales; (ii) NVLink Fusion partner silicon (Alchip, MediaTek) enabling hyperscalers to pick Nvidia for scale-up + Broadcom/Marvell for scale-out in the same rack; (iii) hyperscaler in-sourcing of specific IP blocks (Google's own SerDes team hiring, Meta's internal design group) — a slow bleed that has not materially compressed Broadcom's revenue share in any year since 2020. The structural observation: every hyperscaler that has attempted full silicon self-sufficiency has ultimately retained Broadcom for the hardest blocks (224G SerDes, advanced packaging, memory interface).

> **Prompt:** *What exactly is Broadcom's SerDes advantage, how did they accrue this lead and what quantifiable benefit does a better SerDes do for the product compared to a solution from Marvell. How difficult is it for others to catch-up or is the SerDes lead unassailable for Broadcom now*

> **Response:** SerDes lead is an analog-design franchise accrued via HP DNA (Avago 1999 spin-out) + LSI 2014 (Charlie Kawwas's storage SerDes) + Broadcom Corp 2016 (Samueli's networking SerDes) + TSMC PDK co-development. Quantifiable benefit at 224G PAM4: BER <1e-15 vs ~1e-12 (3 orders of magnitude headroom), ~3.5 pJ/bit vs ~5 pJ/bit power, 30+ inch reach vs ~20 inch. Marvell post-Inphi has ~12-month lag at lane performance — durable but not unassailable; integration depth (SerDes + switch SoC + memory controller in one tape-out) is the real moat, widening at 448G. **Broadcom Tomahawk 6 (BCM78910 series)** — 102.4 Tbps Ethernet switch SoC, TSMC N3P, shipping in production volume March 2026. Doubles Tomahawk 5's 51.2 Tbps. Supports 64×1.6T ports or 128×800G ports, enabling single-tier fabrics spanning >1M XPUs. Implements UEC 1.0 natively (multipath packet spraying, link-layer retry, credit-based flow control). Integrated on-chip telemetry, cognitive routing, Elephant Flow detection. Position: the default "scale-out" switch silicon across every major AI cluster deployed in 2026–2027. Nvidia's competing Spectrum-X1600 (also 102.4 Tbps) is 6+ months behind in volume shipments.

**Broadcom Tomahawk 6 – Davisson** — World's first 102.4 Tbps switch with co-packaged optics (CPO). Integrates 16 × 6.4T silicon photonics engines directly on the switch package using TSMC COUPE (Compact Universal Photonic Engine). Cuts data-transmission energy from ~15 pJ/bit (pluggable optics + copper) to <5 pJ/bit — a >3× power reduction at switch-system level. Operates at 200 Gbps per lane. Third-generation CPO platform (after Bailly). In volume production as of April 2026. Strategic purpose: eliminate pluggable optics as a failure mode and power bottleneck for >100 Tbps fabrics. Broadcom shipped 50K+ CPO switches in 2025; TH6-Davisson is the first CPO product validated for hyperscaler production AI deployment at scale.

**Broadcom Jericho 4 (Q200 series)** — 51.2 Tbps deep-buffer Ethernet fabric router with HyperPort (four 800GE links combined into a single 3.2 Tbps virtual channel). Purpose: lossless RoCE over 100+ km, connecting AI clusters across data centers ("scale-across" — the fourth networking tier beyond scale-up, scale-out, and east-west). Positions Broadcom as the only merchant vendor with a complete scale-up + scale-out + scale-across silicon portfolio. HyperPort claims 70% improvement in network utilization and 40% shorter job completion times on distributed training workloads. In production Q1 2026.

**Broadcom Tomahawk Ultra** — 51.2 Tbps purpose-built for AI scale-up, targeting the NVLink replacement socket. 250 ns switch latency (vs Tomahawk 6's ~400 ns) + 77B packets/sec throughput. Architecturally the silicon substrate for the Scale-Up Ethernet (SUE) protocol Broadcom submitted to OCP, positioning Ethernet against Nvidia NVLink and AMD UALink for intra-rack GPU-to-GPU/XPU-to-XPU fabrics. Adopted by hyperscalers in early 2026 because UALink dedicated switch silicon (Upscale AI) slipped to late 2026.

**Broadcom Thor Ultra 800G AI NIC** — First 800G AI Ethernet NIC compliant with UEC specifications. Replaces Nvidia ConnectX/BlueField NICs in AI-training servers using Broadcom silicon, completing the Broadcom-native server-to-switch data path.

**Broadcom custom XPU IP platform** — Not a single product; a silicon-implementation offering that wraps Broadcom's SerDes (224G PAM4 shipping; 448G in development at <=N2), memory controllers (DDR5/HBM3E/HBM4), PCIe Gen5/Gen6, advanced packaging (CoWoS-L, embedded-bridge), power management, security engines, and TSMC orchestration around customer-owned compute architectures. Five deployed XPU designs as of April 2026: Google TPU v7 Ironwood (production; 192 GB HBM3E, 7.2 TB/s, 9,216-chip superpod at 42.5 FP8 exaflops), Google TPU v8 "Sunfish" (N2, late 2027 target), Meta MTIA v3/Iris (production, 2nm — first 2nm AI chip), Meta MTIA v4/v5 (co-designed, 2027+), OpenAI first-generation XPU (10GW commitment; initial deliveries late 2026), Anthropic rack-scale TPU-Broadcom systems (3.5GW, deliveries from 2027). Margin profile: ~65% on ASIC silicon (per Jensen Huang's own benchmark) vs ~70% on Nvidia's GPUs — meaning Broadcom retains most of the Nvidia rent that hyperscaler in-sourcing was supposed to eliminate.

**Marvell custom silicon design-services platform** — The structural counterpart to Broadcom's XPU platform, positioned as the second-source procurement option. Wraps Marvell's SerDes (224G PAM4 shipping; 448G in roadmap), Inphi-legacy DSP IP, memory controllers (DDR5/HBM3E), PCIe Gen5/Gen6, packaging integration, and TSMC orchestration around customer architecture. 18 disclosed cloud design wins as of FY26 with named programs in production: AWS Trainium 2 (primary; lost Trainium 3 primary socket to Alchip on RDL-interposer defects, retained partial chiplet/packaging scope on ~500K of 2.5M T3 units pending mass-production validation), Microsoft Maia DPU, Meta MTIA-companion DPU, Google Axion ARM CPU, and Google's reported April 2026 MPU + inference TPU exploration (third seat behind Broadcom's through-2031 TPU lock and MediaTek's fast-follower socket — exploratory talks, not yet contractually disclosed). Custom-silicon revenue $1.5B FY26, guided to double by FY28 — implying ~$3B run-rate. Gross margin ~60% non-GAAP — the second-seat structural ceiling: ~5pp below Broadcom's ~65%, ~30pp above Alchip's ~30% pure-services floor.

**Marvell Teralynx (Innovium-legacy)** — Merchant Ethernet switch SoC line attempting to challenge Broadcom Tomahawk. 12.8 Tbps shipping in 2026 — two architectural generations behind Tomahawk 6 (102.4 Tbps). Volume forecast only ~1.8M units cumulative through 2028, vs Tomahawk's tens of millions per generation. Strategic role: provides hyperscaler procurement an alternate to Tomahawk for selected use cases (mid-radix top-of-rack, edge data-center fabrics) but cannot win front-end or scale-out switch silicon at the leading-edge port speeds (200G/lane → 400G/lane) where the AI capex is concentrated. The franchise is structurally subscale relative to the merchant-switching market it targets — the 2021 Innovium acquisition has not closed the architectural gap, and the cadence required to catch Tomahawk 7 (~204.8 Tbps, expected 2028) likely exceeds Marvell's R&D run-rate.

**Marvell Nova / Ara optical DSP** — Coherent and PAM4 digital-signal-processing silicon for optical modules at 400G/800G/1.6T port speeds — the Inphi-legacy franchise. Nova: 5nm 1.6T DSP. Ara: 3nm 1.6T DSP, ~20% lower power than Nova. Marvell holds 80%+ share at 800G DSP, with Broadcom secondary. Gross margin ~65% on pure DSP — the highest-margin franchise in the Marvell stack. The 1.6T transition (Nova/Ara) is the current revenue driver and the LPO transition (below) is the explicit margin headwind on this franchise over the FY26–FY28 window.

**Marvell 1.6T LPO chipset** — Industry-first 1.6 Tbps Linear Pluggable Optics chipset (200G/lane TIA + laser driver), shipping ahead of the broader LPO architectural transition. LPO removes the DSP from optical modules in short-reach scale-up applications, replacing it with linear analog amplification — saving ~50% of optical-module power at the cost of ASP and gross margin per port. Marvell is the first-mover specifically because it owns the DSP socket being eliminated; shipping LPO first secures the post-DSP analog-silicon socket while structurally cannibalising its own ~65%-margin DSP revenue. Net: trades ~10pp of blended optical gross margin (65% → 55% over 24 months) to retain 80%+ unit share through the architectural transition.

**Marvell Celestial AI Photonic Fabric** — Acquired Feb 2026 ($3.25B). **Plain-English framing**: every AI accelerator today carries its working memory (HBM stacks) physically attached to the same chip package — like a laptop with soldered, non-upgradable RAM. Trillion-parameter inference and reasoning workloads exceed any single chip's memory ceiling, forcing the chip to stall waiting for data. Photonic Fabric replaces per-chip memory with a rack-wide memory pool that every chip can reach via fibre-optic cables running at light-speed — chips borrow as much memory as the workload needs from a shared store rather than being capped at what fits on their own package. The $3.25B Celestial purchase buys the silicon-design IP for this optical memory link; the bet is that rack-wide memory pooling becomes the 2027–2028 architectural primitive that replaces today's per-chip-HBM convention. **Technical detail**: Architecturally distinct from co-packaged optics (Broadcom Bailly, TSMC COUPE, AMD COUPE-risk) — Bailly/COUPE attack scale-out switch I/O (optical interconnect from switch port to NIC/server, topology unchanged), Photonic Fabric attacks scale-up XPU-to-remote-HBM connectivity for memory-pool disaggregation across the rack ("end of the memory wall"). The XPU's effective memory becomes a rack-wide photonic-connected pool rather than per-package HBM stacks — a topology change that decouples compute capacity from memory capacity at rack level. Gen 1: 16 Tbps/chiplet photonic interconnect, sampling H1 2026 in memory modules + NICs; Gen 2: 64 Tbps/chiplet, sampling H2 2026 in XPU/switch-integrated form (order-of-magnitude over current HBM3E electrical interface bandwidth per package). Penguin memory chassis target 2026; first material revenue H2 FY28; $500M Q4 FY28 run-rate guide; $1B by end-FY29. **Marvell IP edge**: Optical Compute Interconnect (OCI) — chiplet-integrated photonic I/O combining PIC + driver + control logic on a chiplet that integrates with any XPU via standard CoWoS-L / EMIB packaging; combined with Inphi-legacy DSP for optical signaling, Innovium-legacy switching IP for fabric arbitration, and Marvell packaging IP for chiplet integration — a stack that requires Inphi + Innovium + Celestial together (the rationale for the $3.25B price). The acquisition is a binary bet: if memory-disaggregation becomes the 2027–2028 rack primitive, Photonic Fabric is the only merchant scale-up optical option outside Nvidia NVLink — $500M understates by orders of magnitude. If rack architectures stabilise on copper + NVLink for three more generations, $500M is the ceiling and the $3.25B purchase price faces goodwill-impairment risk. The acquisition is also Marvell's first major operational integration of an advanced-photonics team — the same advanced-packaging execution capability that just failed on Trainium 3.

**Broadcom SerDes platform — the analog moat**. SerDes (Serializer/Deserializer) converts parallel digital data into high-speed serial streams for traversal across copper traces, sockets, and optical modules. Production silicon runs 224 Gbps PAM4 per lane (4-level pulse-amplitude modulation, 2 bits/symbol); 448 Gbps PAM4 is in development for 2027–2028 tape-outs. Each generation requires a complete rebuild of the analog front-end (CTLE, FFE, DFE equalizers + clock recovery + ADC), which is the bottleneck — analog designers do not scale linearly the way digital teams do, and the senior >100G-PAM4 design pool is <500 worldwide. **How the lead accrued**: (i) HP DNA via Avago's 1999 spin-out — three decades of mixed-signal IC design discipline; (ii) LSI 2014 acquisition added Charlie Kawwas's storage-controller SerDes team (HDD physical-layer DNA); (iii) Broadcom Corp 2016 acquisition added Henry Samueli's networking SerDes franchise (1G/10G/100G heritage); (iv) co-developed PDK extensions with TSMC at N3P/N2 for high-speed analog. **Quantifiable benefit at 224G**: Broadcom delivers BER <1e-15 vs ~1e-12 typical for catch-up implementations (3 orders of magnitude headroom), ~3.5 pJ/bit vs ~5 pJ/bit power efficiency, and 30+ inch trace reach vs ~20 inches for second-source. **Catch-up difficulty**: Marvell post-Inphi (2021) is the only credible second-source at 224G with ~12-month lag, not 10-year — the gap is on integration depth (SerDes + switch SoC + memory controller in one tape-out), not raw lane performance. Alphawave Semi licenses SerDes IP but lacks switch-SoC integration. Nvidia self-designs SerDes for Spectrum-X but the Spectrum-X1600 6-month slip behind Tomahawk 6 is the empirical cadence proxy. At 448G, the analog complexity compounds non-linearly with line rate, widening the Broadcom lead because catch-up vendors must rebuild teams from scratch each generation. Verdict: not unassailable but durable for 3–5 years at integration-platform level; raw lane-performance parity is closer.

**Broadcom Silicon Photonics — in-house scope vs assembly**. **Integrated stack synergies — single-vendor networking economics**. Standardising on Broadcom across all four networking tiers (Tomahawk 6 scale-out + Tomahawk Ultra scale-up + Jericho 4 scale-across + Thor Ultra NIC) creates measurable datacenter-level synergies absent in multi-vendor stacks: (i) single 224G PAM4 SerDes IP across all switch SoCs and the NIC eliminates protocol-translation hops and ensures BER consistency end-to-end; (ii) single SONiC-based control plane + unified telemetry / cognitive routing — multi-vendor stacks require multiple NMS systems; (iii) shared UEC 1.0 implementation tunes congestion management, link-layer retry, and packet spraying identically end-to-end; (iv) joint power optimisation at envelope level — TH6 + TH6-Davisson CPO + SUE designed as one power budget vs Cisco/Arista/Mellanox each optimising locally; (v) single procurement contract + single support escalation path — non-trivial at $5–10B AI cluster scale; (vi) co-packaged optics integrated with switch SoC removes pluggable optical module as a separate failure domain; (vii) unified firmware update cadence vs multi-vendor coordination overhead. Counter-evidence: hyperscalers do not necessarily standardise — AWS uses Mellanox/Nvidia + own ENA + Trainium; Microsoft uses Cisco/Arista/Nvidia + Maia; Google uses internally-designed Apollo OCS + ICI. The standardisation premium is real but bounded by hyperscaler-specific architectural choices; it captures most clearly at Anthropic, OpenAI, xAI and at any new entrant building greenfield racks who lacks the scale to manage multi-vendor integration overhead.

**Broadcom business model — silicon vendor, not systems integrator**. Broadcom does not manufacture switches under its own brand and does not operate an ODM model. The product is a switch SoC (Tomahawk 6, Tomahawk Ultra, Jericho 4) shipped to two distinct buyer channels: (i) branded systems vendors — Arista (#1 data-center switch revenue share Q1 2025 at ~21.5%, Broadcom-silicon-based across Etherlink and 7000-series), Cisco Nexus (mixes Broadcom Tomahawk on selected SKUs alongside its own Silicon One), Juniper QFX (Broadcom Tomahawk-based), HPE Aruba CX, Extreme Networks; (ii) ODM / white-box — Celestica, Wiwynn, Quanta, Edgecore (Accton), Foxconn, Inventec — selling bare-metal switches loaded with Broadcom silicon directly to hyperscalers, who run open SONiC/SAI software on top. **Hyperscaler capex inflation**. 2026 aggregate capex for Amazon ($200B) + Alphabet ($175–185B) + Meta ($115–135B) + Microsoft ($120B+) + Oracle ($50B) = $660–690B, of which ~75% ($450–525B) targets AI infrastructure — a 36% YoY increase on 2025. The macro risk is symmetric: if capex sustains current growth, Broadcom's $100B 2027 AI revenue target is mechanically delivered; if capex plateaus or cuts (AI-monetization gap, rate cycle reversal, hyperscaler ROI scrutiny), multiple compression is immediate because AVGO trades at 35× forward vs 5-year average 22× (60% premium).

**Architectural transition — Ethernet over InfiniBand**. Dell'Oro forecasts Ethernet surpasses InfiniBand in back-end AI networks by 2027. UEC 1.0 closed the performance gap; UEC 1.0.1 (September 2025) added Programmable Congestion Management, Congestion Signaling, and multipath extensions. 800G Ethernet deployment is accelerating 100× faster than prior generation transitions. **Ethernet vs InfiniBand — protocol-level compare**:

| Dimension | InfiniBand (HDR 200G / NDR 400G / XDR 800G) | Ethernet (UEC 800G / 1.6T) |
|---|---|---|
| Loss model | Lossless (credit-based flow control native) | Historically lossy; UEC adds credit-based flow control + link-layer retry |
| RDMA | Native (kernel bypass, zero-copy verbs) | RoCEv2 (RDMA over Converged Ethernet) — protocol overlay, now near-parity with UEC |
| Latency (port-to-port) | 90–110 ns | 250–400 ns (Tomahawk 6 scale-out); 250 ns (Tomahawk Ultra scale-up) |
| Routing | Subnet manager — centralised, deterministic | Distributed BGP/EVPN; UEC adds multipath packet spraying |
| Programming model | Verbs API (deeply optimised in CUDA / Magnum IO / GPUDirect) | Sockets + RDMA verbs; UEC standardises AI-specific semantics |
| Scaling ceiling | ~10K endpoints per subnet | ~1M endpoints with UEC + Jericho 4 scale-across |
| Vendor concentration | 100% Nvidia (post-Mellanox 2019) | Multi-vendor: Broadcom, Marvell, Nvidia, Cisco, Arista, system OEMs |
| TCO per port (800G) | $400–600 | $250–400 (merchant-silicon competition) |

Net: InfiniBand survives in classic HPC and Nvidia-bundled new builds; Ethernet (Broadcom-silicon, multi-vendor systems) takes the AI training back-end and all greenfield AI capacity.

**Architectural transition — LPO (DSP self-cannibalisation)**. Linear Pluggable Optics replaces the DSP in short-reach optical modules with linear analog amplification, removing ~50% of optical-module power. Marvell shipped the first 1.6T LPO chipset (200G/lane) ahead of the broader LPO architectural transition — the deliberate strategic choice to cannibalise its own ~65%-margin DSP socket to retain ~80%+ unit share through the architecture change. Sector-level implication: blended optical gross margin compresses from ~65% (pure DSP) toward ~55% (blended DSP + LPO) over 2026–2028, and sell-side models that sum DSP growth + LPO growth without re-marking gross-margin assumptions are double-counting. The non-obvious effect is that LPO weakens Marvell's optical franchise more than Broadcom's because Marvell is the share leader being cannibalised; for hyperscalers, LPO is power-positive (net favourable for capex efficiency); for Nvidia, LPO eliminates a Marvell/Broadcom rent at the cost of intensifying Nvidia's own SerDes-IP exposure on linear interconnect.

**Architectural transition — memory disaggregation / Photonic Fabric**. The 2027–2028 candidate rack primitive: GPU/XPU compute connects to remote HBM pools across a photonic scale-up fabric (16 Tbps/chiplet Gen 1, 64 Tbps/chiplet Gen 2), eliminating the per-chip HBM ceiling that bottlenecks large-context inference and reasoning workloads. Marvell's Celestial AI acquisition ($3.25B, Feb 2026) is the only merchant-silicon bet on this architecture; Nvidia retains its in-house NVLink alternative; Broadcom's CPO + Tomahawk Ultra is adjacent but not a memory-fabric play. The architectural primitive is unproven — if validated by 2027–2028 hyperscaler deployment, it opens a new TAM bucket distinct from CPO and from scale-up Ethernet. If rack architectures stabilise on copper + NVLink + HBM-on-package for three more generations, the Photonic Fabric bet stays sub-$500M revenue and the $3.25B purchase price faces goodwill-impairment risk. The decision-tree is binary at the architecture-validation level rather than incremental.

**UALink wins on**: openness (no Nvidia toll), royalty-free economics, vendor diversity at switch-silicon layer (eventually), avoidance of Nvidia dependency for non-Nvidia compute. **UALink loses on**: production cadence (Upscale AI switch silicon late 2026 vs Nvidia 2024 production — 2-year gap during the 2026–2027 capex peak), absence of CUDA-equivalent collective software, latency at fabric maturity unproven, single startup-vendor switch supply, coalition cohesion (Google, Meta, Microsoft are also NVLink Fusion partners — hedging against AMD's UALink pure-play).

**NVLink Fusion wins on**: production maturity (shipping at scale since 2024 Hopper era), measured latency (~150 ns at NVL72 scale), software integration depth (CUDA/Magnum IO/GPUDirect/NVLink-Sharp), bandwidth leadership (1.8 TB/s NVLink 5.0; 3.6 TB/s roadmap), Nvidia financial backing, partner-silicon ecosystem (Marvell $2B March 2026 deal as proof point). **NVLink Fusion loses on**: lock-in economics (Nvidia retains toll on partner silicon — exactly what UALink members wanted to avoid), strategic dependency for non-Nvidia compute (AMD, Intel, sovereign-cloud customers cannot use it for ideological reasons), partner authority asymmetry (silicon partners are implementers, not architectural decision-makers), Nvidia controls fabric-layer margin capture.

**Broadcom SUE / Tomahawk Ultra wins on**: production maturity (shipping in volume in 2026 — only merchant scale-up silicon at scale), open standard (OCP-submitted), compute-agnostic (works with any XPU — Google TPU, Meta MTIA, OpenAI XPU, Anthropic, AWS Trainium, AMD/Intel GPUs), no vendor toll on protocol, hyperscaler-validated deployments (OpenAI 10 GW, Anthropic 3.5 GW). **SUE loses on**: latency parity (250 ns vs NVLink 150 ns — material for tightly-coupled inference workloads), absence of CUDA-equivalent collective software, dependence on UEC AI extensions still maturing, no integrated GPU-memory-pool semantics like NVLink-Sharp.

**Strategic conclusion**:
- **UALink** is the open-coalition response to NVLink dominance — politically attractive but operationally fragile. It needs Upscale AI to ship competitive switch silicon, AMD/Intel to deliver compute that justifies an alternate fabric, and the consortium to align on roadmap cadence. Coalition cohesion is the principal risk: Google/Meta/Microsoft are paying NVLink Fusion partners simultaneously, so UALink is effectively an AMD-led pure-play with hedging contributors.
- **NVLink Fusion** is Nvidia's containment strategy: rather than fight a multi-vendor open standard from outside, Nvidia opens its fabric to partners while retaining the architectural authority and the toll. Marvell's $2B March 2026 deal is the tell — even hyperscaler design partners trade independence for Nvidia integration when the alternative (UALink) is not yet shipping.
- **Broadcom SUE / Tomahawk Ultra** is the third path that wins in the standards-war ambiguity window. It is the only merchant scale-up silicon shipping in volume in 2026, deployed at OpenAI and Anthropic, and serves as the substrate for any scale-up topology a hyperscaler chooses without forcing a UALink-vs-NVLink-Fusion commitment. The 2-year cadence gap (2026–2028) before UALink switch silicon matures is the window where Broadcom captures architectural rent — and the installed base built in that window creates path dependency past 2028.

**Investment translation**: AVGO benefits from any outcome — captures Tomahawk Ultra revenue regardless of which standard "wins" because greenfield 2026–2027 deployments are pre-commitment and SUE is the available answer. MRVL is binary on NVLink Fusion winning — its $2B-backed silicon-partner role strands if UALink takes share by 2028. NVDA wins fabric-economics if NVLink Fusion entrenches but loses partner trust (every partner is by definition a non-Nvidia compute customer). AMD/Intel are leveraged on UALink succeeding but face compound execution risk (Upscale AI switch + their own compute roadmaps + consortium alignment). Per the existing §Investor heuristics → "scale-up is the real 2026 battle" non-consensus insight: AVGO scale-up payoff is robust to standard selection; MRVL scale-up payoff is binary on selection.

**Procurement normalisation — second-source structural slot**. No major hyperscaler accepts single-vendor lock-in on multi-billion-dollar custom-silicon programs. The procurement consequence: every Broadcom RFP is shopped to a credible alternate (Marvell, Alchip, MediaTek, GUC). The macro effect is that Marvell's revenue floor is structurally durable even when its technical position lags — but the same procurement logic caps its pricing power at the ~5pp gross-margin discount to Broadcom (MRVL ~60% non-GAAP vs AVGO ~65%). The 2027–2028 fragmentation vector is that hyperscalers add a third design partner (Google now using Broadcom + Marvell + MediaTek across different chip classes), splitting the second-source slot and compressing ASPs further. The empirical question: does second-source procurement evolve into three-source procurement, and at what point does the resulting ASP compression reach merchant-foundry economics? The compression first becomes visible in ASIC gross margins moving below ~60%, and is the leading bear-case indicator for both AVGO and MRVL.

**Geopolitical concentration — TSMC**. 92% of leading-edge foundry output (<=N3), 100% of CoWoS, ~95% of advanced packaging sit inside 100-mile PLA strike window per the April 2026 TSM stress test ([[Research/2026-04-19 - TSM - Stress Test]]). **Hyperscaler in-sourcing vector — ASIC**. Google has been designing TPUs for 13 years and still partners with Broadcom for SerDes + packaging + TSMC orchestration. The binary in-sourcing scenario the market persistently prices is not empirically consistent with any hyperscaler's actual revealed behavior through April 2026.

**Hyperscaler in-sourcing vector — switching silicon**. The switching-silicon in-sourcing risk is structurally lower than the ASIC in-sourcing risk and is not materialising in the data through April 2026. **Current programmes**: no major hyperscaler operates a production in-house merchant-class switching SoC. Past attempts: Google published the Aquila research switch (SIGCOMM 2022) but never productionised it for general datacenter switching; Meta evaluated alternatives but runs zero in-house switch-ASIC programmes through 2026; AWS uses Broadcom Tomahawk for general DC switching plus AWS-designed Nitro NICs and Trainium NeuronLink for specific in-rack interconnect — no general-purpose merchant switch-silicon attempt; Microsoft has no in-house switch-silicon programme; Alibaba/Tencent use Broadcom Tomahawk plus selected Marvell Teralynx. **Switching-silicon share inside hyperscale data centres (April 2026 estimate, blending Linley / 650 Group / Counterpoint / management commentary)**: Broadcom 80–90% (Tomahawk 4/5/6/Ultra + Jericho 3/4 across all six US hyperscalers + ByteDance); Nvidia Spectrum-X ~5–10% rising fastest (concentrated at Microsoft Azure for Nvidia-GPU clusters and Oracle Cloud); Marvell Teralynx ~3–5% (selected mid-radix and edge-DC sockets, not back-end AI training); Cisco Silicon One ~1–3% (mostly Microsoft + selected enterprise); Intel post-Tofino ~0%; ODM/white-box vendors (Celestica, Wiwynn, Quanta, Edgecore) are the channel through which Broadcom silicon reaches hyperscalers, not a separate silicon source. **Why hyperscalers don't in-source switching silicon at the same pace as ASIC**: (i) merchant switching silicon is already 80–90% concentrated with no architectural differentiation gap to close — every cluster needs the same switching primitives (no analog to the JAX/XLA/MTIA-software architectural rent that justifies hyperscaler ASIC investment); (ii) switching silicon does not bottleneck AI training the way compute architecture does — owning the switch SoC saves cost per port but does not unlock workload performance; (iii) Tomahawk cadence (51.2 Tbps in 2023, 102.4 Tbps in March 2026, 204.8 Tbps roadmap by 2028) outpaces any plausible in-house programme that requires 3–5 years per generation; (iv) the software stack (SONiC, SAI, P4) is already open and standardised — no proprietary IP advantage to capture by owning silicon; (v) 224G PAM4 / 448G SerDes IP depth is the binding constraint, the same scarce-analog-design-team problem that caps ASIC in-sourcing. **Where in-sourcing IS happening at the IP-block level**: Google designs its own SerDes for ICI (intra-pod TPU interconnect) but pairs with Broadcom for the inter-pod Apollo OCS substrate and for TPU silicon implementation; Meta licenses selected IP from Open Compute but designs no switch SoC; AWS Nitro and NeuronLink are NIC and intra-rack interconnect silicon, not general datacenter switching. **Verdict**: Broadcom's 80–90% merchant switching share is at structural risk from merchant competitors (Nvidia Spectrum-X, Marvell Teralynx, Cisco Silicon One) rather than from hyperscaler in-sourcing. The empirical absence of any general-switching ASIC programme at any hyperscaler in 2026 — despite a decade of ASIC architectural-ownership consolidation on the compute side — is the strongest indicator that the merchant-switching franchise is structurally more durable than the merchant-ASIC franchise. The bear case for switching silicon is competitive-share erosion (Nvidia Spectrum-X gaining at NVL72 sockets, Marvell winning second-source at edge-DC sockets), not hyperscaler vertical integration.

## Investor heuristics

**Consensus frame — sector**. The sell-side consensus frames the sector as a binary substitution: "AI ASIC + Ethernet play vs Nvidia GPU + InfiniBand play" — the "Android to Nvidia's Apple" narrative for Broadcom, and the "Open Ecosystem Champion vs Broadcom Vertical Integration" narrative for Marvell. Both frames are partially correct but structurally understated. The empirical reality is that every major AI cluster contains Nvidia compute + Broadcom networking silicon + Marvell optical DSP + Marvell or Alchip second-source ASIC content concurrently — substitution dynamics operate within franchises (Tomahawk vs Spectrum-X within switching; Marvell vs Alchip within design services) rather than between tickers. Revenue growth for AVGO does not require Nvidia to lose share, and revenue growth for MRVL does not require AVGO to lose share. The pair is more accurately framed as "primary supplier (AVGO) + structurally locked second source (MRVL) + niche specialists (Alchip, MediaTek, Astera Labs)" rather than as competitors fighting for the same wallet.

**What is priced in — MRVL**. 36× forward PE FY27E / 26× FY28E (vs 5-year average 23×, sector ~27×) and +168% trailing twelve months to ~$158 embeds (i) $14–16B FY28 revenue (from $8.2B FY26, +30% FY27 guide), (ii) custom-silicon FY28 doubling to ~$3B from $1.5B FY26, (iii) Celestial Photonic Fabric ramp to $500M Q4 FY28 / $1B by end-FY29 on the acquisition roadmap, (iv) Google MPU + inference TPU exploratory talks converting to formal contract in FY27, (v) AWS Trainium 4 design-partner socket retention after the T3 primary loss, (vi) NVLink Fusion winning the 2027–2028 hyperscaler scale-up rack standard against UALink, (vii) blended optical gross margin holding above ~58% as LPO compresses DSP. **Non-consensus insight — chokepoint arithmetic**. Merchant switching silicon share (80–90%) is structurally more durable than custom ASIC share (60–70%) because switching is compute-agnostic: every cluster, regardless of compute vendor, consumes Tomahawk/Jericho. Custom ASIC franchise is customer-specific: loss of Google or Meta would be material. Sell-side modeling typically weights custom ASIC as the high-growth vector and networking as the stable franchise; the inverse relationship of durability to volatility matters for risk-adjusted TAM modeling. The Jensen Huang Dwarkesh admission — "ASIC margins ~65% vs Nvidia ~70%" — reframes the hyperscaler shift from GPUs to ASICs as a ~5-point margin transfer to customers, not the market-implied assumption that ASICs cannibalize Broadcom. The same arithmetic compounds at MRVL ~60% non-GAAP — every "second seat" win is structurally a lower-margin seat than the incumbent's, capping how high MRVL can ever credibly close the AVGO 65% vs MRVL 60% gap.

**Non-consensus insight — scale-up is the real 2026 battle**. Consensus fixates on scale-out (Tomahawk 6 vs Spectrum-X1600) because it is the visible topology tier. The contested socket is scale-up: Nvidia NVLink + Fusion vs Broadcom SUE (OCP) vs AMD/Intel/Meta/Microsoft UALink. Scale-up unit economics per port are 3–5× scale-out because intra-rack radix + latency tolerance are tighter. Whoever wins scale-up architecture wins the compute-adjacent silicon margin pool — and the fact that UALink dedicated switch silicon slipped to late 2026 while Broadcom's Tomahawk Ultra is already shipping as the SUE substrate is an empirically dominant position that sell-side has not fully modeled. The MRVL angle: NVLink Fusion is the third path; if it wins, MRVL is the non-Nvidia silicon beneficiary inside Nvidia's ecosystem; if UALink wins, MRVL's $2B-backed fabric bet strands. The Marvell scale-up payoff is binary on standard selection; the Broadcom scale-up payoff is robust to which standard wins because Tomahawk Ultra/SUE is the third-path substrate either way.
